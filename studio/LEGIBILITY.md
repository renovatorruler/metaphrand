# Legibility: the audience must be able to reconstruct what just happened

A companion law to show-not-tell, not a repeal of it. Show-not-tell governs
the MEANING layer — don't state a theme, don't narrate a feeling, trust the
audience to interpret. Legibility governs the EVENT layer underneath that:
before an audience can interpret anything, they have to be able to
reconstruct, in plain terms, what object they're looking at and why the
plot event that just happened, happened. Withholding interpretation is
craft. Withholding comprehension is just confusion, and confusion doesn't
feel like mystery to an audience — it feels like being lost, and a lost
audience stops leaning in and starts checking out.

## The two failure modes this catches

**1. The unlabeled world-object.** A prop, device, or place central to the
plot is described entirely in insider terminology, with no plain-language
or visual anchor establishing what it IS before the story starts using it.
Real-world jargon can be more misleading than invented jargon, because it
sounds authentic to a lay reader/viewer without actually informing them —
a string of period-accurate Apollo callouts reads as "some technical
procedure," not specifically "a Moon landing," unless something anchors it.

**2. The undertraced causal chain.** A plot event depends on a chain of
several unstated premises (three unrelated bureaucracies act independently
/ a federal power overrides a local one / an outcome that sounds like mercy
is actually the worst case). Each individual link may be defensible in
isolation, but the audience has to hold the WHOLE chain in their head in
real time, live, once, with no ability to reread. If any single link is
underexplained, the audience loses the thread — and once lost, they can't
tell which parts of their own confusion are the story's intended opacity
(a character being stonewalled on purpose) and which parts are a real gap.

## Diagnostic: run this before trusting a scene is clear

Read the scene cold, as if for the first time, and try to state — out
loud, in one plain sentence each — three things: (1) what is this
object/place, (2) why is this event happening, (3) is the outcome good or
bad for the character we're following, and why. If any of the three
requires information the scene never actually gave you, the scene fails
legibility, no matter how well-crafted the prose is.

## Fix techniques (in order of preference — cheapest/most invisible first)

1. **The unmistakable image.** One concrete, recognizable visual detail
   that a general audience decodes on sight, requiring no dialogue at all.
   (A painted lunar backdrop behind a cockpit window beats any amount of
   correct NASA terminology — the audience doesn't need to know what
   "guidance internal" means if they can see the Moon through the glass.)
2. **The planted plain word.** One ordinary, non-technical word spoken
   naturally by a character for whom saying it is completely in-voice —
   ideally as a joke, an aside, or a throwaway, not a definition. (A wife's
   dry "get back from the moon by ten" instead of any character explaining
   what the machine is.)
3. **The incidental answer.** Route the plain information through
   something a character was ALREADY going to say for their own reasons —
   an inventory recitation, a checklist read-aloud, a label being applied —
   so the audience learns it as a side effect of watching the character do
   their own business, not as a briefing aimed at the audience.
   (A bureaucrat's inventory list naturally includes the plain descriptor
   because that's literally his job in the scene.)
4. **The character who breaks to explain.** When the causal chain is
   genuinely too dense for images or incidental answers to carry alone, let
   a SPECIFIC character, for a SPECIFIC in-character reason (loyalty, guilt,
   decency, self-interest), say the plain version to another character —
   never to the audience. This costs the most (it's the closest to "telling")
   so it's the last resort, and it must be earned by the relationship: the
   explainer needs a reason to break from the norm and say the true thing
   out loud, or the beat reads as authorial.
5. **Recognize when opacity is the intended effect, not a bug.** Some
   confusion is the point — a stonewalling bureaucrat SHOULD sound like he's
   dodging the question. Don't fix intentional opacity by making the
   antagonist suddenly clear; fix it by giving the AUDIENCE a second,
   separate channel (a different character, an image, an incidental detail)
   that gets them the plain truth even while the antagonist keeps stonewalling
   the protagonist. The character stays opaque; the audience doesn't have to.

## What this pass is NOT

- Not permission to add an as-you-know-Bob monologue. Every fix above is
  motivated by something a character is already doing or would plausibly
  do, not a pause in the plot to brief the audience.
- Not a reason to state theme, meaning, or feeling. Legibility is "what
  happened and why," never "what it means" — that's still show-not-tell's
  job, untouched.
- Not a demand for total clarity everywhere. Held cards, withheld reveals,
  and intentional dramatic irony are unaffected — this pass targets baseline
  WHAT-IS-HAPPENING comprehension, not the story's controlled reveals.

## Origin

Caught by the author on THE FOUR OLDS: the barn simulator was never
visually or verbally anchored as a Moon-landing trainer (sc03), and the
Dawes farm seizure scene (sc05) buried its causal chain — why federal power
overrode a local process, why "excluded from auction" was the worst
outcome rather than a mercy — in a single dense bureaucratic exchange with
no second channel for the audience once the antagonist (correctly, by
design) refused to explain himself. Fixed via technique 1 (painted lunar
backdrop), technique 2 (Peg's "back from the moon"), technique 3 (Pell's
own inventory line naming it), and technique 4 (Wade breaking from
procedure to tell Cricket, and the audience, the plain version).

Runs in the SUBSTANCE battery, alongside [[intrigue-builder-pass]] and
LIKABILITY — before finishing polish, since a legibility fix can add or
reshape a beat, not just clean a line.
