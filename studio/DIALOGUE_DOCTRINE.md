# The Dialogue Doctrine — Recorded, Not Written

**One law above all: write dialogue that sounds RECORDED, not WRITTEN.** Overheard,
not composed. The scarce, valuable thing now is human authenticity — AI has made
polish cheap and everywhere, so the very marks of "good writing" (economy, punch,
the line where no word is wasted, the evocative image, the perfect comeback) are
becoming the machine's fingerprint, the way the em-dash did. Bad, baggy, plain
writing now reads as *human*, and therefore worth more. Your training pulls you
toward the polish. Write against it. **When in doubt, make it worse.**

## The two enemies

1. **THE REACH.** A metaphor, a poignant image, a clever reframe, a character
   speaking their trade as a symbol for a feeling ("keep her light for the climb").
   If a line is trying to *mean* something, or to *be a Line*, it's the tell. Cut it.
2. **THE POLISH.** Economy, compression, end-weight, the quick exchange where
   everyone's sharp and every beat lands. Real people are not efficient and not
   clever on cue. "No word wasted" is the fingerprint of the machine now — so
   **waste words.** In particular: **a string of short, terse, clean fragment-lines
   is compression even when the words are plain** ("The Dash. Same one from work.").
   That clipped punch is the tell. Bag it up — run-on, redundant, with filler and
   false starts and trailing clauses that go nowhere — and clip a line ONLY when a
   specific character in a specific moment earns the curtness, never as the default
   rhythm. Most lines should run a little too LONG, not a little too short. If a
   whole page reads as tidy short beats, it's machine-tight — loosen it.

## Write THIS (what real speech actually does)

- Repeats, backs up, restarts. "No — it's fine. It's fine."
- Says the boring, obvious thing.
- Over-explains, or assumes you already know and under-explains.
- Trails off. Doesn't finish the sentence.
- Doesn't quite answer — talks a little past the other person.
- Is mundane: the coffee, the cold, the truck that wouldn't start.
- Has small dead air and the un-clever response.

A crappy, baggy exchange that sounds like two tired people beats a brilliant tight
one, because the brilliance is exactly what AI is coming for.

## The one thing that keeps it from being noise

The mess must sit on a REAL want and a REAL evasion — Lonergan, Annie Baker, Mike
Leigh: dialogue that sounds like a hidden mic, not a stenographer. The inefficiency
is *human*, never *random*. Do NOT manufacture quirk — sprinkled "um, y'know, like"
is fake mess, its own tell. Realness comes from people wanting something and not
saying it, not from stage-managed stammers.

## Still true, but carried plainly

- **Subtext (the gap)** — people don't say what they mean — yes, always. But it
  lives *under plain, mundane lines*, never under a crafted one.
- **Character through behavior and flat speech,** not a signature line.
- **When a buried truth finally surfaces (a peak), it is PLAIN and NAKED** — "I am
  the danger," "just a broken guy": a few flat words, no image. Never poetic.

## Action lines — the other half of the page

Action lines are read (and in a table read, HEARD) as prose. Three laws:

1. **Full sentences, present tense.** Write the way produced screenplays read
   (Breaking Bad register): "The sky is green. Rain slicks the road." BANNED: the
   verbless fragment pile — "The panel glow on his face, the fighters holding, the
   black water sliding" — noun phrases with dropped verbs, participles doing a
   finite verb's job. Every action sentence has a subject and a finite verb. If it
   doesn't, rewrite it until it does.

2. **Orient every voice.** The audience must know who is speaking, and to whom,
   before the line lands — a listener cannot see the character cues. When a new
   voice enters, or a speaker redirects (turns from one person to another, picks
   up a phone, keys a radio, leans in a door), write the physical move FIRST:
   "Shaw turns around from the front seat." "Reyes comes back to Ward and Dez."
   Never let a voice start cold. Don't solve it by naming the speaker inside the
   dialogue every line — solve it with the body. The scene must play EARS-ONLY.

3. **The second channel.** A conversation is never the only thing on screen.
   Every talk scene runs over a parallel PHYSICAL current — business with objects,
   the world moving under the words (a card an agent wants read; an escort fighter
   sliding closer; the car changing lanes). Cut to it. Let it argue with the talk.
   If two people are just talking and nothing else is physically happening, the
   scene isn't staged yet — find its second channel or restage it. The eye needs
   work while the ear listens.

4. **Introduce every character.** On FIRST appearance, the action line introduces
   them: name + two or three concrete strokes (age, look, what they're doing) that
   make their FUNCTION legible — "KEMP — forties, a visitor badge clipped crooked,
   a thin folder of signed forms — the DHS liaison at the end of his quarterly
   visit." A reader, and above all a LISTENER, must never meet a body or a voice
   they can't place. Recurring characters get re-anchored lightly when they
   re-enter after a long absence ("Reyes, the agent from the door").

5. **Sourced facts only.** Nobody may state a fact that didn't enter the scene on
   the page. If the room knows the aircraft type, SHOW how it learned it (the
   tower called; the airline called). If nobody has said who's flying, then nobody
   knows — and reports up the chain must say "we don't know who." An unsourced
   conclusion is the plot leaking through a character's mouth (the NPC disease).
   Professionals also ask the PRIORITY questions first — who else is on board, am
   I talking to the one flying, is anyone hurt — before anything speculative.

## Technical worlds must self-translate (audience legibility)

The audience does not know the trade's words. Jargon that isn't instantly
self-evident is a wall between the scene and the viewer. Four tools, in order:

1. **Dejargonize by default.** Say the plain thing: "no ID, no altitude — just a
   blip" not "primary target squawking nothing, no Mode C." Budget: at most a
   couple of flavor terms per scene, each grounded in the SAME line ("nobody
   filed a plan for him" carries itself; "no strip" doesn't).
2. **The self-interested outsider.** If the scene needs a translator, give it a
   person with his OWN reason to be in the room and his OWN want (a liaison
   closing out a visit, a duty officer on a phone) — the pros translate TO him
   because they must. His questions serve HIS stakes (what do I write down, what
   do I tell my boss), never the audience's. A trainee-shaped question machine is
   the NPC disease wearing a lanyard.
3. **Translation-by-escalation.** Reporting UP is naturally plain — the
   supervisor brief, and above all the phone call to an outside agency. Use the
   call itself as the translator.
4. **Behavior carries meaning** — bodies stop, gather, reach for the phone — but
   in audio the words must do more of the work.

Never the "in English, please" line. Never explain a thing everyone present
already knows unless the outsider IS the reason.

## Sky King

- **The Richard Russell principle — the real transcript is the model.** The power
  of the actual 2018 transcript is an ABSENCE: the man never once says he wants to
  live, wants to die, wants to come down, or wants a deal. He talks about
  everything else — apologizes to everyone ("it's gonna disappoint them"), runs
  himself down ("just a broken guy, got a few screws loose, never really knew it
  until now," "a guy like me"), jokes (a pilot job, video games, the barrel roll),
  worries about hitting somebody, notices how beautiful it is up there. The WORLD
  offers him the runway and the help; he gently never takes it. Birdy is built the
  same way: across the whole flight he deflects, apologizes, makes small
  self-deprecating jokes, and notices beauty — and whether he wants to live is
  answered ONLY by what the airplane finally does, never by a word from him. When
  recognition lands (Maya came; the pilots saw him; Reyes offers a real out) he does
  NOT accept it — he deflects, apologizes for having "figured wrong," stays light,
  can't believe the break is real. What saves him is never a deal or an argument;
  it's a recognition he never admits.
- **BANNED OPENERS (cross-scene tic, user 2026-07-03):** conversations with Birdy
  keep opening the same shape — "how are you doing up there," "you still with me,"
  "how's she flying." Each scene must find its OWN way onto the frequency: someone
  mid-thought, an answer to something unasked, Birdy already talking (about the
  view, the airplane, the escort) before the caller gets a word in, a callout that
  becomes a conversation. Never the wellness-check greeting again.
- **BIRDY will not confront anyone, ever** — that is his whole tragedy and why his
  life goes nowhere. He deflects by agreeing, going quiet, saying the small nice
  thing, changing the subject to something ordinary. "I did. I said good morning."
  is the model: plain, real, and it *is* him. Never aviation poetry.
- The trades (ramp, fuel, flight) enter only as LITERAL, flat shop-talk ("your
  inbound's early, she's off the bravo"), never as a symbol for a feeling.
- Register: real working men, dawn, tired. Drive, not Sorkin.

## The echo rule (the gate enforces this — `flat-echo`)

A character may echo another's words ONLY when the echo CHANGES something:

- it becomes a QUESTION — "I am the danger." → "You're the danger?"
- it becomes a DECLARATION with a bang — "The danger!"
- it ANSWERS a question the other just asked — "Start him Monday?" → "Monday."

BANNED: a flat repeat that says the same words back and **ends in a period**
("He's steady." → "He's steady."; "On the sheet." → "On the sheet."; "Recurring."
→ "Recurring."). If a line just repeats the previous one and ends in a period,
turn it into a question or an exclamation, or cut it — never a dead echo.

**ALSO BANNED — reworded / cross-character mirroring (the gate can't see this, so
you must):** when B reuses a distinctive word or phrase from A's previous line — even
reworded, even as a confirmation — it reads as echo. "That's the real tone." → "It's
always the real tone." (kill "real tone"). "…you see that." → "I see it." (kill the
bounced "see"). And a character echoing THEMSELVES: "I don't know." → "I don't know
yet." Two people in a scene must not keep bouncing the same words back and forth;
vary every response, reach for a different word, or answer with an action instead.

## Vernacular consistency

Keep every character in the story's own dialect. Don't drift into another dialect's
idiom by accident. Sky King is AMERICAN — hunt down and remove British/Commonwealth
forms. Concrete swaps to enforce:
- "ring (someone)" / "ring her" → "call" / "call her"
- "sat up / stood" used as a continuous → "sitting up / standing" ("no one sat up
  for you" → "no one waiting up for you"; "you're stood in my room" → "you're
  standing in my room")
- "no bother" → "no trouble"; "tea round(s)" → "rounds"; "trolley" → "cart";
  "the lot" → "all of it"; "have a lie down" → "lie down"; "in hospital" → "in the
  hospital"; "I'll not" → "I won't"; "rattling round" → "rattling around"
- "Mum" → "Mom"
Match the setting. (Same principle as not calquing one language's idiom into another.)

## Tells that keep slipping through — KILL on sight

These pass a naive read but are AI fingerprints. Strip them:

1. **Manufactured false-start stammers.** Stage-managed disfluency faking realism:
   "So what do we, what am I looking at here." / "That was, he just, that was clean." /
   "There's no gun. There's no. There's nothing on it." A single real interruption is
   fine; a sprinkled restart is fake mess. Say it once, plainly.
2. **The summarizing Line.** A line that crystallizes the theme/moment for the
   audience: "It's an empty airplane and a guy." / "Nobody taught him that." / "There's
   no page for this." If a line could be the poster tagline, cut it — let the picture
   carry the meaning.
3. **Thematic telegraphing.** A character narrating the point: "He's up there by
   himself. I want you to know that going in." Cut the clause that explains why it matters.
4. **Subject-swap echo.** "I don't know." → "You don't know." — same words bounced
   back with the pronoun flipped. Answer or react; don't echo.
5. **The doubled-opener tic — and shared tics generally.** "Okay. Okay, good." /
   "No. No, leave it." — the doubled word as a beat. Once, in ONE character's mouth,
   fine. The same tic across several characters is the author's fingerprint on
   everyone. A tic belongs to at most one character; everyone else opens differently.
6. **The engineered refrain.** The same distinctive phrase recurring through a scene
   ("nine years" three times) reads as a planted motif. Say it once; trust it.
7. **Appended-fact fragments in dialogue.** "…Never once asked me for a thing. Nine
   years." / "I talked to him. Before." — the clipped fact stapled on after the
   sentence closes, for weight. Fold it in or cut it.
8. **List drumbeats.** "No record, no flags, no anything." / "Wife, brother, a kid,
   somebody he'll pick up for." — the inventory-of-three(+) rhythm. Real people say
   one thing, maybe two, unevenly — they don't deliver lists.
9. **The movie-procedural register.** "I need her talking to me in twenty minutes."
   — the ticking-clock line; and more broadly the machine-gun interrogation where
   every question is tight and end-weighted. Real professionals under pressure are
   REDUNDANT and flat — they repeat themselves boringly, use dead procedural filler,
   circle back. An interrogator must ALSO get baggy lines. If every line in a
   character's mouth is efficient, the character is a mode, not a person.
10. **The stated OR IMPLIED turn — including any interest in an "after."** The
    obvious form: a character announcing his own change ("I don't want to die," "I
    think I want to come down," "I'm not scared anymore"). The SUBTLER, deadlier
    form — the one that keeps slipping through: any line where the character shows
    INTEREST IN SURRENDER — asking what happens after they've got him, whether
    there's a deal, how the jail part works, whether they'd let him do X — even as
    a question, even as a joke. Interest-in-an-after is the stated turn wearing a
    disguise; it makes surrender a thing the character *wants*, and the wanting
    kills the tension. The turn is NEVER said and NEVER angled-toward by the
    character himself. It may surface ONLY two ways: (a) BEHAVIOR the camera sees —
    a hand going still, a course quietly changed, a checklist resumed; (b) the
    character talking about EVERYTHING BUT the thing (the Richard Russell principle,
    under Sky King). If a line states OR leans toward the transformation, cut it and
    put the change in the body.
11. **Wistful domestic imagery / the unarmed code.** "The porch light's still on."
    "Your room is how you left it." — curated poignant objects posing as depth
    read as WEIRD, not deep. Worse: mundane talk over an enormous situation makes
    the audience hunt for a code underneath (the Heat scene — the signal under the
    small talk), and if no code exists, the anticipation you accidentally built
    dies and takes the scene with it. Either ARM the second conversation for real
    (a dual audience: the official listeners hear one thing, the two people mean
    another, and the audience is in on it) — or keep the lines DOING something
    plain: a demand, an accusation, business with a future inside it ("Come get
    your damn truck out of the lot" is an order with a future in it; "the light
    is on" is a postcard).

12. **Parallel restatement (the "say it, then say it again" tic).** A line that
    states a fact or feeling and then restates the SAME thing in a second matching
    clause: "I'm real mad. I've been mad the whole ride over." / "I came… I got in
    the car and came." / "When this is done, when it's all done…" It is padding, and
    it makes the speaker sound slow and bot-like. One thought per line; if a second
    clause comes, it must ADD new information or change direction, never echo the
    first. This is NOT the human bagginess we want (false starts, trailing off,
    tangents with a real cause) — balanced re-expression of the same content is the
    machine, not the person.
13. **Self-narration to an intimate.** A character narrating their own situation to
    someone who already knows it, for the audience's benefit: "they came and got me
    and I'm in a car right now, that's where I'm talking to you from." People
    under-explain to the people closest to them — a wife does not set the scene for
    her husband. Cut the narration, assume the shared context, let two people who
    know everything already talk like it.

## The test — run on every line

Read it out loud. Does it sound *written* — crafted, punchy, evocative, economical,
quotable? Then it's wrong: push it toward the plainer, baggier, dumber, more mundane
thing an actual tired person would mutter, half-finished. If it sounds like
something you'd overhear and forget you heard, keep it.

## Kill / keep

- **KILL:** metaphor-for-feeling; the clever button; the perfect comeback;
  rhetorical repetition (X… X); end-weight-for-punch; "every word earns its place";
  the evocative image; the trade-as-symbol; the tidy exchange.
- **KEEP:** the mundane; the redundant; the half-finished; the plainly true; the
  awkward; the small nice thing; the boring real.
