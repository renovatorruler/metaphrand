# Voice Guide

> **The layer law (2026-06-12):** "if it sounds like writing, cut it" now has an enforcement
> arm — the `clear-pane` skill (~/.claude/skills/clear-pane). Intrigue belongs to events,
> meaning, and intent; the text layer is a clear pane. Six families, thirty-one devices,
> mandatory in every pipeline, every story.

How the prose should sound, what every scene has to carry underneath, and what keeps the reader turning. Run any draft through this. If a page fails it, the page gets rewritten — not patched.

## The one rule

**If it sounds like writing, cut it.** (Leonard.)

AI prose is the statistical average of all prose, so it reads like no one — smooth, balanced, tasteful, and dead. A real voice is *specific*: it has grain, tics, a sensibility, things it stares at and things it will not say. Every line should sound like one particular human wrote it, not the mean of everyone who ever did.

## 1. The grain

**The narrator is never neutral.** A neutral narrator is just the AI average wearing a tie. In close third, the prose wears the voice of whoever we're following — their diction, their rhythm, what they'd notice, what they'd never say out loud. Solve the character's voice and you've solved the narrator's. (Per-character coloring: see `04-VOICE_FROM_CHART.md`.)

**This book's compass: Elmore Leonard.** Lean, dialogue-forward, cool, propulsive, invisible. The rules we hold to:

- Don't open on weather or atmosphere. Open on a person doing or saying something.
- Dialogue carries the scene. The prose steps back. White space is a tool.
- "Said." No adverbs on it, no fancy tags (no *snarled*, no *said gravely*).
- Barely describe faces or places — only what this POV would actually clock.
- Violence is fast and flat. No choreography, no lingering.
- Cut the parts readers skip.
- Dialect and regional voice: sparingly.

*(The compass is a slot. A different book sets a different writer here; everything below stays the same.)*

## 2. The slop kill-list

Hunt these and cut them. Each one is a tell readers now read as machine-written.

- **Fake gravitas by fragmentation** — chopped sentences and one-word paragraphs for "impact." *He counted. You counted first, always. Four.* → *Caleb counted four riders.*
- **Second-person life-lessons** smuggled into close third. *You looked at the road. You counted what was on it.* Cut the sermon; just say what he did.
- **The reach for poignancy** — the tidy lump-in-the-throat closer. *…and meaning it, and all of them, for a minute, believing him.* End flat. Trust the reader to feel it.
- **Telling the feeling / abstract face-reads.** *Something mean and amused crossed his face.* Show the act, not the label.
- **Rule of three.** *Smooth, balanced, tasteful.* Two is honest; three is a jingle.
- **Negative parallelism.** *Not just a song — a statement.*
- **Em-dash and semicolon literariness.** Most of the time a period does it.
- **Synonym-cycling.** *The protagonist… the man… the hero…* Use the name, or "he."
- **Any line you *notice* as writing.** If it's admiring itself, it's out.

## 3. Every scene carries four layers (PaRDeS)

Before writing a scene, name all four. Only the first is on the page; the rest work from underneath.

- **Peshat — the surface.** What literally happens. It must stand on its own: a reader who got *only* this would still have a complete, gripping scene. Write it clean and straight.
- **Remez — the hint.** What it means metaphorically, what it rhymes with — the resonance just beneath the surface. Felt, never stated. *(Opening scene: the slave patrol — the hunt, the harbored fugitive, "which plantation you run from.")*
- **Derash — the aim.** What the scene is *for* — its thematic and moral work, what we're trying to land. *(Opening: America's oldest sin doesn't stay buried; the measure of a man is who he'll shelter when it comes back.)*
- **Sod — the secret.** The truth we're driving toward that we are *not* describing — the thing the surface hides. Sod governs what must stay **off** the page. *(Opening: it isn't the past. It's the future.)* If a scene's Sod detonates later, the surface gives zero tells. **A leaked Sod kills the reveal** — that's the whole meaning of "don't tip it."

## 4. The drama — the scene's engine (Mamet)

A scene that isn't a fight is a postcard. Before the layers, the scene needs:

1. **Who wants what from whom?**
2. **What happens if they don't get it?** (the stakes)
3. **Why now?**

If two characters are only trading information, it's dead. Make them want something from each other, and make one of them lose.

## 5. Momentum — the page-turner rules

The reader must never reach a place to comfortably stop. These hold at every scale — the line, the scene, the chapter, the book.

- **Never close a loop without opening a bigger one.** Every answer hands the reader a new question. The instant they feel caught up — *I'm square with this story, I could set it down* — the book dies in their hands. The unit isn't the twist; it's the unanswered question.
- **Run on dread, not surprise.** Surprise is a one-second jolt; suspense carries ten pages. Let the reader know more than the characters and point them at the thing they can see coming — Hitchcock's bomb under the table. Most of the pull should be *I'm scared of what's about to happen,* not *I wonder what happens.*
- **Momentum is subtraction.** It's what's left when you cut everything that lets the reader rest: the establishing scene, the info-dump, the quiet beat that ties off a thread. The enemy isn't bad prose — it's the satisfying pause.
- **End on the turn, not the landing.** No scene and no chapter closes at rest. Cut the last paragraph that resolves the tension; stop on the tilt.
- **Yes, but / no, and.** No problem is ever solved clean. Every solution spawns a worse one; the screw only tightens.
- **Stakes you can see, and a clock when you can get one.** A face, a child, a thing that can be lost — never an abstraction. A deadline sharpens any scene (*ten minutes to pack*).

**Modulation — because octane fatigue kills the same book.** Sustained maximum tension numbs: if every scene is a gunfight, the gunfights stop landing. A page-turner is a wave, not a flat line at 11, and the valleys are not optional.

- **Release is not resolution.** This is the whole trick. The pause you cut is *resolution* — the reader feeling safe, caught up, free to stop. A breather is the opposite: relief from the *action* while the dread keeps a hand on the reader's throat. The loop stays open; only the surface goes quiet. Give rest from the noise, never from the fear.
- **Change the color of the tension, don't kill it.** Action-tension is the exhausting kind. Drop it and pick up another frequency — relational, or the dramatic irony of what we know is coming. Two people not saying the thing can run tighter than a gunfight, with no gun in the room.
- **Valleys do the work.** The quiet scenes are where the reader falls deeper in love with the people, which is the only reason the next peak hurts. A quiet beat that deepens the bond is raising the stakes — propulsion, not a pause. So "cut the quiet beat" has a second half: cut the one that does nothing; keep the one that deepens the bond or tightens the screw.
- **Exhale on the next wave.** End the breather by letting the next threat show on the horizon: the reader relaxes, and on the relaxation sees it coming. Recover and reload in the same beat.
- **It's a marathon.** You can't sprint a reader four hundred pages. The dread is the constant; the surface breathes — or the reader breathes for you, by closing the book.

## 6. The pass

Run a draft through, in order:

1. **Drama** — is there a want, a cost, a *now*? If not, fix that before anything else.
2. **Surface** — is the Peshat clean and self-sufficient? Does it tip the Sod anywhere? Cut the tell.
3. **Voice** — is it in this POV's grain, Leonard-lean?
4. **Slop** — run the kill-list. Cut every line that sounds like writing.
5. **Momentum** — does the scene open more than it closes, and end on the turn? Could the reader comfortably stop here? (If they could, that's a defect.) And mind the wave: after a peak, *change the color* — release the action, never the dread — instead of stacking another peak.
6. **Cold read** — does a specific person sound like they wrote this, or the average of everyone who ever did?
