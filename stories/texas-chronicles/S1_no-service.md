# TEXAS CHRONICLES — S1: "NO SERVICE"

*A complete case: a man tries to collect a forged debt off Del's estate; August reads him and turns it.
Concept: how you defend a claim with no court — you read the man and you make him stake. Serialized
threads planted in the tag: Del's deliberate, early summoning of Miles; the circling on his accounts;
Miles's own reason for fleeing the Union. Placeholders: MILES HART (heir/Watson), DEL HART (dead uncle),
AUGUST (master/lead), SHELBY (associate), NORWOOD (the claimant), RIO BRAZOS MUTUAL (agency). v6.*

---

**COLD OPEN — SERIES SIGNATURE**

A silver coin rings down onto a diner counter to pay for a coffee, and a weathered hand sweeps it into a brass till already heavy with more. Over it, plain text:

> Forty years ago, Texas left the United States. It has lived without a government ever since, and out here the law is private.

Hold. Cut.

---

**EXT. INTERSTATE 35, TEXAS SIDE — DAY**

A single bus runs down a wide, clean highway. Green country to either side — irrigated fields, fat cattle, a pump-jack nodding in the distance, and on the horizon a travel-stop the size of a cathedral.

**INT. BUS — CONTINUOUS**

MILES HART (33, a good coat gone shabby) watches the country roll past. He lifts his phone. The top corner reads NO SERVICE. He thumbs it and kills airplane mode, but nothing happens. He leans toward the woman across the aisle — sixtyish, in plain clothes cut from very good cloth, a paperback open in her lap.

MILES: Is there wifi on this, or—

She looks at him like he sneezed in church, and goes back to her book.

**EXT. DALLAS — INTERMODAL STOP — DAY**

The bus sighs open, and Miles steps down into the city. Cool air breathes off a misted patio in the hundred-degree heat. A family of seven loads into a truck built to hold them. Gold and silver move hand to hand on the sidewalk, and nobody watches it go.

A young woman leans on an old truck kept like a watch, holding a square of cardboard that reads **HART.** This is SHELBY (29, Dallas-born, dressed for the work and not for him).

SHELBY: You're shorter than your picture.

MILES: ...Sorry?

SHELBY: Del kept one on the wall.

MILES: Del — okay, there's supposed to be a lawyer. For the estate.

A beat. Her read shifts. He doesn't know.

SHELBY: Your uncle died three weeks ago. (off his blank face) Get in. There's a man at the office you're going to want to not pay.

**INT./EXT. SHELBY'S TRUCK — DALLAS — DAY (MOVING)**

Miles, rattled. The city goes by: a money-changer's window stacked with gold, a small drone lifting off a rooftop with a parcel slung under it.

MILES: Wait. What man at the office?

SHELBY: Norwood. He says Del owed him. Came around the day after the funeral with a note and a smile.

MILES: A note for how much?

SHELBY: Eleven hundred grams. Says Del cheated him on a deal. Del can't argue, being dead, so Norwood figures it's easy money.

MILES: And it comes out of the estate. Out of — that's my estate. That's what I came to sell.

SHELBY: That's why August hasn't paid him.

A tearing roar passes overhead. Miles flinches and ducks. Two FIGHTER JETS rip low across the sky and are gone, Union markings on their wings.

MILES: (heart going) What was—

SHELBY: Union. They do a pass. (she doesn't look up) The sky's all they've got.

**INT. RIO BRAZOS MUTUAL — DAY**

A busy, prosperous storefront — brass and good wood, a wall of cards, a scale for weighing gold on the counter, men working the phones, a calculating machine clattering in the back. At a desk sits NORWOOD (50s, pressed, a salesman's easy warmth), a leather folio across his knees. Rising from the back without hurry is AUGUST (60s), in the plainest, best-made clothes in the room.

Miles scans the room for whoever's in charge and lands on the polished man.

MILES: (to Norwood) Sorry — I'm Del's nephew, I'm here about the estate, are you—

NORWOOD: (warm, rising) Ah. The heir. My condolences, truly. Your uncle and I had a piece of business, and I'm afraid the timing's—

AUGUST: (mild, from behind him) Sit down, Norwood.

Miles turns. The plain old man is the one the room answers to.

AUGUST: (to Miles) You'd be looking for me.

NORWOOD: (smooth, unbothered) Eleven hundred grams, August. I have his note. I have his mark. I take no pleasure in it, a man barely buried — but a debt doesn't die with the debtor.

AUGUST: No. It doesn't.

He takes the note, glances at it, hands it back. Doesn't keep it.

MILES: (low, to August) Can't we just check it? Pull his records, see if the deal was even—

AUGUST: Check it where, son. There's no where. (beat) You don't look a thing up here. You look at the man.

He turns back to Norwood.

AUGUST: You want eleven hundred out of Del's estate, you'll stake it, same as anyone. Put up your surety, and we open that note to showdown — me holding it in the light, you watching me hold it. If the claim's good, you leave eleven hundred richer. If it isn't, your stake is mine. That's the cost of asking.

NORWOOD: (a beat; the warmth holds) Of course.

He counts out gold onto the desk — quick, confident, a man with nothing to hide.

AUGUST: (watching the hands, not the gold) You're quick to it.

NORWOOD: I'm a busy man.

AUGUST: Mm.

He picks the note back up and holds it to the window light.

AUGUST: Walnut Street deal. He signs it March twelfth.

NORWOOD: If that's the date on it, that's the date.

AUGUST: (quiet) We put Del in the ground on the ninth.

The room goes still.

AUGUST: You've got a dead man signing your note three days after his own funeral.

Norwood's warmth goes somewhere and doesn't come back.

MILES: (catching up) The date's — it's *after* —

AUGUST: He wrote a claim against a dead man and bet the only one left to check was a boy from up north who couldn't tell the funeral from the deal. (to Norwood, not unkind) Open it to showdown. Hold it up to me. Go on.

Norwood doesn't move.

AUGUST: Then you fold. And your stake stays here.

Norwood rises, slow, and buttons his coat. The smile comes back, colder.

NORWOOD: Keep it. (at the door, he stops) You think I'm the only one who came sniffing? Half the metroplex moved on Del's accounts before he was cold. (beat) You ought to ask yourself why every one of them was so sure there'd be nobody left to stop it.

He goes.

Miles looks at the gold on the desk — Norwood's, a moment ago. Now it isn't.

MILES: That's... mine?

AUGUST: That's Del's. Which makes it the seat's. Which today makes it yours.

He slides a file across the desk. Miles's name is on the tab, in an older man's hand.

AUGUST: He left you that.

Miles opens it. His own name. His Union address. Pages of notes in a hand he's never seen, about a life nobody down here should have been following. And a date on the cover.

MILES: This is— he set the whole thing up. The seat, the partnership, all of it. (off the date) This is dated in February.

AUGUST: It is.

MILES: Shelby said he died three weeks ago. He made me his partner *months* before—

AUGUST: (nothing on his face) Del did a lot of things early.

A beat. Miles doesn't know what to do with that, so he reaches for the thing he came in with.

MILES: Look — whatever this is, I can't stay. I've got something back home I have to deal with. I came here to sell.

AUGUST: I know what you came to do. (beat) Sit in the chair a while first. You just watched a man try to rob your uncle in the grave, and you watched him pay for it with no judge in the building. (mild) You're not as ready to leave as you were this morning.

Miles looks at the file. At the gold. At the door Norwood went out. He doesn't say yes.

He also doesn't go.

**CUT TO BLACK. END S1.**
