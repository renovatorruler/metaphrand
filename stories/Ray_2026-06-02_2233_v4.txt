RAY

The man on the next stool had a son in the service and the bad sense to be proud of it out loud. Ray let him get all the way to "somebody's got to stand up for this country" before he set his beer down.

"Name the rubber company," Ray said.

"What?"

"The one that owns the plantations your boy's defending. You can't. But the fella who signed the shipping contract can, and he won't be in any rice paddy, and neither will his sons. Yours will. That's not standing up for your country. That's standing up for a man you'll never meet, who'd step over your kid's body to make his train." He picked the beer back up. "But you go ahead and be proud."

The proud man's mouth worked and nothing came out of it. Down the bar somebody laughed and somebody bought Ray a beer, and that was the war, as far as Ray ever fought it — a back booth, a captive jury, and not one round he could lose.

What none of them saw was Ray's right hand under the table, balled up so tight the knuckles had gone white, his whole arm humming like a strung wire. The fight in him was real. It had just never found but the one way out, and that was through his teeth. Put a rifle in that hand instead of a sentence and it would go to ice on him — he knew it for a fact, and he'd spent thirty years making sure he never had to learn it twice.

He'd learned it once. He'd been fourteen.

When Tommy was nine he came up from the creek with his lip split and one eye gone to plum, grinning like he'd won money. Some kid two years bigger had been working him over down there, so Tommy went back the next day, and the day after that, and got taken apart each time, until the bigger boy quit coming to the creek altogether — not because he'd won, but because there's a thing in a kid who keeps standing back up that a grown man can't stand to look at for long. Ray watched the whole week of it from the porch. Ran the numbers, and stayed right where he sat, and told himself it was the smart play, and told himself a great many things, across a great many years, from that porch.

Tommy never had Ray's trick of turning the thing into words. So he didn't announce he'd enlisted — couldn't have made the speech if you'd paid him for it. Ray came down one morning and the boy was at the kitchen table with a folded paper squared in front of him, smoothing it flat under his thumb, and he looked up and said, "Went down to the courthouse." That was the whole of it.

Ray gave him the courthouse back, and then some. The rubber companies, the shipping contracts, the casualty tables by province, what a body's worth to the men who spend them. It was the best he ever argued, because it was the only time it ever counted, and Tommy sat through every word of it turning his coffee cup in slow circles, and when Ray finally ran dry the kid nodded like he'd been agreeing the whole time. "I know all that, Ray." And got up to pack.

Ray went to the window. He watched his brother cross the yard in the gray morning with that paper in his shirt pocket, walking off toward the worst idea Ray had ever heard out loud, and Ray's hand came up flat against the cold glass — and stayed there. Didn't knock. Didn't call out. He stood at that window with his heart going like a fist on a door until the boy was a smudge at the end of the road, and he never did put a name to what he felt, only that he could not watch and could not look away, and that he'd have traded his good right arm to be the one walking.

The telegram came in November. Ray read it on the porch and didn't sit down and didn't make a sound.

At the service he had the only dry face in three pews. When the line came past with their soft hands and their he's with God now, he took each one and let it go and gave back nothing, until Tommy's old Sunday teacher held on and told him the Lord never sends a man more than he can carry. Ray looked at her till she let go. "He didn't send it," he said. "A man in an office did. I can get you the name." And walked out, and left her standing there with her mouth open the way he'd left a hundred men at the bar — except there was no jury this time, and nobody laughed, and it gave him not one thing.

Because he wasn't grieving, and it took him weeks to find the bottom of that. Grief was clean. This had a current in it. The fire he'd spent his whole life watching from the porch had gone out in the one man who'd ever carried it where Ray could see — and now it had no place left to be but in Ray, who'd never once let it live there, and it wanted out.

One cold morning it found a way. He put his father's pistol in his coat and drove two towns over to the storefront with the flag in the window, to find the man who signed the papers and put a hole in him for it.

The recruiter was younger than he'd pictured, and tired, and didn't look up right off. And standing in that doorway with his hand inside his coat, Ray caught his own reflection in the dark glass of the door — a man, squared up, hand on a gun — and he felt the weight of the thing sitting in his palm sure and easy as a spoon. Steady. The same hand that went to ice every time the bar talk turned from the war to the going. Not now. Not with this. His body had known the whole time what he was, and had only ever been waiting on him to pick up the right tool and find out it didn't shake at all.

The recruiter looked up. "Help you?"

Ray took his hand off the pistol. "I want to sign up."

The first time the world came apart around him, he stood in it and waited to freeze — the way booth-Ray froze, the way he'd frozen on the porch. Mortars walking the tree line, dirt jumping, a man he'd split a cigarette with an hour before just gone. And the cold hands didn't come. What came was a terrible quiet, the inside of a held breath, the noise dropping away to nothing — and he found he'd already moved, was already low and going forward, dragging a kid named Echols out of the open by his webbing with one hand and putting fire back down the line with the other, all of it a half-second ahead of any deciding. He got Echols behind the berm and held that corner nine minutes he could never afterward account for, and when it went quiet and the kid was crying and thanking him, Ray couldn't hear a word of it for the thing ringing in his own chest, calm as Sunday: there you are. There you are.

He came home two years on, on his own two legs.

The booth was still there. So was the smoke and the bar and a sharp kid Ray didn't know holding the floor, nursing one beer, explaining to the room that it was a rich man's war and a poor man's funeral, that nobody could even name the rubber company — and the regulars grinning, a couple of them cutting looks at the stool by the door where Ray sat, because some of them remembered whose lines those were.

The kid felt the attention and swung it onto Ray, friendly, hungry, the way Ray used to swing it onto a whole room. "You were over there. So tell me I'm wrong."

And here's the thing the old Ray could not have done if his life hung on it: he didn't. He looked at the kid — the fast mouth, the captive jury, the loud safe little war a man can win every night of his life without once standing up — and felt no more pull toward it than toward a crib he'd grown out of. He finished his beer. Set a coin on the bar. On his way out he stopped at the kid's shoulder, and the whole room leaned in for it, the king come back to hold court.

"You're not wrong," Ray said. "Doesn't matter. Go anyway."

And he went out into the daylight, and the door swung shut on the kid still sitting there with his mouth open and his beer going warm, trying to work out whether he'd won.
