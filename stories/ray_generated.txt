**TITLE: THE PEN AND THE PISTOL**

**FADE IN:**

**INT. RAY’S APARTMENT – KITCHEN – NIGHT**

Rain taps the window. A single lamp burns low.

RAY, 38, sits at the table. A half-written letter in front of him. His fingers hover over the pen. He doesn’t touch it.

Across the room, on the fridge, a photo: RAY and TOMMY as boys, arms slung over each other’s shoulders, grinning. Tommy’s teeth are crooked, Ray’s even. Tommy’s fist is raised mid-punch. Ray’s is open, relaxed.

RAY picks up the letter. Reads it aloud, voice flat.

“Dear Mrs. Henderson. Thank you for your continued support of the veterans’ outreach initiative…”

He sets it down. Stares at his hands. They don’t tremble. They don’t move.

A knock at the door. He doesn’t answer.

The knock comes again — harder.

He stands. Walks to the door. Opens it.

ANNIE stands there, soaked. Her coat is dark with rain. She holds two mugs.

She doesn’t speak. Just hands him one.

He takes it. Looks past her, down the hallway.

ANNIE  
You’re still writing that letter.

RAY  
It’s not about writing it. It’s about getting the tone right.

ANNIE  
Tone doesn’t change the fact he’s dead.

Ray doesn’t look at her. He sips the coffee. Too hot. Sets it down.

ANNIE  
Tommy wouldn’t want you to stop living because he did.

Ray finally looks at her. His eyes don’t waver. But his jaw tightens.

RAY  
He believed in the wrong thing.

ANNIE  
Yeah. And you told him it was wrong.

RAY  
I told him it was stupid.

ANNIE  
Same thing in his ears.

Ray turns back to the letter. Picks up the pen. Sets it down again.

**INT. VETERANS’ OUTREACH OFFICE – DAY**

RAY sits in a plastic chair. Across the desk: RUTH, 50s, sharp suit, sharper eyes. A framed photo of EARL on her shelf — same build as Tommy, same squint.

RUTH  
He signed the papers, Ray. Voluntarily.

RAY  
I asked him not to.

RUTH  
You told him he’d regret it if he didn’t. That he’d be a coward if he stayed.

RAY  
I said he’d be unfulfilled. That’s different.

RUTH  
He read it the same way.

RAY  
He read *me* the wrong way.

RUTH  
Maybe. Or maybe he heard what he needed to hear.

Ray stands. Walks to the window. Looks down at the street.

RUTH  
He went in clean. Didn’t flinch. Didn’t ask for backup.

RAY  
He was always like that.

RUTH  
Yeah. Like Earl.

Ray doesn’t turn. His hand twitches at his side.

RAY  
I’m not Earl.

RUTH  
No. You’re not.

Ray sits back down. Picks up the form on the desk — a new one. Fills out nothing. Just holds the pen.

RUTH  
You could still go. Fill his slot. They’re short.

RAY  
I’m not going.

RUTH  
No. You’re not.

Ray stands again. Walks to the door. Pauses.

RAY  
I’ll be in touch.

RUTH  
You won’t.

He leaves. Door clicks shut behind him.

**INT. RAY’S APARTMENT – LIVING ROOM – NIGHT**

RAY sits on the couch. The letter lies open on the coffee table.

SULLY enters, wet coat flapping. He drops into the armchair.

SULLY  
Tommy’s widow called. Again.

RAY  
Tell her I’m not available.

SULLY  
She said you don’t have to be. She said you owe her.

RAY  
I owe him nothing.

SULLY  
He took your name off the letter. The one he sent before he left.

Ray looks up.

SULLY  
He crossed your name out. Wrote his own.

RAY  
He didn’t.

SULLY  
He did. I saw it. In the mail room. Before it got shredded.

Ray stands. Walks to the hallway closet. Opens it. Pulls out a duffel bag. Unzips it. Inside: a pistol. His father’s. Wrapped in oilcloth.

He closes the bag. Lifts it.

SULLY  
You’re not taking that.

RAY  
I am.

SULLY  
Tommy wouldn’t want that.

RAY  
He wouldn’t want this either.

He walks out. Door slams.

Sully stares at the empty space.

**EXT. RECRUITING STATION – DAWN**

A faded sign: *U.S. ARMY — BE ALL YOU CAN BE.*

Ray approaches the glass door. The reflection in the glass shows him — coat zipped, duffel slung over one shoulder. But in the glass, he sees something else.

His reflection holds the pistol steady. Right hand on the grip. Left hand supporting the wrist. No shake. No hesitation.

He stops.

Looks at the reflection. The reflection looks back.

Then, slowly, he unzips his coat. Pulls the pistol out. Opens the chamber — empty. Slides it back into the duffel. Zips the coat shut.

He steps inside.

The Recruiter looks up from his desk. Late 20s. Tired. A clipboard in front of him.

RECRUITER  
Name?

RAY  
Ray Henderson.

RECRUITER  
Henderson. Got a brother in the 101st.

RAY  
Tommy.

RECRUITER  
Yeah. He came in last week. Signed the same form you’re about to.

Ray doesn’t move. Doesn’t blink.

RECRUITER  
You want the same slot? I can get you in tomorrow.

Ray looks past him. To the wall. A poster: a soldier shaking hands with a child. Below it: *HONOR. DUTY. COURAGE.*

Ray walks to the desk. Pulls out the chair. Sits.

He opens the duffel. Pulls out the pistol — but doesn’t lift it.

He sets it down gently on the desk. Between them.

Then he picks up the pen.

**INT. RECRUITING STATION – MAIN ROOM – MOMENTS LATER**

Ray signs the form. Neat, precise script.

The Recruiter watches, silent.

RAY  
I’m not enlisting.

RECRUITER  
Then why sign?

RAY  
To prove I don’t need to.

He slides the form back.

RECRUITER  
You’re not the first to walk in with a gun and walk out with a pen.

RAY  
No.

RECRUITER  
But you’re the first who didn’t pull it.

Ray stands. Looks down at the pistol. Then at the pen.

He picks up the pen.

Walks to the door.

Pauses. Looks at his reflection.

This time, he sees only himself. No gun. No steady hand. Just a man holding a pen.

He opens the door.

**INT. RAY’S APARTMENT – KITCHEN – NIGHT**

RAY sits at the table. The letter is gone. In its place: a blank sheet.

He holds the pen. Writes.

The camera pulls back.

Through the window: Annie stands on the porch. She watches him through the glass.

He doesn’t look up.

She doesn’t knock.

She turns and walks away.

**INT. RAY’S APARTMENT – LIVING ROOM – LATER**

Ray sits on the couch. The duffel sits beside him. Empty now.

He holds a photo — him and Tommy, older. Tommy in uniform, Ray in a suit. They’re not smiling.

Ray sets the photo down.

He picks up the phone. Dials.

RAY  
June? It’s Ray.

A beat.

RAY  
I need to know if you still love the man who writes letters.

A pause. Then:  
RAY  
Because I’m done writing them.

He hangs up.

**INT. RAY’S APARTMENT – HALLWAY – NEXT DAY**

Ray stands outside the door. Knocks.

ANNIE opens it. She’s holding a suitcase.

She doesn’t step back. Doesn’t smile.

RAY  
I’m not the man you fell for.

ANNIE  
No.

RAY  
But I’m not the man who died, either.

She looks at his hands. They’re still. Strong.

ANNIE  
What do you want?

RAY  
I want to live.

She steps aside.

He walks in.

**INT. RAY’S APARTMENT – LIVING ROOM – NIGHT**

Ray sits in the armchair. Annie sits on the couch.

Between them: a fire.

He holds the pistol. Not loaded.

He sets it on the coffee table.

Then he picks up the pen.

Writes.

He doesn’t stop.

He doesn’t look up.

The fire crackles.

**FADE OUT.**
