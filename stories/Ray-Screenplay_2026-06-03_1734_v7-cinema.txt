RAY

A screenplay


FADE IN:

INT. DELANEY'S TAVERN - NIGHT

A low room going blue with smoke. A jukebox plays something with a fiddle in it. Half the working men in town are here, and every chair is turned, by a few degrees, toward the back booth.

In that booth: RAY HENDERSON, late thirties, slight, a face all edges and quick eyes, nursing one dead beer he has no intention of finishing.

Across from him, rising and red, CARL, sixty, a feed cap, three drinks in.

CARL:
Somebody's got to go and stand up for this country. That's all I'm saying. My grandson shipped out in March.

RAY:
I know he did, Carl. You've told the room twice.

CARL:
Then you know it's not a debate to me.

RAY:
Let me ask you one thing. That ground he's standing on over there. Who owns it?

CARL:
What kind of question is that?

RAY:
Somebody owns it. It's a rubber plantation. Somebody holds the lease, signs the checks. You can't tell me his name. The boy can't either.

CARL:
What's any of that got to do with my grandson?

RAY:
That man knows the boy's name to the letter. Your grandson is the cheapest line in his ledger.

A few heads turn. Not in delight. The way men look at a dog that's slipped its chain.

CARL:
So he's a sucker. We're all suckers, and you're the one man in the county smart enough to see it.

RAY:
I'm saying you're sending him to die for a thing you can't even name.

CARL:
He's not dying for a name. He's dying for the man next to him in the hole. You ever do that, Henderson? Stand next to somebody when it counted? No. You sat in a booth and you talked.

That lands. The room goes quiet, but it's Carl's quiet. A couple of men murmur, and it's with him.

The room is tilting toward Carl. Ray could let it lie. He doesn't.

RAY:
He's not dying for the man next to him either. He's dying because a stranger in an office needed a number, the boy put his hand up, and you call it honor so you don't have to call it what it is.

The line hangs there in the smoke. Nobody laughs. Nobody nods. It just dies.

Carl looks at him a long moment. What's in his face now isn't anger. It's closer to pity.

CARL:
You're the smartest man in this room, Henderson. And there's nothing underneath it. It never once cost you a dime to be right.

He puts money on the bar and stands.

CARL (CONT'D):
I'm going home to write my grandson.

He goes. Two or three men drink up and follow him out, and not one of them looks at Ray on the way past.

Nobody buys Ray a beer. The warmth has gone out of the room and left him alone at the cold end of an argument he won.

And UNDER THE TABLE, where not one of them can see it: Ray's right hand is a fist, knuckles gone white, the whole forearm trembling like a strung wire. A fight in a man with nowhere on earth to put it.

He notices it himself. Lays the hand flat on his thigh. Presses until it stops.

At the far end of the bar, half in shadow, a younger man has watched the whole thing. TOMMY HENDERSON, early twenties, big through the shoulders, a plain open face. Ray's brother, nothing like him.

He comes down and sets his bottle next to Ray's.

TOMMY:
You had him. Halfway in, you had him dead to rights.

RAY:
And?

TOMMY:
And you kept cutting till the whole room walked out with him.

(beat, not unkind)

You've got all the words, Ray. Always did. You just never had to use 'em on anything but winning a room.

He looks around at the half-empty bar.

TOMMY (CONT'D):
Look how that went.

RAY:
Drink your beer.

Tommy drinks his beer.

EXT. COUNTY ROAD - NIGHT

The two brothers walk home along a dark road, hands in pockets, breath showing. Far off, a dog. Tommy keeps half a step back, working up to something.

TOMMY:
Pop ever tell you about Anzio?

RAY:
Pop never told anybody anything. That was his whole act.

TOMMY:
He told me. Once. Said the worst part wasn't the shooting. It was the boat in. Sitting in the hull in the dark, listening to the water, knowing he had to get up and walk into it.

RAY:
And you've been carrying that around like it means something.

TOMMY:
You don't think it does?

Ray hears it now. The thing underneath the question. He stops in the road.

RAY:
I think a scared kid talked himself onto a boat, and came home, and didn't say four words a year for thirty. That's not courage, Tommy. That's a man it broke. Don't go making it a sermon.

Tommy holds his eyes a moment. Whatever he walked out here to say goes back down where it came from.

TOMMY:
Yeah. Maybe.

He steps around his brother and walks on up the road alone. Ray watches him go, and doesn't call after him.

INT. HENDERSON FARMHOUSE - KITCHEN - MORNING

Modest, clean, worn soft by use. RUTH HENDERSON, sixties, iron in a housedress, works at the stove without turning around. On the wall by the door, a framed photograph: a young man in uniform from an older war. EARL, the dead father. Tommy's build, Tommy's squint.

Ray comes down in yesterday's shirt, pours coffee.

RUTH:
Your brother's not in his bed.

RAY:
He's a grown man, Ma.

RUTH:
He's nineteen and a half and he was up before the light, and he put on his good shirt to go nowhere.

Ray sips. Doesn't bite.

RUTH (CONT'D):
(still not turning)
You know what your father said about the two of you? Just once. He said Tommy was the kind who'd go and do a thing. And you were the kind who'd stand there after and lay out all the reasons it shouldn't have been done.

RAY:
That's a hell of a thing to say about your kids.

RUTH:
He didn't mean it cruel. He meant it the way you'd say which way the weather was turning. Just a thing he saw.

She finally turns. Looks at her son a long moment, the way you'd watch a bird that's forgotten how to fly.

RUTH (CONT'D):
He's at the courthouse, Ray.

Ray goes very still. The coffee stops halfway.

EXT. CREEK - DAY (FLASHBACK)

A blaze of summer twenty years gone. A small boy, TOMMY, nine, drags himself up a muddy bank. Lip split, one eye swelling to a plum. Behind him, a BIGGER BOY, eleven, stands with his fists still up and his chest heaving. And somehow he's the more frightened of the two.

Tommy spits a red string into the dirt.

And grins.

He turns around, and walks back down the bank toward the bigger boy, to take some more.

EXT. FARMHOUSE PORCH - CONTINUOUS

Forty yards off, on the porch rail, a thin sharp boy, YOUNG RAY, fourteen, sits with a book open on one knee. He's been watching the whole thing.

He does not get up.

He works the arithmetic of it behind his eyes. The other boy's reach. The odds. And he arrives where he always arrives, at the sensible answer. He stays exactly where he is.

He turns a page.

INT. HENDERSON FARMHOUSE - KITCHEN - DAY (PRESENT)

A single folded sheet of Army paper, squared dead center on the oilcloth. Tommy sits before it, smoothing it flat under his thumb. Over and over.

Ray comes in fast, still in his coat. Stops. Reads the table.

TOMMY:
Went down to the courthouse.

A long beat. Then Ray pulls out the chair across from him, sits, and the machine starts up. Quiet, reasonable, relentless.

RAY:
Okay. Okay. Let's do this properly. You're a number to them, Tommy. A blank line some clerk hasn't filled in yet. You know what a body's worth to the kind of men who spend 'em?

He goes to the drawer, comes back with a folded newspaper, slaps it on the table. Columns of names, small print.

RAY (CONT'D):
By province. I read it so I'd know the number. You'll come home in a bag or you won't come home at all, and either way nothing changes. Not one thing. This war was a lie before you were born, and it'll still be a lie a long time after it's done with you.

Tommy turns his coffee cup a slow quarter-circle. Says nothing.

RAY (CONT'D):
Say something.

TOMMY:
You're not wrong.

RAY:
Then tell me which part of it I've got wrong.

TOMMY:
No part. You're never wrong, Ray. You're just never any use.

It lands somewhere. Ray covers it by talking faster.

RAY:
Don't do the thing where you turn it around on me. This is about a kid signing his whole life over to a lie.

TOMMY:
I know all that, Ray.

Tommy says it gently, and it stops Ray dead, because it's true, and because it's the end of it.

Tommy stands. Picks up the paper, folds it back into his shirt pocket, careful, like it's worth something.

TOMMY (CONT'D):
I'm going to go pack.

He goes. Ray sits alone at the table with the newspaper full of dead men. The one argument, in a life of winning them, that he has just lost.

INT. FARMHOUSE - HALLWAY - LATER

Through the open door of a small bedroom, Tommy folds shirts into a duffel with the same care he gave the paper. A YOUNG WOMAN sits on the edge of the bed, watching him. ANNIE, early twenties, steady-eyed, plain and pretty. She isn't crying. She looks like she's decided not to.

Ray passes in the hall and stops, just out of their sight.

ANNIE (O.S.):
You could've told me first. Before the courthouse.

TOMMY (O.S.):
You'd have talked me out of it.

ANNIE (O.S.):
No. I wouldn't have. That's the part you don't get about me.

A silence. The soft sound of cloth.

ANNIE (O.S.) (CONT'D):
I'd have just wanted to be there. That's all.

In the hall, Ray listens a moment. Then he moves off, before either of them can see his face.

INT. TOMMY'S ROOM - NIGHT

The house asleep. The packed duffel waits by the door. Tommy a long shape under the blanket.

Ray sits at the little desk with a sheet of paper and a pen. The man with all the words.

He writes a line. Reads it back. Tears the sheet off, crumples it, drops it.

Writes another. Crumples that one too.

A third joins the pile, a small white heap of everything he can't put down on paper.

He gives up, and sits a while with his hands flat on the bare desk.

Then he digs in his own coat pocket and comes out with a battered brass lighter. The one thing their father carried home from Anzio and never once talked about.

He turns it over in the lamplight. Crosses to the duffel. Works the lighter down under the folded shirts, where Tommy will find it a long way from here.

He draws the strings tight. Stands over his sleeping brother a moment. Doesn't wake him. Goes.

EXT. FARMHOUSE - DAWN

A bus idles at the end of the lane, headlights cutting the mist. Tommy, duffel on his shoulder, hugs his mother. Ruth holds him a beat too long, then lets go all at once, like dropping something hot.

Tommy comes to Ray. They don't hug. Ray puts out his hand; Tommy takes it.

TOMMY:
Look after her.

RAY:
You'll be back before she's used to you gone.

TOMMY:
Yeah.

(holds the handshake)

Hey. When I get back, you and me are gonna have it out about Pop. For real. I've got things to say about him. I just gotta go find the words for 'em first.

He grins, Ray's own joke turned back on him, and climbs onto the bus.

Ray stands in the lane and watches the taillights shrink and wink out at the bend. Long after the others have gone in, he's still there.

INT. HENDERSON FARMHOUSE - KITCHEN - DAY (WEEKS LATER)

Rain on the window. Ruth at the sink. Ray at the table grading nothing, a pencil idle in his hand.

A car in the lane. Not a neighbor's engine. Too smooth, too official.

Ruth's hands go still in the dishwater. She doesn't turn around.

RUTH:
Don't get it.

Ray gets it.

EXT. FARMHOUSE - PORCH - CONTINUOUS

A young ARMY OFFICER on the step, cap under his arm, an envelope held in both hands like it's heavy.

He says the words. The rain takes most of them. They land on Ray's face, and it doesn't crumple. It goes cold and still, and stays that way.

Behind him, in the doorway, Ruth has come out. She takes the envelope. She reads it once. She folds it once, along the crease the Army already made.

She goes back inside. A moment later, from somewhere down the hall, the small domestic sounds of a woman stripping the beds.

Ray stays on the porch in the rain. He does not sit down. He does not make a sound.

FADE TO BLACK.

[END OF ACT ONE]


INT. FIRST METHODIST CHURCH - DAY

A flag-draped coffin on a stand. A young soldier folds the flag with terrible precision. The pews are full of wet faces.

One face is dry. RAY, in his father's old black suit, sits bolt straight, something live held down hard under all that stillness.

After, the line files past to take his hand. He receives each one and gives nothing back. A machine for taking condolences.

MRS. ELLIS, seventies, Tommy's old Sunday-school teacher, holds his hand in both of hers and won't let go.

MRS. ELLIS:
The Lord never sends us more than we can carry, Raymond.

Ray looks at her. Holds the look until her certainty falters.

RAY:
He didn't send it. A man in an office did.

(quiet, even)

I can get you the name, if you want it.

He withdraws his hand. Mrs. Ellis is left with her mouth open, the way a hundred men have been left in the back booth. Except there's no jury here. Nobody laughs. It gives him nothing at all.

Across the aisle, ANNIE watches him. Black dress, dry eyes. She is not watching the coffin. She is watching Ray.

EXT. CHURCHYARD - DAY

Headstones, wet grass, a low sky. The crowd thins toward parked cars. Ray stands apart, by the wall, smoking a cigarette he doesn't want.

Annie comes and stands beside him. Not touching. They look out at the same nothing.

ANNIE:
You didn't cry.

RAY:
No.

ANNIE:
Everybody noticed.

RAY:
Everybody can go to hell.

A beat. She almost smiles. First true thing anyone's said to her all day.

ANNIE:
He wrote me. Twice a week. Terrible letters. Spelled "necessary" four different ways.

(beat)

The last one, he said you two were going to "have it out about your father" when he got back. Said you didn't understand him yet, but you would.

Ray's jaw tightens. He flicks the cigarette into the wet grass.

RAY:
He was wrong. I understood him fine. Better than he understood himself. That was always the problem. It's the reason he's in that box.

He says it flat, like a fact about somebody else. Annie watches him not feel it.

ANNIE:
You know what's funny? He used to say you were the brave one. The one with all the words.

She lets that sit. Then she walks back toward the cars, and Ray is left holding it.

INT. DELANEY'S TAVERN - NIGHT

Ray in the back booth, alone, a bottle this time, not a beer. SULLY slides in across from him, careful, like approaching a dog that's been hit.

SULLY:
Whole town's asking after you.

RAY:
The whole town can go to hell.

SULLY:
Yeah. You said. At the funeral, to Edith Ellis. It's all anybody's talking about.

Ray pours. Sully puts a soft hand flat on the table.

SULLY (CONT'D):
Ray. I'm no good at this, but hear me out. You didn't lose an argument. You lost your brother. And to you those are gonna feel like the same thing, 'cause that's how you're made, but they ain't, and if you don't get that straight it's gonna eat you alive.

RAY:
You want to know the name.

SULLY:
What?

RAY:
The recruiter. The one who sat my brother down and made it sound like a calling. Henley. Sergeant Henley. Office over in Mercer. I looked it up.

Sully's face changes. He sees, all at once, exactly where this is going.

SULLY:
Ray. No. Tommy signed that paper himself. You said so yourself, standing in your own kitchen.

RAY:
A man in an office, Sully. I said I could get the name.

He stands, leaves money on the table, walks out. Sully stares at the empty booth.

INT. RAY'S HOUSE - NIGHT

One bulb over the kitchen table. Ray sits before an open wooden box. He lifts out a thing wrapped in oilcloth, peels it back: EARL's service revolver, blue-black, heavy. A small box of shells beside it.

He breaks the cylinder. Feeds it, one round at a time. His face is nothing. His hands are sure.

On the wall, the photograph of Earl watches him do it.

He snaps the cylinder shut. Stands. Slides the gun into his coat. It sits there, heavy, over his heart. Takes his keys off the hook.

EXT. MERCER - RECRUITING STATION - DAY

A storefront on a tired street, two towns over. A faded poster in the window: a square-jawed soldier pointing out at the sidewalk. A small American flag.

Ray's car eases to the curb. He sits a moment, both hands on the wheel.

Then he crosses to the glass door. And stops.

In the dark glass, his own reflection: a man, squared up, a weight under his coat. He looks at it a long beat.

INT. RECRUITING STATION - CONTINUOUS

A cramped office, two desks. At one sits the RECRUITER, SGT. HENLEY, late twenties, tired, a head cold, buried in triplicate forms. Younger than Ray pictured. Smaller.

Ray steps in. Stops just inside the door. His hand goes into his coat, finds the grip, closes around it.

INSERT - RAY'S HAND inside the coat: on the revolver. Dead steady. Not a tremor.

FLASH - the white fist shaking under the tavern table.

BACK TO the hand. Still as a stone in a wall.

Ray doesn't move. The hand on the gun doesn't shake. For the first time he can remember, it just doesn't shake.

Henley looks up, sniffles.

HENLEY:
Help you?

Ray looks at him. This tired boy, this stranger in an office. He takes the revolver out of his coat.

And sets it down on the desk. Between them.

Henley goes very still, eyes on the gun.

RAY:
That was my father's.

HENLEY:
(carefully)
All right.

RAY:
You signed up a kid named Henderson. Three weeks back. Tommy. Big. Couldn't spell "necessary."

Henley's eyes flick to the gun, to Ray, to the gun.

HENLEY:
I sign up a lot of kids, mister.

RAY:
He's dead.

A silence. Henley swallows.

HENLEY:
I'm sorry.

Ray looks at the gun on the desk a long moment. Then he reaches past it and picks up the pen lying in the tray.

RAY:
Give me the form.

HENLEY:
The what?

RAY:
The same one you gave him. I'm taking his slot.

Henley doesn't move. Ray pulls the chair out, sits, sets the pen to the page.

HENLEY:
Mister, I gotta be straight with you. You come in here with a sidearm and your brother in a box. That's not somebody I'm supposed to sign up. That's somebody I'm supposed to call the sheriff on.

RAY:
Henley.

(looks up, calm, terrible)

I have spent thirty-eight years being the smartest coward in three counties. So give me the form. Or I will stand here and explain to you, in language you are not going to enjoy, exactly what kind of man talks other men's brothers into a lie and calls it a career. Trust me. You'd rather I just signed.

A long beat. Henley slides the paperwork across the desk.

Ray signs. Neat, even, schoolteacher's script. He stands, leaves the loaded revolver lying on the desk, and walks out into the daylight.

Henley sits alone, staring at the gun.

EXT. JUNE'S HOUSE - DUSK

A neat house, a neat yard. JUNE, thirties, pretty, sensible, stands behind the screen door. She does not open it.

JUNE:
Carol Mercer told me. She heard it from Sully's wife.

RAY:
June.

JUNE:
You enlisted. You. Ray Henderson.

(a laugh with no humor in it)

I fell for the one man in this whole county who was too smart to go. Who could stand there and prove to you in thirty seconds flat why nobody should. That was the whole of who you were to me.

RAY:
I know.

JUNE:
Then who is this?

Ray opens his mouth. The man of words. And for the first time in his life, nothing comes. Not one word. He stands there empty, in front of the woman who loved the words.

June watches the silence go on too long. And something in her face closes for good.

JUNE (CONT'D):
Yeah. That's what I thought.

She shuts the inner door. Ray stands on the step in the dusk, and after a while he goes.

EXT. ANNIE'S PORCH - NIGHT

Ray on the step. Annie opens the door. Not surprised. Still in black. The dead man stands between them, as he will from now on.

ANNIE:
You shouldn't be here.

RAY:
I know.

ANNIE:
People will say things.

RAY:
Let them.

She studies him. Then she looks down at his hands, hanging at his sides. Steady. She has spent a long time around men whose hands were steady.

ANNIE:
I'm not going to be a way for you to keep him.

RAY:
That's not why I came.

ANNIE:
And you're not going to be a way for me to keep him either. If you come through that door, it's not for him. It's for us. It's about the most selfish thing either of us will ever do, so it had better be true.

Ray meets her eyes.

RAY:
He was wrong about the words, Annie. It's about the only true thing I've got left.

A long moment. Then she steps back from the door.

EXT. FORT BENNING - TRAINING GROUND - DAY

A different world. RAY, head shaved, in fatigues, hauls himself under wire through red mud while a DRILL SERGEANT screams an inch from his ear. Around him, boys ten years younger move faster, easier.

Here, his quick mouth is worth exactly nothing. He's slow. He's last. He gets up out of the mud and does it again. And again. He keeps his mouth shut and learns, by inches, how to move.

EXT. TRAIN STATION - DAY

Steam, duffels, a hundred goodbyes. Ray in dress uniform, a stranger's haircut.

Ruth sees him off. Dry-eyed. She has stood on this platform before, for his father, for his brother. She doesn't hold him too long this time. She's learned not to.

RUTH:
Your father came back from his war and didn't say four words a year.

RAY:
You told me. You meant it as a warning.

RUTH:
I'm not warning you now.

(she touches his face, once)

I'm telling you that was when I loved him best. All that quiet. So come back quiet if you have to, Raymond. I don't care how. Just come back.

The train takes him. Ruth stands on the empty platform a long time.

EXT. TROOP SHIP - DECK - NIGHT

Black water, no horizon. Ray stands at the rail among rows of silent young men, the engines thrumming up through the deck. Somewhere below, a kid is being sick. Somewhere, his father once sat in a hull like this in the dark, listening to the water, knowing he'd have to stand up into it.

Ray listens to the water.

FADE TO BLACK.

[END OF ACT TWO]


EXT. FIREBASE LANDING ZONE - DAY

Heat shimmer, red dust, the whine of a departing chopper. Ray, now just one more grunt among grunts, hauls his pack toward a sandbagged perimeter. The men here have the flat eyes of men who've been somewhere.

A SERGEANT named DECKER, thirties, lean and unbothered, looks Ray over. Clocks the older face, the schoolteacher's stillness.

DECKER:
You're the replacement. Henderson.

RAY:
Yes.

DECKER:
You're old.

RAY:
I came late to it.

DECKER:
(almost smiles)
Stick close to the kid. Echols. He's green as you are, but he's got a week on you, so out here that makes him your daddy. Don't try and be a hero. Don't be a coward either. Do the boring thing in between and you'll go home. Most of you will.

EXT. PERIMETER - FOXHOLE - NIGHT

Ray and ECHOLS, eighteen, jittery, share a hole and a cigarette. The dark is loud with insects and far-off thunder that isn't thunder.

ECHOLS:
You scared?

RAY:
Yes.

ECHOLS:
You don't look it. You look like a man waiting on a bus.

RAY:
My hands. My whole life, anytime it came to something that mattered, they'd go cold. Quit on me.

He turns one hand over in the dark, like it belongs to somebody else.

RAY (CONT'D):
So I learned to talk instead. Never once had to find out what was underneath.

ECHOLS:
And now you're gonna find out.

RAY:
Now I'm gonna find out.

Echols laughs, nervous, then goes quiet. The thunder-that-isn't gets closer.

EXT. PERIMETER - FOXHOLE - PRE-DAWN

The world comes apart.

A mortar walks the wire. CRUMP. CRUMP. Closer. Geysers of red dirt. Tracer light. Men screaming names. A SOLDIER three feet from Ray is there and then simply is not, a wrongness and a ringing.

Ray presses himself into the bottom of the hole. And he waits for it. For the cold hands, the freeze, the boy on the porch who stays exactly where he is and turns a page.

CLOSE ON RAY'S FACE. Waiting to fail. Certain of it.

It doesn't come.

The roar drops away to almost nothing, the inside of a held breath. And in the silence, calm as a man reaching for a cup he's reached for a thousand times, something under his ribs says:

RAY (V.O.):
(barely a breath)
There you are.

And Ray is already moving.

EXT. PERIMETER - OPEN GROUND - CONTINUOUS

Whatever this is, it's past brave. There's no fear left in it at all. Echols is out in the open, pinned, screaming, a leg wrong beneath him. Every instinct Ray has ever owned says stay down, let the open ground belong to someone else.

He goes.

Low and fast and sure, across ground that's jumping with fire, his rifle up and working with a competence his hands have apparently always had. He reaches Echols, grabs him by the harness, and drags him backward toward the berm, one-handed, still firing.

A round takes Ray high in the shoulder and turns him half around. He doesn't stop. He gets Echols behind the sandbags, puts his body between the kid and the open ground, and holds the corner. Fires, reloads, fires, through nine minutes he will never afterward be able to account for.

Then, as suddenly as it came, the storm moves off. Gray light. Smoke drifting sideways. The wire holds.

EXT. PERIMETER - BERM - DAWN

Quiet. Echols, alive, white-faced, gripping fistfuls of Ray's bloody sleeve, sobbing, saying thank you, thank you, thank you, over and over.

Ray doesn't hear a word of it. He sits against the sandbags, his shoulder a red ruin, and his face is strange. It isn't triumph and it isn't shock. He has the look of a man who's just met himself and found, against all his own evidence, that he was there the whole time.

Decker drops down beside them, looks at Ray's shoulder, looks at his face.

DECKER:
I told you not to be a hero.

RAY:
(distant, almost gentle)
I wasn't. I was just finally here.

Decker doesn't understand it. He calls for a medic.

EXT. AIR EVAC - DAY

Ray on a stretcher, shoulder packed, watching the firebase fall away beneath the chopper. The red ground, the wire, the place where he found the one thing his whole life of words had been standing in for.

INT. DELANEY'S TAVERN - NIGHT (MONTHS LATER)

The same blue smoke. The same fiddle. The same back booth.

A NEW KID has the booth now. Twenties, sharp, a beer he's not drinking, holding forth to whoever will listen.

NEW KID:
It's a rich man's war and a poor man's funeral. Not one of you can even name the company that owns the rubber over there. Not one.

A couple of men nod. A couple keep their eyes on their beer. Nobody's enjoying it much.

At the bar, not the booth, a man orders a beer he means to drink on his feet. RAY. Older. A stiffness in one shoulder. He isn't here to stay.

An old regular leans to the kid and says something low. The kid's eyes come up, find Ray, and light.

NEW KID (CONT'D):
You're Henderson. You were over there. Go on, then. Tell me I'm wrong.

Ray drinks his beer. Doesn't turn around.

NEW KID (CONT'D):
Tell me it's not a lie.

RAY:
It's a lie.

That stops the kid. It's not the fight he came for.

NEW KID:
So you're on my side.

RAY:
Every word of it. You're right.

Ray sets the empty bottle on the bar. Puts on his hat.

RAY (CONT'D):
Doesn't change a thing. Your number comes up, you go anyway.

NEW KID:
Then what was the point of any of it?

Ray's already gone, out the door into the daylight. The kid sits there holding the rest of a speech nobody wants to hear.

EXT. ANNIE'S HOUSE - DAY

A small house at the edge of town. Wash on a line. ANNIE on the porch step, shelling peas into a bowl, the way Ruth does.

Ray comes up the walk. Uniform gone. One arm that doesn't swing quite right. She watches him come and doesn't get up.

ANNIE:
The whole town's still talking.

RAY:
Let them.

He stops at the bottom of the steps. They look at each other. He doesn't have a speech. The words are gone, for good, and he lets her see the whole of him standing there with nothing out in front of it.

ANNIE:
He used to say you were the brave one. The one with all the words.

RAY:
He was wrong about the words.

(beat)

Right about the rest of it, though.

Annie looks at him a long moment. The man who came home quiet, the way his father did. The way her own man never got the chance to.

She moves the bowl of peas over, to make room on the step.

Ray climbs the stairs and sits beside her. After a moment he reaches into the bowl, takes a pod, and shells it into his palm. His hands don't shake at all.

Neither of them says anything. They don't need to.

The wash moves on the line. Somewhere a dog. The afternoon goes gold and long.

FADE OUT.

THE END
