# अमल — Backstory & the Submerged (the iceberg)

*Ground truth lives in the bible and the plot doc. Almost none of this is ever spoken. It is what the actors and
the writers know and must not explain, fed to the work as weight and checked by the backstory-leak
gate. If a character says it out loud, it has leaked.*

---

## The recurring image: the train (never explained)

A woman on a crowded train in the heat, a bundle in her arms, a child who looks asleep. One by one the
passengers near her go quiet, because the child has not moved in a long time and the flies have begun
to settle on the cloth. Someone says something to the guard, and then the police are coming down the
aisle.

There is no caption and no date, her face is never clear, and the scene could be now or twenty years
ago or a dream. It returns more than once, and it is never placed in time.

**Under it (cast and writers only, never on screen):** the woman is Sugna; the child is her infant
son, dead of fever, his body packed with opium by the network her family was bonded to; the train is
the Ratlam line; the young officer at the edge of the bust, who let the network's part of it disappear
and took his first cut, is Ratan. That is the whole of it, and it is said nowhere.

How it works on screen: the train comes in unmoored fragments, the face withheld, the time blurred.
The only bridge to the present is a rhyme in the body, a way of holding a bundle, a cloth, the sound
she makes, the flies, that recurs around Sugna now. The viewer's spine joins them; the script never
does. It may stay unexplained to the end. The blur is the point: in the belt this is not one event in
the past, it is the weather.

---

## Ratan — the iceberg

Panwar Rajputs, small landholders out past Garoth, opium growers since the Company's day, a patta held
three generations and the family's whole standing. It was cancelled in his father's time over a short
weight that Rana's father engineered to take their land and their water. His father drank and broke and
died small. Ratan joined the very department that had weighed them out, so that he would stand on the
side of the scale and never be weighed again. The train was his first cut and the thirty years since
were the rest of them. A wife, Kanta, he stopped talking to somewhere along the way. He goes through the
kuldevi rites at the Harsiddhi than — the Paramara goddess of Ujjain — without believing a word, and eats
his dal-bafla alone. The ancestral signet, the Parmar Jhujhar's own ring, has been pawned off his finger
for years; he pays the interest and never redeems it.

*Surfaces:* his tiredness, a stillness when flies settle, a flinch at a train horn. *Stays under:* all
of it.

---

## Sugna — the iceberg

Married young into a bonded family, then the fever took her son and the network took the rest. After
the train she did her time and came out with nothing and a record, and was given to Bherulal, who would
take a ruined woman because his own debts were already deep. Another opium house, another patta, another
child, Leela. She goes to Hussain Tekri at Jaora when the dead get heavy, and sits with the other women
under the neem, waiting on a healing that does not come.

*Surfaces:* her silence, the way she keeps her hands busy. *Stays under:* the son, the train, that she
is the woman in it.

---

## The others — the iceberg

- **Bherulal:** the opium empire of the belt, Sugna's husband. He took a ruined woman with a record in a
  leaner year and has built high since — three daughters, then at last a son. Now he is maneuvering an
  additional patta in the boy's name; a new licence needs clean standing, so the daughters are being
  married off to fund and ally the push — Leela to old Dhanraj. When she died he read it as suicide and
  buried it as a fall: a suicide in the house would sink the licence application, a fall does not. He
  never learns his wife's hand was in it. *(अफ़ीम का पट्टा लड़के से बढ़कर है — he trades his daughters for
  the son's patta; the spine-sentence made a man.)*
- **Rana:** his family rose on the ruin of families like Ratan's, the muscle first come up out of the
  Chambal ravines in the dacoit years and later laundered into votes. Three generations turned the same
  opium into land, then into a licence to allot licences. He is an MLA now, and untouchable.
- **Daulatram:** he runs the route, trucks now and trains before and the Chambal roads always; the train
  mule twenty years ago was his house's work, though no paper will ever say so.
- **Pratap:** Daulatram's nephew, fifty-five, two pattas, and a first wife who died young under a snakebite
  certificate. Sugna knows what kind of man the route makes.
- **Deva:** twenty-four, a constable from Itarsi — no opium country, no patta, no knowledge of the trade
  (the outsider's eye the audience rides). He moved the family to the border town for his sister Manju's
  match across into Rajasthan, to keep their mother near her. He is the fork the show keeps in view, and he
  can become Ratan or break the wheel in one house.
- **Bhanwar Singh:** the government doctor, a Rajput of Ratan's own age and kind, who has signed clean what
  he knew was dirty for as long as Ratan has. Ratan's cold mirror — the finished man who never lifts the
  sword. When he stamps Leela a fall, same-day, it is the *speed* — his own signature on a hundred such —
  that tells Ratan the apparatus is lying.
- **Govind:** Ratan's oldest friend, who knew the boy who walked into the Chambal ravines and brought the
  dacoits out. The conscience; the one who still sees the finer material under the surrender. He keeps the
  memory of who Ratan was, and at the third friend's pyre he says the thing — *who will remember us* — that
  stirs the corpse.

## Malwa, woven in (lived, never a brochure)

- **The deep root:** this soil grew opium for the Company, then the Raj, then the Republic, the Malwa
  crop that went out through Bombay to China two centuries ago. The same flower and the same families
  under three masters; the patta is a two-hundred-year leash, and everyone here was born already holding
  it.
- **The land:** the black cotton soil that cracks open in the loo; the Chambal ravines at the edge of
  the district, dacoit country once and a smuggling road still; Gandhi Sagar's still water; the Shivna
  at Mandsaur and the eight-faced Pashupatinath above it.
- **The faith:** Pashupatinath for the show of it, and Hussain Tekri at Jaora for the desperate, where
  the afflicted come for haaziri and the women wait under the trees.
- **The line:** Ratlam Junction, where everything in the belt has always left from: the gum, the girls
  who run, the bundles that do not move.
- **The custom:** amal, the opium-water poured for a guest, a wedding, a death, courtesy and poison in
  the same cup; the weapon hiding in plain sight in the manners of the place.
- **The grain of it:** Malwi in the mouths (काँई, कोनी, थारो, म्हारो); poha and jalebi at the stall by
  the mandi where the garlic and the methi are auctioned by the sackful; dal-bafla; the Holkar-era
  weights still used out of habit.

---

## The leak rule

None of the above is exposition. It is fed to each scene as what the person carries, not what they say.
The train is never captioned, never dated, never solved aloud. If the connection between the train and
Sugna ever becomes a line of dialogue, we have lost it.
