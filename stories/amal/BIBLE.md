# अमल (AMAL) — World Bible

*A gritty realist crime drama set in the opium belt of Malwa, Madhya Pradesh, in the register (not
the plot) of* Paatal Lok, Delhi Crime, *and* Aranyak. *अमल means "the way a thing is done" — practice, habit, conduct (Arabic ʿamal). The drug itself is
**अफ़ीम** (addict = अफ़ीमची, husk = डोडा); the title is about the **habit, the way it is all done** — the
rot as routine — not the substance. (अमल for the opium *substance* is a Rajasthani usage, not Malwi.)*

---

## Part 0 — The register (read first, every session)

**The bar:** a specific Indian *place* that is almost a character; a crime that is a keyhole into a
systemic rot; a flawed, weary, compromised investigator who is not a hero; realism over plot; the
powerful grinding the powerless; a thematic underlayer holding it all.

**What this is NOT.** Not a thriller, not a drug-war shoot-'em-up, not poverty-porn. Nobody here is a
cartoon: not the mafia man, not the neta, not the addict. The belt is beautiful and the belt is
rotten, both at once, and the poppy is medicine and poison on the same stem. If a scene plays the
place for misery or for thrill, it's wrong. Play it for *truth*.

**The craft laws bind here too.** Every line obeys The One Law (`docs/06-THE-ONE-LAW.md`): nothing
arranged for effect, no end-loaded detail, no two-word punch. **English sluglines and action,
Devanagari dialogue.** And it must not be a monorail. Run the structural gates (weave, heart,
doorways) so a human B-story breathes under the case, per Part VII.

## Part I — The Place

The **Malwa opium belt** covers Mandsaur, Neemuch, and Ratlam in western Madhya Pradesh, on the
black-soil plateau above the Chambal. Real ground: this is the only region in India (with parts of
Rajasthan and UP) **licensed to grow opium**, and it raises the bulk of the country's legal crop.
Wheat and soybean fill most of the fields; in winter, the licensed plots fill with poppy.

Our town, **अमरगढ़ (Amargarh)**, is fictional, a composite tehsil town in the belt: a mandi, a bus
stand, a CBN weighment shed, a Hanuman temple, a liquor contractor's godown, three netas' bungalows,
and a thousand small farmers in the villages around it. The texture is the show's blood: the cold
dawns of lancing season, the dust, the dry wells, the white-and-purple poppy fields, the smell of the
latex, the men who sit at dusk passing **amal** (opium-water) hand to hand at a wedding or a death.
Here the drug is woven into the courtesy and the grief of the place at once, its beauty inseparable
from its rot.

---

## Part II — The Patta (the engine)

The licence, the **पट्टा (patta)**, is the heart of everything here, the way the insurance policy is
to Ancapistan. The real mechanics, lightly fictionalised:

- The **Central Bureau of Narcotics** (CBN, headquartered in Gwalior) licenses each grower through
  the **District Opium Officer**, plot by plot, year by year. The gum goes to the government factory
  at Neemuch.
- The patta comes with a **quota**. A licensed plot yields, say, 8 to 12 kg of opium latex, and the
  grower must deliver a fixed minimum to the CBN measurement officer or **lose the licence.** Losing
  it is civil death of a Malwa kind. The patta is inherited over generations, it is the family's
  standing, and it makes the daughters' marriages. The belt's own saying, **"अफ़ीम का पट्टा लड़के से
  बढ़कर है"**: *the opium licence is worth more than a son.* That sentence is the show's spine.
- So the patta is both a blessing and a leash. The state grants it and the state can take it, and a
  man will do almost anything, to anyone, to keep it.

---

## Part III — The Shadow (the diversion economy)

Every licensed gram is worth far more sold sideways than handed to the government, so the legal crop
and the illegal crop come off the same flower:

- A grower **skims**: he delivers the required gum and quietly keeps the surplus. The measurement
  officer who weighs it is therefore the most quietly powerful man in the belt, and the most
  corruptible.
- **दोड़ा चूरा (doda chura)**, the poppy husk, is the bulk of what gets smuggled out, cheap and
  addictive; opium and morphine move in smaller, dearer amounts.
- The **afeem mafia** buys the skim and runs the husk on the highways, and it feeds a labour
  underclass hooked on doda: the men who scrape the fields by dawn and lose their wages to the same
  crop by dusk.
- **Lancing season (लुआई)**, when the pods are scored at evening and the gum scraped at first light,
  is the tense stretch when theft and diversion peak and every field is watched. If a body turns up,
  it turns up in these weeks.

---

## Part IV — Power, and who it crushes

Top to bottom: the **state** (CBN, the factory, the law), and the **netas** who control *who gets a
patta*. Patronage is power, and a licence is a vote and a debt. Below them sit the **big-farmer
families** with many pattas, and the **measurement officers and police** who take their cut of the
looking-away. Below those, the **small farmers**, one bad measurement from ruin. At the bottom, the
people the patta is bought and paid with: the **women** whose marriages it arranges and who carry its
cost, and the **doda-addicted labourers** who do the work and eat the rot. Caste runs through all of
it: who holds land and pattas, and who only ever scrapes them. The system is the antagonist, and
individual villains are only its instruments.

---

## Part V — The investigator, and the cast

**Inspector Ratan Singh Panwar** (रतन सिंह पँवार), CBN, about 52, and not an upright hero but a
Paatal-Lok man. He has policed and measured this belt for thirty years, taken his cut, looked away,
survived. The wound that makes it personal: his *own* family's patta was cancelled a generation ago,
and losing the thing "bigger than a son" broke his father and his childhood. He has spent his life on
the other side of the scale ever since. This case drags him back to that wound.

The world around him (archetypes, to be made into people, never cartoons):
- the **measurement officer**, the quiet king of the scale, everyone's creditor;
- the **neta**, genial and ruthless, who hands out pattas like blessings;
- the **mafia man**, a transporter and a businessman, not a monster;
- the **big-farmer patriarch**, the one who will, in the end, choose the patta over his child;
- the **woman** the patta was meant to marry off, the show's moral centre and not its victim-prop;
- the **doda addict**, the belt's truth-teller, who has nothing left to protect;
- a **junior officer** under Ratan, the B-story's other life (see Part VII).

---

## Part VI — The spine and the underlayer

**Spine:** *the patta is bigger than a son.* A world where a state-granted licence to grow poison
outranks a human child. You inherit the patta; it makes your standing and your children's marriages.
The season's case proves the saying true: by the end, someone has chosen the licence over their own
blood.

**Underlayer:** opium itself is medicine and poison at once, legal off one plot and illegal off the
next, the milk that numbs grief and the milk that kills. The **amal** custom (opium-water served at
weddings and funerals) is the image of it: the drug as courtesy *and* as curse, the same cup. The
state's leash, the family's pride, the labourer's chains, all one flower.

## Part VII — The story engine (so it is never a monorail)

Each case is a **keyhole**, a single death that opens the rot of the belt, complete in its own arc,
while a season-long line tightens underneath it (the spine, and Ratan's own buried patta-wound). And
it runs **two threads, woven** (the gate `metaphrand/weave.py` exists to fail a monorail):

- **A-story:** the case. Ratan works a death through the patta economy.
- **B-story:** a human life off the case that *refracts* it. Say the junior officer's family pressing
  a marriage the same way Gudiya's father did, or Ratan's own household and the patta-wound he won't
  speak of. The B-story carries the emotion the case can't, and rhymes with it without naming it
  (apply the logic in `heart.py`: bonds as ledgers, the feeling banked early and spent later).

Structure: the two doorways (`doorways.py`). The death that pulls Ratan in past refusing, and the
midpoint discovery that makes the ending unavoidable.
