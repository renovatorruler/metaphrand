# अमल — The Train (English version, for review)

*Full directed scene, English throughout. Indian characters (Indian-accented English), a neutral-US
narrator for the action. Performance notes in (parentheses). This is the script — nothing is rendered
until you approve it.*

---

**INT. RAILWAY COMPARTMENT — DAY**

A crowded train, the heat sitting heavy in the carriage. By the barred window, a WOMAN holds a baby
wrapped to the crown in a faded green cloth, one hand flat on its back. Across from her sit SURESH
(forties, tired), his wife KAMLA, mid-sentence, and his younger brother RAJESH.

KAMLA: (cheerful, chattering) I told you na, we should have caught the morning train. Now sit in this heat. And that pickle box — did you put it up top or down below? I can't even remember now.

SURESH: (tired) It's somewhere.

KAMLA: And phone jijaji the moment we get off, otherwise he'll sit with a long face the whole wedding.

KAMLA: (swatting at flies) Uff, where have all these flies come from. Sister — they're buzzing right over you. Have you kept something sweet there?

The woman only shakes her head. She says nothing.

KAMLA: (warm, prying) How many months is he? It's a boy, na?

KAMLA: (concerned) You've wrapped the poor thing so tight in this heat — won't he suffocate? Open it a little from the top.

WOMAN: (flat, guarded) He's sleeping. He's fine.

Suresh has stopped listening to his wife. He is watching the woman — the flat hand, the cloth drawn over the child's face. He stands, stretches, and angles for a look at the baby. She turns the bundle away.

SURESH: (low, to Rajesh) Rajesh. Come here a second. Look at that woman. The baby. It's so hot and she's covered even his face. And since the train started — did you see that baby move even once?

RAJESH: (dismissive) He's sleeping, bhaiya. Small babies sleep all day. And they catch cold quickly also. You, honestly.

---

**EXT. SMALL STATION — DAY**

The train stands at a low platform. Suresh comes back along it to the barred window with a paper packet of snacks and holds it up to Kamla inside.

SURESH: (over-friendly) Here, take this. Do you want water also?

KAMLA: (pleased) Arre, so many things you've brought. Keep some for Rajesh also. Did you get the salty one or the sweet one?

As Kamla takes the packet, Suresh leans in at the bars, and his eyes go past her — to the woman and the bundle.

SURESH: (casual, probing) Everything all right, sister? How's the little one keeping? ... Can I see his face, just once?

The woman draws the green cloth closed and turns the bundle in.

WOMAN: (guarded) He's fine. He's sleeping.

The guard's whistle goes. Suresh climbs back up.

---

**INT. RAILWAY COMPARTMENT — DAY**

Suresh sits, but he keeps looking down the carriage. He can't let it go.

SURESH: (quiet, certain) I'm telling you, something is wrong. I asked to see the baby's face — twice — she wouldn't show. My heart won't accept it.

RAJESH: (brushing it off) Bhaiya, leave it. A woman's travelling alone with a small child, and you're staring and questioning her. Someone will make a scene.

Kamla leans in.

KAMLA: What is it? What are you two going on about?

RAJESH: (low) Bhaiya thinks there's something wrong with that woman's baby.

KAMLA: (hushed, drawn in) You know — I also didn't feel right. I told her to open the cloth and she just wouldn't. And the child hasn't made one sound, not once. And those flies... ram ram.

RAJESH: (wavering) You two are imagining things. ... Though the flies are a bit much.

SURESH: (decided) At the next station, I'm telling the constable. Let them check, that's all.

---

**INT. RAILWAY COMPARTMENT — DAY (NEXT STATION)**

The woman has gone very still, listening to the shape of it. At the next station she watches the two men get off, leans to the window, and sees Suresh talking to a constable, pointing back at the carriage. She gets up, leaves her trunk, and moves quickly into the next bogie, the bundle held close.

KAMLA: (pointing, urgent) She went that way — into the next bogie.

---

**INT. RAILWAY COMPARTMENT — NEXT BOGIE — CONTINUOUS**

The constables reach her in the crowded aisle.

CONSTABLE: (stern, calm) Madam, stop a moment. We need to talk — two minutes.

WOMAN: (agitated, loud) What is it? What have I done? That man's been after me since morning. He saw a woman alone, so — that's why I left my seat and came here.

WOMAN: (accusing) He was looking at me with dirty eyes. His own wife is sitting right there — ask her.

SURESH: (insistent) Madam, just show the baby once. Just once.

WOMAN: (clutching the bundle) My baby is sleeping.

CONSTABLE: (reassuring) We won't wake him, madam. Just one look, and you go on your way.

Cornered, she folds the green cloth down from the top. A baby's face shows in the gap — eyes closed, still. It could be a child asleep. The constable lets his breath out.

CONSTABLE: (relieved, then annoyed) He's only sleeping. Why are you troubling her for nothing, bhai sahab? Come on.

SURESH: (desperate) Sahab, wait. Look properly. Open it fully.

CONSTABLE: (sharp, angry) That's enough now! The lady's travelling alone and you've been after her since morning. Move aside.

Suresh looks at the bundle, at the flies. Then he steps in and pulls the child out of her arms.

WOMAN: (screaming) No! Don't touch him! Let go! Give him back to me!

The green cloth comes away. The face that looked asleep does not change, and below it the small body has been opened at the belly and stitched and split, and the opium spills out onto the berth. For one second no one moves. Then she throws herself at him, and the constables catch her and hold her.

WOMAN: (wailing) My baby! Give him! He's my baby!

Her screaming fills the carriage, and goes on filling it, as we pull away.

**CUT TO BLACK.**
