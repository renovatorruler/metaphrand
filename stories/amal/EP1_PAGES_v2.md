## SCENE 1 — THE SAKA (the promise)
**EXT. BROKEN GROUND — NIGHT (1305)**

There is no establishing shot. We are close to him, and the only light is the fire. An old hand, bloody to
the wrist, closes on the worn grip of a sword. The blade is old and notched in a hundred places, and on one
finger sits a heavy gold signet, its mark almost rubbed smooth. The blade goes forward into a man we never
see, and a shape twists away into the dark. On one side there is fire, and men keep coming out of it. The old
hand turns the sword to meet them, and meets them again. For half a breath the view opens: an old man on dark
ground, the smoke behind him lit saffron, the blood to his elbow, more men closing in — and he does not go
down.

CUT TO BLACK.

## SCENE 3 — DEVA ARRIVES
**EXT./INT. AMARGARH CBN POST — DAY**

A low government building the colour of the dust around it, the paint peeling off the board outside, a CBN
jeep up on bricks with one wheel gone. A shared tempo pulls away in a cloud of grit and DEVA steps down with
an iron trunk on his shoulder, his uniform still creased from the shop. He stands and takes the place in, and
it is not what he pictured on the bus.

Inside, a MUNSHI sits behind a register under a slow fan, a glass of tea going cold by his elbow. He does not
look up when the door's shadow falls across his book.

DEVA: कांस्टेबल देवा। इतरसी से ट्रांसफर हुआ है। रतन सिंह पँवार साहब को रिपोर्ट करना है।

MUNSHI: साहब राउंड पे हैं।

DEVA: अच्छा। कब तक आ जाएँगे, जी?

MUNSHI: आ जाएँगे।

Deva waits for more. There is no more. He sets the trunk down, then thinks better of it and keeps standing.

DEVA: मैं... यहीं बैठ जाऊँ? या क्वार्टर —

MUNSHI: बैठ जा।

He sits on the bench. A KISAN comes in past him, a man who has been here before and knows the order of
things. He lays a folded note on the open register and slides it across without a word. The munshi takes it
under his palm, stamps a paper, and turns it around for the farmer to lift. It is done in seconds. The farmer
touches his forehead and goes.

Deva watches the note go under the blotter, and is not sure what he just saw.

DEVA: वो... किसलिए दिया उसने?

MUNSHI: *(turning a page)* चाय-पानी।

DEVA: किस बात की?

The munshi looks up at him for the first time, then goes back to his register.

MUNSHI: नया है। बैठ जा, बेटा। साहब आएँगे तो बता देंगे, किस बात की।

Deva sits back down. He watches the stamped paper go out the door in the farmer's hand, and says nothing.

## SCENE 4 — THE TEACHER
**INT. AMARGARH CBN POST — DAY**

RATAN comes in heavy and unhurried, the khaki gone shapeless on him, and lowers himself into the chair behind
the desk as if it costs him something. Deva is on his feet at once.

DEVA: साहब। कांस्टेबल देवा। आपके अंडर पोस्टिंग मिली है।

Ratan looks at him — the new uniform, the open face — for a second longer than nothing, and then it is put
away. He pulls the day's files toward him. A second KISAN is already at the desk, turning his cap in his
hands, a thin file in front of him.

RATAN: हाँ।

KISAN: साहब, ट्रैक्टर पलट गया था रात में, बड़े भाई पे। बस इतना लिख दीजिए कि हादसा था। घर में मातम है, साहब, और कुछ नहीं चाहिए।

Ratan opens the file. He does not read it. He signs.

RATAN: हो गया। जा।

The farmer sets a small fold of notes by the inkpad and is gone before it lands. Ratan moves it under the
blotter with two fingers, the way the munshi did, without looking at it. Deva has watched all of it.

DEVA: साहब... पोस्टमॉर्टम नहीं होगा?

RATAN: किसके लिए?

DEVA: आदमी मरा है, साहब। एक बार देख तो —

RATAN: देख के क्या मिलेगा?

DEVA: *(this stops him; he has no answer ready)* ...पता नहीं। पर नियम तो —

RATAN: रोज़ कोई न कोई मरता है यहाँ। सबका पोस्टमॉर्टम करूँ तो और कोई काम नहीं हो पाएगा।

He looks at Deva properly now, and does not look away as fast as he might.

RATAN: तू इतरसी से है ना।

DEVA: जी।

RATAN: वहाँ अफ़ीम नहीं होती।

DEVA: अफ़ीम? *(he half-laughs, thinking it a joke, then sees it is not)* मतलब... सच में?

RATAN: यहाँ की खेती है, सरकारी पट्टे पे। पूरा इलाक़ा इसी पे चलता है। *(a beat)* ज़्यादा देखेगा तो सबसे पहले अपनी नौकरी जाएगी।

Deva does not answer. Ratan has already pulled the next file toward him.

## SCENE 5 — THE FILE
**INT. AMARGARH CBN POST — DAY**

A SIPAHI comes in off the road, dust to the knees, and puts a thin file on the desk — barely a page in it.

SIPAHI: सुबह कोटेश्वर के पास, खेत के किनारे एक लड़की पड़ी मिली, साहब।

RATAN: किसकी?

SIPAHI: बड़े घर की। *(he lowers his voice a little, out of habit)* गिर गई होगी रात में, अँधेरे में। लाश ज़िले के अस्पताल में रखवा दी है। घरवाले कह रहे हैं जल्दी निपटा दो — जलाना है, शादी का घर है, बात फैलनी नहीं चाहिए।

RATAN: कौन सा बड़ा घर?

SIPAHI: भेरूलाल सेठ का।

The name means nothing to Ratan. He does not open the file. Deva, beside him, looks down at the closed cover.

DEVA: *(half to himself)* लड़की थी?

SIPAHI: *(a shrug, not unkind, just used to it)* थी। अब नहीं है।

RATAN: रख दे, ढेर पे।

Deva looks at the single thin page that is all there is of her. The sipahi sets the file on the day's pile and
goes back out to the road.

## SCENE 6 — SHE FELL
**INT. AMARGARH CBN POST — EVENING**

The light has gone amber. The pile has come down to the last file, the thin one. Ratan opens the cover, reads
to the end of the first line, and uncaps his pen.

DEVA: साहब। एक बार देख आएँ? पास ही तो है, कोटेश्वर। आधा घंटा।

RATAN: *(not looking up)* गिर गई।

DEVA: आपने देखा नहीं और —

RATAN: रिपोर्ट है ना सिपाही की।

DEVA: पर साहब, लड़की है, बड़े घर का मामला है, अगर कल कोई पूछे —

RATAN: कोई नहीं पूछेगा।

He says it flat, certain, the certainty of a man who knows exactly who asks questions in this district and who
does not. He writes the cause across the form, caps the pen, and closes the file without ever turning to the
name on the second page. The day's fold of notes goes into his breast pocket. He stands, tired, and reaches
for his cap.

Deva starts to say something and stops himself. He watches the day's notes go into Ratan's pocket, and this
time he stays quiet.

## SCENE 7 — TWO MORE YEARS
**INT. RATAN'S HOUSE — NIGHT**

A small kept house, everything in its place and nothing new in years. KANTA sets a plate in front of him and
straightens, a hand on her back. On the side table, an opened government envelope.

KANTA: पेंशन के काग़ज़ आ गए आज। दफ़्तर से फ़ोन भी था। दो साल और।

Ratan sits. He turns his hand over and looks at the pale band where the ring used to be, then at the plate.
He does not start eating.

KANTA: दो साल बाद घर पे रहेंगे। *(she says it lightly, testing it)* दिन भर क्या करेंगे, यही सोच रही हूँ। आप तो कभी घर पे रुके ही नहीं।

RATAN: तेरे हाथ का खाना खाऊँगा।

KANTA: *(a small breath that is almost a laugh)* मक्खन मत लगाइए। खाना खाइए, ठंडा हो रहा है।

He eats. She stands a moment longer than she needs to, looking at the top of his bent head from across the
small kitchen, across a distance that has grown over the years without either of them deciding it. Then she
takes the envelope off the side table and puts it away in a drawer, where the other unspoken things are kept.

## SCENE 8 — THE PYRE
**EXT. CREMATION GROUND — DAY**

The pyre has caught. Not many have come — a handful of old men, a son with his head freshly shaved, a dog
keeping its distance. A singer works through a Nirgun bhajan, the kind sung where there is no idol and no
comfort to be had from the words. RATAN stands at the edge with his hands behind his back. GOVIND, his own
age, stands next to him and watches the fire instead of the body.

GOVIND: चार जने थे हम लोग, याद है? तू, मैं, ये, और कैलाश। *(a beat)* अब दो बचे।

RATAN: हम्म।

GOVIND: देख कितने लोग आए। तीन महीने पहले वर्दी में था आदमी, हुकुम चलता था इसका। रिटायर क्या हुआ, सब भूल गए। आज इतने भी नहीं कि चिता को कंधा देने वाले पूरे पड़ें।

Ratan watches the smoke go up and does not answer. Govind is quiet a while, then says the thing more softly,
not to wound, just because it is in him.

GOVIND: अपने को कौन याद करेगा, रतन? दो-चार बरस, और अपनी भी यही राख है। न बेटा, न नाम — कोई पूछने वाला नहीं कि आदमी कैसा था।

Ratan looks down at his own hands. He does not say anything.

---

## SCENE 9 — WHAT YOU WERE
**EXT. CREMATION GROUND — LATER**

The others have gone. The pyre is down to embers. Govind crouches and feeds a last stick into it, more for
something to do than for the fire. Ratan stands as if he means to leave and does not.

GOVIND: याद है चंबल?

RATAN: छोड़ अब, गोविंद।

GOVIND: नहीं, सुन। बीहड़ में जब तू अकेला घुस गया था, उन डाकुओं के पीछे। पूरा थाना बाहर खड़ा था, पतलून गीली किए, और तू अंदर। *(he shakes his head, still amazed at it after all these years)* और शाम तक तू उन्हें बाँध के बाहर ले आया। अकेला। अख़बार में फोटो छपी थी तेरी।

RATAN: बहुत साल पहले की बात है। दूसरा आदमी था।

GOVIND: वही तो। *(he looks up at Ratan from his crouch)* तू अलग था, रतन। हम सब भरती हुए थे पेट के लिए। तेरे में कुछ और था। *(a beat)* आदमी कहाँ बदलता है, यार। बस दब जाता है, ऊपर से। मिट्टी पड़ जाती है। पर नीचे वही रहता है।

Ratan looks at his hands again. Govind lets it sit; he has said it before and he will say it again, and they
both know it. Ratan turns to go. At the road, where one way runs home and the other toward Koteshwar, he
stands a moment. Then he takes the road to Koteshwar.

## SCENE 10 — THE FIELD
**EXT. KOTESHWAR — FIELD'S EDGE — DAY**

The place has been walked over by everyone who came to look and to settle it — sandal-prints, a tractor's
tread, the dragged smear where they lifted her. A few villagers stop their work to watch the old inspector
who never comes to a scene now standing at one, in no hurry, looking at the dirt.

RATAN: किसने पाया?

A young CHARVAHA (herder), hanging back, answers because Ratan is looking at him.

CHARVAHA: मैंने, साहब। सुबह, भैंस लेके आया था। यहीं पड़ी थी।

RATAN: किस तरफ़ मुँह?

CHARVAHA: नीचे। मेड़ के नीचे।

Ratan crouches at the bund and puts his hand flat on top of it. It comes up to his knee. He looks at it a
long time. He looks at the dry, hard earth, and at the distance to the well across the field, and at the bund
again. He has not worked a scene in twenty-five years. He works this one.

RATAN: *(quietly, to no one)* घुटने भर का मेड़।

CHARVAHA: साहब?

RATAN: कुछ नहीं।

An older KISAN, sensing the wrong kind of attention coming to the village, speaks up, helpful, eager to close
it.

KISAN: रात में अँधेरे में गिर गई होगी, साहब। चक्कर आ गया होगा। लड़कियाँ कमज़ोर होती हैं।

Ratan does not look up from the ground. A girl does not trip over a thing she could step across and break her
skull on flat dirt. He stays crouched there, reading it, until the kisan runs out of things to say and the
field goes quiet around him.

## SCENE 11 — THE SIGNET
**EXT. SAHUKAR'S SHOP — DAY**

On the way back from the field the jeep stops outside a narrow pawnshop. Ratan gets down without a word and
goes in; the SAHUKAR rises to meet him and the two move to the back of the shop, out of earshot — a private
matter, an old one. DEVA waits by the jeep. On the step sits CHARAN, very old, one eye gone milky, shelling
peanuts into his lap, in no hurry. He has been looking at Deva's new uniform a while.

CHARAN: किसके साथ लगे हो, बेटा?

DEVA: रतन सिंह पँवार साहब के नीचे। थाने पे।

CHARAN: *(slowly)* पँवार। पता है अंदर किसके लिए गया है?

DEVA: नहीं।

CHARAN: एक अँगूठी। सोने की मुहर। बाप-दादा की। हर महीने ब्याज भरने आता है ताकि सेठ बेच न दे। किसी को बताता नहीं।

Deva looks at the shop doorway.

CHARAN: मैं उस घर का भाट हूँ। सात पुश्तों के नाम याद हैं मुझे। *(he turns his good eye toward Deva)* वो मुहर जिस उँगली की है, उस आदमी का सिर कट गया था लड़ाई में — और तलवार फिर भी चलती रही। सात सौ बरस पहले, इसी ज़मीन पे। सब गिर गए उस दिन। वो गिर के भी नहीं गिरा। *(a beat)* उसी का खून है तेरा साहब। और आज उसी अँगूठी के लिए सेठ के आगे गिड़गिड़ा रहा है, अंदर।

He goes back to his peanuts. After a while Ratan comes out, his face shut, his ring finger bare. He gets into
the jeep without a word. Deva looks at the bare finger, and at him, and says nothing.

## SCENE 12 — THE PEN, UNCAPPED
**INT. AMARGARH CBN POST — DAY**

Ratan pulls the thin file back off the closed pile and opens it on the desk. The MUNSHI watches over his
register, not understanding, then understanding and not liking it.

MUNSHI: साहब, वो तो कल बंद हो गई थी। आपने ही साइन किया था।

RATAN: खोल रहा हूँ फिर से।

MUNSHI: *(carefully)* पोस्टमॉर्टम के लिए? साहब, बड़े घर का मामला है। भेरूलाल सेठ का। उन्होंने ख़ुद कहलवाया था जल्दी निपटाने को।

RATAN: तो?

MUNSHI: तो... कुछ नहीं, साहब। बस... बात ऊपर तक जाएगी।

Ratan takes a fresh requisition form, writes the order across it in his own hand, and signs it. He blots it
and turns it to face the munshi.

RATAN: अस्पताल फ़ोन कर। लाश किसी को नहीं छोड़नी — न घरवालों को, किसी को नहीं। पोस्टमॉर्टम होगा, यहीं ज़िले में, डॉक्टर भँवर से। आज ही।

The munshi looks at the form, and at the inspector who has signed papers in this room for fifteen years and
never once asked for a postmortem on anything. He picks it up as if it might be hot. Across the room, DEVA,
at the wall, watches the man amend his own signature, and something in his face that had been going out comes
back a little.

---

## SCENE 13 — A CLOSED FILE
**INT. AMARGARH CBN POST — MISHRA'S ROOM — DAY**

MISHRA sits behind a clean desk, a neat man, a pressed uniform, a glass of water and no files. He waves Ratan
to a chair with real warmth, the warmth of a man who has never had to raise his voice to get his way.

MISHRA: अरे बैठ, रतन। बैठ। चाय बोलूँ? *(he doesn't wait)* सुन, ये कोटेश्वर वाली फ़ाइल। पोस्टमॉर्टम भिजवा दी तूने?

RATAN: भिजवा दी।

MISHRA: *(a small, fond sigh, leaning back)* पच्चीस साल की नौकरी है तेरी, यार। दो साल बचे हैं। और तू एक गिरी हुई लड़की पे — *(he spreads his hands)* किसलिए? बता तो सही, हुआ क्या है।

RATAN: मेड़ घुटने भर का था।

MISHRA: तो? लोग घुटने भर से भी गिर के मर जाते हैं, फिसल के सिर लग जाए तो। *(he leans forward now, still warm, the warmth doing the work a threat would do)* देख। फ़ाइल बंद थी। बंद रहने दे। आराम से दो साल काट, पेंशन ले, घर बैठ। इस झंझट में क्या रखा है — एक लड़की, बड़ा घर, ऊपर तक रसूख़। जाने दे, भाई। मेरे लिए नहीं, अपने लिए।

RATAN: हो गया, मिश्रा। चली गई फ़ाइल।

For a moment the warmth is not quite there in Mishra's face. Then it comes back, smooth.

MISHRA: चली गई। ठीक है। *(he smiles)* चाय पी के जा।

Ratan stands and goes without the chai. Mishra watches the door a moment, then picks up the phone.

## SCENE 14 — THE GOD WHO DRINKS
**INT. KAAL BHAIRAV SHRINE — DAY**

A black stone face under a heap of marigold and silver foil, fierce, smeared with vermilion. The PUJARI takes
a bottle of country liquor, tips it into the shallow silver dish set below the god's mouth, and the level in
the dish drops as they watch. DEVA, a step behind Ratan, stares at it.

DEVA: *(low, to Ratan)* साहब... वो... कम हो रहा है।

PUJARI: काल भैरव हैं, बेटा। अवंती के कोतवाल। *(he refills the dish without ceremony, as a man feeds an
animal he respects)* इन पे फूल-पत्ती नहीं चढ़ती ज़्यादा। न मिठाई, न सिफ़ारिश। बस यही — दारू। और जो झूठ बोले इनके सामने, उसका हिसाब ये ख़ुद लेते हैं। किसी थाने की ज़रूरत नहीं।

Deva looks at the god, half-spooked. Ratan looks at it longer — the one kotwal in the whole district who
takes only this, and no man's money, and keeps his own accounts. Then he looks away, and puts a coin in the
plate, and does not meet the stone eyes again.

## SCENE 15 — STANDING, NOT AGE
**EXT. KOTESHWAR VILLAGE — DAY**

Ratan and Deva go lane to lane, and the lanes close as they come. A man remembers a buffalo to see to. A
woman pulls a child inside. The trade has taught this village to have nothing to say to a uniform that is
actually asking. At last an old man on a cot outside a shop is the one who talks — too idle to be afraid, and
he likes an audience.

BUDDHA: पुलिस? लड़की वाली बात है ना — भेरूलाल की?

RATAN: शादी की कोई बात थी उसकी?

BUDDHA: बात क्या, पक्की हो चुकी थी। धनराज सेठ से।

DEVA: धनराज सेठ के बेटे से?

BUDDHA: *(a dry laugh)* बेटे से नहीं, बेटा। ख़ुद धनराज से।

DEVA: *(still not getting it)* वो कितने साल के हैं?

BUDDHA: होंगे साठ-पैंसठ।

DEVA: और लड़की सोलह की।

BUDDHA: अरे यहाँ उमर कौन देखता है — हैसियत देखते हैं। *(leans in, relishing it)* भेरूलाल की तौल दो साल से पूरी नहीं पड़ रही, पट्टा डाँवाडोल। तो बेटी चढ़ा दी बूढ़े सेठ को — कर्ज़ा भी उतरा, रिश्ता भी ऊँचा। *(spits to the side)* बड़े घर। इनके यहाँ बेटी भी माल है, साहब।

Ratan has gone still — the particular stillness of a man into whom one piece has just slid against another. He
turns to go.

BUDDHA: और सुनो — जलाने भी नहीं दे रहे उसे अभी। लाश अस्पताल में पड़ी है। *(he eyes the uniform)* तुम्हारे ही महकमे ने रोक रखी है।

Deva looks at Ratan. Ratan says nothing — it is his own order holding the girl in the morgue.

---

## SCENE 16 — MANJU
**INT. DEVA'S HOUSE — NIGHT**

A small, warm, plain room, lit by one tube-light. AMMA presses a second roti onto Deva's plate before the
first is gone. MANJU sits to the side, her eyes down, a girl who is being discussed.

AMMA: खा, खा। दुबला हो गया है। *(to the room, bright with it)* और सुन, लड़के की फोटो आई है राजस्थान से। अच्छा घर है, खाता-पीता। मंजू वहाँ राज करेगी। सरहद पार है बस, पर बस का सीधा है, तीन घंटे में।

Deva looks at his sister. He has spent the whole day learning what a girl is worth in this belt, and the word
*राज करेगी* sits wrong in his ear now, like a coin that rings false.

DEVA: मंजू। तू देखी है फोटो? तुझे... ठीक लगा?

MANJU: *(barely looking up)* जैसा आप सब ठीक समझें, भैया।

DEVA: मैं तुझसे पूछ रहा हूँ। तुझे।

Manju glances at her mother, then down again, and says nothing, which is its own answer. Amma fills the
silence the way mothers do.

AMMA: अरे, क्या पूछ रहा है इससे। शरमाती है। *(she pats Deva's hand)* तू बस अपनी नौकरी ठीक से कर, बाक़ी सब हो जाएगा।

Deva starts to say something — about old men and young girls and what a marriage can be made to mean — and
lets it go. This match is a good one; it is not a sale. And still he cannot get the day out of his mouth.

---

## SCENE 17 — CLEAN
**INT. AMARGARH CBN POST — DAY**

The report is already back. The same day it went out. The MUNSHI hands it across almost apologetically. Ratan
reads it: typed, stamped, two lines — death consistent with a fall, no foul play indicated. He holds it and
does not put it down.

RATAN: उसी दिन?

MUNSHI: डॉक्टर साहब ने जल्दी कर दी, साहब। बोले, आपका मामला है, अटकाना नहीं।

Ratan has handed Dr. Bhanwar a dozen reports like this over the years, and not one of them ever took less
than a week, because a real postmortem takes a week. He knows exactly what a same-day clean report is, in his
hands, in his ink. He spent twenty-five years making them. The clean page does not close the girl's death for
him. It opens everything standing behind it.

He folds the report into his pocket, and for once he is not tired in the way he is usually tired.

---

## SCENE 18 — GO HOME, PAWAR SAHIB
**INT. DISTRICT HOSPITAL — DR. BHANWAR'S ROOM — DAY**

DR. BHANWAR SINGH does not get up. A tired, clean-shaven man in a pressed shirt, a Rajput of Ratan's own age
and kind, with a room that smells of phenyl and old paper.

BHANWAR: रिपोर्ट मिल गई होगी। साफ़ है। गिरने से मौत।

RATAN: उसी दिन कैसे साफ़ हो गई, भँवर?

BHANWAR: *(not rising to it)* नियम से हुआ है। पच्चीस साल से यही नियम है, पँवार साहब। *(he meets Ratan's
eye, flat)* आपके भी इतने केस इसी नियम से बंद हुए हैं। एक बार भी आपने नहीं पूछा "उसी दिन कैसे।" आज अचानक क्या बदल गया?

RATAN: पता नहीं।

It is the truest thing he has said in years, and it costs him to say it out loud. Bhanwar hears it. He does
not soften — that is Mishra's trade, not his. He keeps the cold courtesy of one finished Rajput to another,
and that formality is its own wall.

BHANWAR: घर जाइये, पँवार साहब। आपके दो साल बचे हैं। एक मरी हुई लड़की के लिए उन्हें ख़राब मत कीजिए। वो वापस नहीं आएगी, और आप भी नहीं आएँगे।

Ratan stands a moment longer, looking at the man across the desk — the same khaki, the same twenty-five
years, the same arrangement, a mirror that talks. He had reached, with the name, for the old brotherhood;
Bhanwar gave him back आप. Then he leaves. There is no clean channel left to him now; he has just stood in
front of the last one.

---

## SCENE 19 — OFF THE BOOKS
**EXT. KOTESHWAR — FIELDS — DUSK**

No paper will help him now. Every paper in the district lies, and he knows it, because he taught it how. So
he does the thing he has not done in twenty-five years. He goes out among the field hands at the end of the
day, on foot, off the books, and asks — whose girl, whose house, who saw her last. They are afraid of the
asking, and of him, and of the big house, all at once.

RATAN: *(to a HAND coiling rope)* तू उस रात खेत पे था?

HAND: मैं कुछ नहीं जानता, साहब। मज़दूर आदमी हूँ। सुबह आता हूँ, शाम जाता हूँ।

RATAN: मैं तुझे फँसा नहीं रहा। बस पूछ रहा हूँ।

The hand looks at him a long moment, weighing the old inspector against the thing everyone in the village has
already agreed to forget. Then, quietly, giving him only the smallest true thing:

HAND: बड़ा घर है उधर, साहब। भेरूलाल सेठ का। *(he goes back to his rope)* उधर का मामला है। मुझसे मत पूछो।

The name is still nothing to Ratan. He turns and looks across the darkening fields at the big house standing
at the end of them, lit up, and he walks toward it.

---

## SCENE 20 — THE HOUSE
**EXT./INT. BHERULAL'S HOUSE — NIGHT**

A prosperous house, gold in the doorway, a generator humming somewhere. Ratan comes up the steps with his cap
in his hand — a policeman come to ask a family about a dead girl. He knocks. There are feet inside, and a
bolt drawn back, and the door opens.

It is SUGNA.

Twenty-five years. Neither of them moves. Somewhere inside the house a woman is crying and stops.

SUGNA: अब आए हो।

RATAN: ...सुगना।

She looks at the cap in his hands, and the khaki, and back at his face.

SUGNA: वर्दी में आए हो। पूछताछ करने।

RATAN: मुझे नहीं पता था कि ये तेरा घर है। सच कह रहा हूँ, सुगना। मुझे नहीं —

SUGNA: तुझे कभी कुछ पता कहाँ था।

He has no answer to that, the way he has had no answer to it for twenty-five years. Behind her the house is
dim, full of people sitting in the heavy quiet that comes after a death. On a shelf by the door, a girl's
things — a school bag, a comb, a folded dupatta, set aside the way you set aside what you cannot yet put
away. He looks at them, and at his sister's closed face, and it begins to come.

RATAN: *(quiet)* किसकी... किसकी लड़की थी?

Sugna's jaw works. She will not say it. From inside, an AUNT's voice, taking the policeman at the door for
only a policeman:

AUNT (O.S.): लीला थी। सुगना की बड़ी बेटी। सोलह की थी बस।

The name goes into him and does not stop. Leela was his sister's daughter. He read the first line of that
file and signed it a fall and never turned to the name on the second page; he took the day's money and went
home and ate his dinner.

RATAN: मैंने...

SUGNA: तूने क्या?

He cannot say it. She stands and watches him not say it. BHERULAL fills the inner doorway then — heavy, still,
taking in the brother-in-law in the uniform standing at his door at the worst hour he could have come.

BHERULAL: कौन है? *(he sees Ratan, and stops)* ...ओह।

SUGNA: *(not turning to her husband)* मेरा भाई। पुलिस में है। पच्चीस साल से नहीं आया। *(a beat)* आज आया है।

She steps back from the door — not to let him in, but because she is done holding it open — and turns into the
mourning house, and leaves him on the step with her husband's eyes on him.

---

## SCENE 21 — THE BIKE THAT SLIPPED
**INT. BHERULAL'S HOUSE — NIGHT**

A side room, away from the mourners. Bherulal sits Ratan down and sits across from him. He is not grieving in
front of Ratan; he is working out a problem, and Ratan is the problem.

BHERULAL: पच्चीस साल इस घर का मुँह नहीं देखा तुमने। सुगना का भाई हो, और शादी में नहीं आए, बेटी होने पे नहीं
आए, कुछ नहीं। *(he lets that sit)* और आज, इसी रात, चले आए। वर्दी पहन के। और लाश रुकवा दी अस्पताल में — माँ तड़प रही है, बेटी को जला तक नहीं पा रही।

RATAN: लड़की कैसे मरी, भेरूलाल?

BHERULAL: *(ignoring it, smooth, a man laying out terms)* जो हुआ सो हुआ। लीला चली गई। उससे क्या लौटेगा? अब अगर पोस्टमॉर्टम हो, बात फैले — तो किसका नुक़सान? तुम्हारी बहन का। इस घर का। *(a beat, the real thing, said lightly)* छोटा है ना, चार साल का। उसके नाम पट्टा बनना है अगले साल। उसके लिए घर का नाम साफ़ चाहिए। एक मरी हुई लड़की के लिए ज़िंदा बच्चे का भविष्य नहीं डुबाते।

Ratan says nothing. From the side, by the door, one of Bherulal's MEN speaks — easy, flat, the way you'd
mention the weather.

AADMI: इलाके में हादसे होते रहते हैं, साहब। आदमी रात को निकलता है, बाइक फिसल जाती है, बस। होता रहता है। आप तो जानते ही हो।

It is said without heat, which is what makes it what it is. Ratan looks at the man, and then past him, toward
the room where his sister sits among the women and will not look at him at all.

---

## SCENE 22 — THE PEN, ALL NIGHT
**INT. RATAN'S HOUSE — NIGHT**

A glass of doda water goes cold at his elbow — the poppy-husk brew he drinks to put the days down. Tonight it
does not work. On the table in front of him are two papers. One is the clean report, his way out, the
honourable one: he did his duty, it is official, he can stop now and keep his pension and his life. The other
is a blank requisition form.

He turns his hand over. The pale band where the ring used to be is white in the lamplight. Down the passage,
KANTA appears in her doorway in her nightclothes and sees him sitting up over papers at this hour, which he
has not done in years. She does not ask. She knows the shape of him well enough to know it is not a thing he
will say. She goes back in and the light under her door goes out.

He does not pick up the pen for a long time. Outside, the night thins toward grey. And then, near morning,
he picks it up.

---

## SCENE 23 — HIS OWN NAME
**INT. AMARGARH CBN POST — DAWN**

The post is empty and grey, the fan still. Ratan writes out a fresh requisition by hand — a second
postmortem, this one at the district headquarters hospital two hundred kilometres away, out of Bhanwar's
reach, and Mishra's, and the big house's. At the bottom he does not press the office stamp into it. He signs
it himself, his own name, in full, the way a man signs a thing he means to stand behind.

DEVA comes in early, the dawn grey behind him, and stops when he sees the old man already at the desk. He
sees what the requisition is, and the name on it, and what it will cost. He does not say anything. He stands
a little straighter.

Ratan caps the pen and sets it down. He has the look of a man who has finally decided something.

CUT TO BLACK.

*[End of Episode 1.]*
