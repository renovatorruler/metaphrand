# FREE ROSS DETECTIVE AGENCY — Series & Character Bible

**What this document is:** the consolidated reference for the series' protagonist, premise,
season-one architecture, and episode format. World canon lives in `ARENA.md`; this document
is people and story. Items the author may want to refine are marked **[TO CONFIRM]**.

**Format:** a TV series, or equivalently a series of novels in the Dresden Files mold —
episodic cases + a season/book arc + a series-level meta. The structure has two registers:
**the meta** (series-long) and **the per-arc** (one season / one book). Season one is
specified below; the meta beyond it is open.

**Working title (canon):** *Free Ross Detective Agency* — which is also, diegetically, the
name of the agency founded in the season-one finale.

## Part I — The Protagonist: Ross

**Full name canon: ROSS UNDERWOOD.** His late wife: **MABEL** (active role in the prequel; see PREQUEL.md).

### The design rationale (a writing law for this series)
The audience itself may be hostile to an anarcho-capitalist world. Therefore the protagonist
is their proxy: **an unwilling immigrant to Ancapistan** — a man from the Union States who
did not choose this place, does not believe in it, and resents being here. The world must
earn Ross exactly as it must earn the viewer. He is never a tour guide; he is a skeptic
being slowly out-argued by results.

### Who he is
An ex-NYPD cop — *not* homicide division — from New York City, Union States. A disgraced
officer who, at the start of the story, sits in a **New York State prison**, close to
finishing an **8-to-10-year sentence**, looking forward to one thing only: reuniting with
his **daughter**.

### The backstory (the fall)
- Ross's wife came from **El Dorado** — a fictional Latin American country in the grip of a
  war that has made it the largest source of immigration into the Union States.
- A **serial killer** was murdering women from these immigrant communities in New York. The
  police did not take the victims seriously.
- His wife pushed Ross to act. He wouldn't go against his superiors. So she began taking
  matters into her own hands — and the killer, whose pattern shifted opportunistically,
  **killed her.**
- Ross went over the edge hunting the case. He eventually identified a suspect; the NYPD
  lacked the evidence to hold him and let him go. The suspect **taunted Ross — indirectly,
  deniably — confirming to Ross that he was the man.** No proof existed.
- Ross, drinking heavily since his wife's death, found the suspect in a bar and beat him
  into a coma. The coma lasted over a year. The sentence followed: 8-to-10, state prison.
- **Canon, stated plainly for the writers' room: the suspect is the real serial killer.**

### The wound he carries into the series
He was right, and being right cost him everything: wife dead, badge gone, freedom gone,
daughter growing up without him. And the system he served — the one with warrants, evidence
standards, and superiors — is the system that let the killer walk and locked Ross up. He
arrives in Ancapistan believing in nothing, least of all a world with no police at all.

### The character explanation (canon — full theory in METHOD.md)
Why Ross is worth following, in the House/Bosch sense of a creed that generates the gift:

- **The creed: EVERYBODY KNOWS.** People lie to themselves before they lie to anyone; the
  truth of a case already exists, whole, in someone's head. Detection is not discovery —
  it is the forced admission. He makes people show their hands. (House: "everybody lies."
  Bosch: "everybody counts or nobody counts." Ross: "everybody knows.")
- **The method: the dream-read.** Everybody *subconsciously* knows, and the subconscious
  broadcasts it, encoded. Ross interprets waking reality the way an analyst reads a dream —
  treating testimony as a production, not a report (a lie is false about the world but true
  about the liar). The full PaRDeS theory of detection — Peshat sovereign, no read without
  a snag, every read staked, Sod yielded never asserted, retro-illumination,
  countertransference as the failure mode — lives in METHOD.md and is **never named or
  explained on screen**.
- **Why he's better than the average detective here:** Ancapistan's agencies are superb at
  facts — it's an actuarial industry; the evidence market is efficient. The one thing the
  market can't price is the human holding the cards. The Union criminalized his way of
  knowing; Dallas is the one jurisdiction where it's legal tender. Same man, two systems:
  bad cop, great detective.
- **The wound completes it:** the one time he trusted his read at full stakes he was right,
  and being right destroyed his life. The best reader of men alive no longer trusts his own
  reads — and the season-one chiasmus is the system forcing his hand open.

## Part II — The Killer

- Woke from the coma. Recovered his functionality. Relocated to the **Free States** and
  resumed killing.
- The agencies of Ancapistan come to realize **a serial killer is operating among them** — a
  nightmare case for a private-law system.
- **THE KEY BACKSTORY (canon): he GREW UP IN ANCAPISTAN.** He left for
  the Union States — where *he* was the new immigrant — and after the coma he came home and
  **reactivated his old profile**: real coverage history, real established facts, real
  community standing, all dormant and all his. This is why he operates so freely. While
  every agency hunts among recent arrivals from the Union, the killer is an old citizen.
  The misdirection is structural: everyone looks at the new immigrants; it's one of their own.
- **His signature: the long con** (full statement in PREQUEL.md Part C): elaborate,
  unnecessary ruses — weeks-to-months engagements, several victims in parallel — luring
  victims into complacency before the kill, when a short con would have served. The excess
  is the signature. The behavioral "why" is deliberately unset.
- **In method terms (METHOD.md Part V): he is a counterfeit pardes.** His surfaces survive
  every fact-check (that's what the months are for), and he plants the hints his victims
  "discover" — people believe their own reads, so he weaponizes theirs. What he cannot
  launder is his own selection: the unnecessary part of the lie is the confession, and
  **cleanliness is the fingerprint** — real lives are messy; a composed life has full
  employment. This is why procedure fails against him honestly, and why only Ross can hunt
  him.
- **[TO CONFIRM]:** whether any Ancapistan agency connects that this is the same killer who
  operated in the Union. The author's note: unsure if the dots are connected in-world; what
  is canon is that only the underdog agency makes the lateral move of going after the one
  man who knows him best.
- Name and Free States victimology: **unset [TO CONFIRM]**.

## Part III — The Underdog Agency

### Origin (canon)
A large, established agency had two co-founders. They fell out; one was **pushed out**. The
exile started a new agency **under his own name** — and the courts ruled he could not use
it, because the company that expelled him *is* his name. The author's analogy: **as if Apple
had kicked out Steve Jobs, and Apple was literally called "Jobs."**

- So the big agency bears the founder's name; his new agency must trade under something
  else. **[Both company names unset — TO CONFIRM. The big agency = his surname.]**
- The founder is completely miffed. His entire drive is to **defeat his ex-partner in front
  of everyone.** Under-resourced, over-motivated — the classic underdog shop.

### The daring, illegal plan
Holding that Ross is **the one person who knows the most about this serial killer** from his
Union States days, the agency does something wildly illegal under Ancapistan's own norms:
**spring a convict out of a Union prison.**

- The method: they **fake a program** offered to Ross's prison — an "ex-law-enforcement
  rehabilitation program."
- **Ross says no.** He doesn't want a program; he wants his daughter, and his release date
  is close.
- His refusal — plus a **warden growing suspicious** — collapses the soft approach, and the
  operation becomes a **prison break**. They take him anyway.
- **THE STORY STARTS HERE.** The series opens at the break/abduction; everything in Parts I
  and II arrives as backstory.

### The opening state (pilot)
They smuggle Ross into Ancapistan, hand him a badge — agency credentials — and tell him:
hunt this man. **Ross refuses.** Not because he doubts his old identification — he is
*correct* about who the killer is — but because **he believes that man is still in a coma.**
Therefore this Free States killer cannot be the same man, therefore this isn't his fight,
therefore he wants to go home to his daughter. The pilot ends with that refusal standing.

## Part IV — The Team

### The love interest: the in-house arbitrator
A woman at the same agency. Her job — and this is also structural canon about how these
companies work, cross-referenced to ARENA.md: **every agency team embeds its own arbitrator
the way every police team here would embed a judge.** She predicts and pre-rules on the
evidence as a neutral third-party arbitrator would, so the company can steer its case — what
to stake, what to fold, what a showdown would return. She is the team's oracle of *how this
will actually be ruled.*

- She has a **daughter** — who, in the season-one climax, is kidnapped by the serial killer.
- Name, history, how the romance builds: **unset [TO CONFIRM]**.

### The future nemesis: Enya (planted in the prequel)
**Enya** — Ross's NYPD partner from the prequel era (full dossier in PREQUEL.md Part B): a
new recruit, his opposite in every way, the true believer in the Union States system, a

## Part V — Season One (8 episodes)

### Episode format (the House MD model, canon)
Each episode contains:
1. **The primary case** — the episode's "patient": one main dispute/investigation Ross and
   the team must handle.
2. **The clinic duty** — a stream of small, quick cases threaded through, serving as comic
   relief and as the showcase of brilliance (House's walk-in clinic, transplanted).
3. **The arc thread** — the season-long trace of the serial killer advancing underneath.

### The arc's shape
- Episode by episode, Ross keeps coming close to the serial killer — while officially
  refusing to believe it's his man.
- The trail ends in **Cainland**: the killer has been hiding there, and he has **kidnapped
  the daughter of the in-house arbitrator** — the woman Ross has come to love.
- **The climax inversion (the season's thesis in one move):** Ross — a man dragged out of
  his comfort zone against his will at every previous step, the unwilling immigrant, the
  kidnapped cop — **willingly walks into Cainland**, the most dangerous place in the world,
  to get her back. The season's bookend: it opens with him refusing to leave a prison cell
  for Ancapistan, and ends with him volunteering for the one place even Ancapistan fears.
- **Cainland is not what everyone thinks** (consistent with ARENA.md Part V): as in the
  Bible, where Cain after his exile built cities, the exiles have built *something* — not
  elaborate cities, but structure. The season-one climax is where the audience first sees
  inside. (The full nature of Cainland's structure remains the author's held card.)
- **The ending:** Ross catches the serial killer **and kills him.**

### The bets — the chiasmic device (canon, and the season's signature)
Throughout the season, Ross has been placing stakes in the Texas Hold'em fact-betting system
— case by case, fold by fold. At season's end, read backward, **his betting record forms a
chiasmus: the bets reveal that on some level he knew who the serial killer was all along.**
The factual record as an X-ray of a man's unadmitted convictions — the system itself
becomes the instrument of his self-revelation. And the money those bets earned becomes his
seed capital.

### The finale
With his winnings, Ross founds the **Free Ross Detective Agency** — the series title made
diegetic — and **gives partnership to the teammates who choose to join him.** The unwilling
immigrant ends season one as a founder: a man who was carried into Ancapistan in a trunk,
choosing to stay, on his own name and his own terms.

**[Open consequence — TO CONFIRM]:** Ross is an escaped Union convict; his daughter is in
the Union; his sentence was nearly over when the agency stole his release from him. The
daughter thread (does she come over? custody? does she believe the escape story?) and the
Union's pursuit posture are unset and presumably feed the series meta.
