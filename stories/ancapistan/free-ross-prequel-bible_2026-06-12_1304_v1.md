# THE PREQUEL — Development Bible

**What this document is:** the structured reference for the prequel to *Free Ross Detective
Agency*. World canon: `ARENA.md`. Main-series canon: `SERIES.md`. Items awaiting the author
are marked **[TO CONFIRM]**.

**What the prequel is for (canon about the project itself):** the prequel will **never be
published or produced**. It is the development forge — maximum creative leeway, story
problems permitted, because its purpose is to hammer out the characters, the world's
texture, and the setup until they're true. Everything learned here feeds the main series;
nothing here needs to survive as shippable narrative.

**When it is set:** the years of Ross's NYPD service, while the serial killer is active in
the Union States — ending in Mabel's death and Ross's fall (the events summarized in
SERIES.md Part I, here given at full length). The Cainland plotline runs in this same era,
sealed off from the rest. **[Exact years TO CONFIRM — the main story is "decades after the
war," and Ross's sentence is 8–10 years before the main story's start.]**

---

## The Four Plotlines

The prequel braids four strands. Three of them (Ross, Enya, the killer) interweave in New
York. The fourth (Cainland) **never touches the others** — by design. The eventual collision
between the Cainland world and Ross's world is reserved for the main series, near its end.

---

## Plotline A — Ross Underwood, NYPD

*(Full name now canon: **Ross Underwood**. His wife: **Mabel**.)*

### The institutional doctrine: the NYPD is anti-intuition (canon, load-bearing)
This is the explicit inversion of the entire cop-intuition tradition — Holmes, the gifted
gut, the detective who "just knows." In this Union States, the NYPD is **officially and
openly anti-intuition**:

- Departmental doctrine holds that intuition is the engine of bigotry — hunches are
  prejudice wearing a halo, the root of racist policing. Therefore intuition is not merely
  unprofessional; it is the named enemy.
- There are **anti-intuition training regimes**: officers are drilled to operate
  left-brained — procedure, checklist, documented inference only — and to suppress
  right-brain pattern-leaps entirely.
- **Tone guardrail:** this is not played for laughs. It must be plausible, sincere, even
  half-defensible — an institution that has decided the cost of intuition exceeds its value,
  enforced by people who believe it.

**Ross's defining trait runs head-on into this:** he operates on intuition. His gut is
genuinely good — and every time he uses it, he is *in trouble*, flagged, written up,
re-enrolled in training. The tragedy of the prequel is that the man whose intuition was
right about the killer lives inside the one institution that treats his gift as a disease.
(And the main series' irony is born here: the place that finally values how his mind works
is the place with no police at all.)

### Mabel (active role)
Ross's wife, from **El Dorado** — the war-torn Latin American country sending the Union its
largest immigrant influx. The prequel gives her an active storyline, not a fridge:

- Women from her community are being murdered. The police do not take the victims seriously.
- Mabel pushes Ross to act; Ross — disillusioned but obedient — will not go against his
  superiors.
- So **Mabel takes things into her own hands.** Her amateur investigation is an active
  strand: what she finds, who she talks to, what she gets close to. **[Her methods, allies,
  and discoveries: TO CONFIRM.]**
- The killer — whose pattern is the long con, but who improvises when circumstance offers —
  kills her. **[Whether she had become a target through her investigation or crossed him by
  accident: TO CONFIRM — the author's phrasing: "he does things here and there," i.e., the
  death comes by his opportunism, not necessarily by his signature.]**

### Ross's arc in the prequel
Disillusionment → grief → obsession → the identification (right, by intuition, provable by
nothing) → the release of the suspect → the deniable taunt → the binge → the bar → the coma
→ the sentence. The prequel ends with Ross in a New York State prison and the killer alive.

## Plotline B — Enya

*(Name canon: **Enya**. Surname **[TO CONFIRM]**.)*

- A new recruit, partnered with Ross. **The opposite of Ross in every way.**
- **The true believer:** where Ross is disillusioned with the Union States system, Enya
  believes in it — sincerely, energetically, to the bone. She is the stand-in for everyone
  who thinks the Union States are doing the right thing.
- She **participates in the UN dramas** — the Eurovision-style staged performances that have
  replaced speeches (ARENA.md Part VI). What that participation looks like for a New York
  cop — performer? honor guard? civic delegation? — **[TO CONFIRM]**, but it is canon that
  she takes part and takes it seriously.
- She also wants to catch the killer. "But she's different" — her hunt runs by the book the
  department wrote: procedure, doctrine, anti-intuition compliance. Two partners chasing the
  same monster with opposite cognitive licenses.
- **The romance rule (strict):** no romantic interest is shown between Enya and Ross. He is
  a married man and stays one. She is attractive; he is the protagonist; **the audience will
  ship them on its own — let it.** The show gives them nothing. A later season of the main
  series deals with what this was.
- **The future (main-series canon, planted here):** in season 2 or 3 of the main series,
  **Enya becomes Ross's real nemesis.** The prequel's job is to build her so that the
  nemesis turn, when it comes, is the tragedy of a true believer — not a twist.

## Plotline C — The Serial Killer (Union States era)

*(Name **[TO CONFIRM]**.)*

- The prequel shows him **operating**: the cons, the engagements, the kills — his earlier
  version, at work in the Union States.
- **The signature (canon): the long con.** He lures victims into complacency through
  elaborate, unnecessary ruses — engagements lasting weeks or months, several running in
  parallel with several victims. The reference contrast: Buffalo Bill's fake-injured arm is
  a *short* con — minutes of theater for a moment of trust. This man builds *relationships*
  — jobs, courtships, charities, whatever the ruse requires — and at the end of the long
  con, he kills, even though a short con would have served. The excess IS the signature.
- **Why he does it: unknown, deliberately.** The author has not chosen the
  behavioral-analysis explanation. **[OPEN — the BAU "why" is unset; candidate explanations
  should be developed, not assumed.]**
- **The backstory (canon — and the main-series reveal mechanism):** he **grew up in
  Ancapistan.** He left it for the Union States — where *he* was the new immigrant — and
  after the coma, he goes back. This is why he operates so freely in the main series:
  **he isn't a newcomer slipping through screening; he is an old citizen with an old
  profile, reactivated.** Coverage history, established facts, community standing — all real,
  all dormant, all his. While every agency hunts among recent arrivals from the Union, the
  killer is one of Ancapistan's own, come home. (This is now also recorded in SERIES.md
  Part II.)
- **[TO CONFIRM]:** how much of the Ancapistan origin the prequel itself shows. The prequel
  is unpublished and may explore it fully; what the main series' audience learns, and when,
  is a separate decision the author holds.

## Plotline D — Cainland (the Wild West strand)

### The sleight of hand (the strand's governing trick)
Cainland plays as a **Wild West drama** — saloons, sheriffs, horses, dust. The audience is
meant to assume the 1800s. It is actually the **2050s (or thereabouts)**. Nothing announces
this; instead, *something is always slightly off*. The design rule: these are people of
today — modern knowledge, modern consciousness, modern idiom under the period surface —
forced to live at an 1800s material level. They didn't forget the modern world; they were
exiled from it. The wrongness accumulates in details until the viewer questions the era.
**[The reveal moment, if any, inside the prequel: TO CONFIRM.]**

### Isolation rule
**This plotline never interacts with the New York plotlines.** No shared characters, no
crossover, no winks. The collision between Cainland's world and Ross's is the main series'
endgame property.

### The naming convention (canon)
Cainland's people were named by Bible-soaked parents who identified with **the line of
Cain** — so the population carries names like Enoch, Lamech, Jabal, Jubal, Tubal-Cain,
Naamah, Irad, Adah, Zillah. And, humanly: plenty of them **hate it** — some rename
themselves after *other*, untainted biblical figures; some go by diminutives and nicknames
engineered to bury the original. (A man named for a murderer's line, introducing himself by
a nickname that means nothing — that's a character note in two words.)

### The triangle
- **The sheriff** — a good man. The standing irony: the lawless land's lawman, and genuinely
  the best man in it. Working name: **Sheriff Enoch** *(Cain's son, for whom the first city
  was named — fitting for the man holding a town together)* **[TO CONFIRM]**.
- **His twin brother** — the "evil twin," in quotes, because he isn't truly evil: more
  lawless than his brother, looser, freer with everything — but not a monster. Working name:
  **Lamech** *(the line's boastful killer — a name he'd wear with a smirk)* **[TO CONFIRM]**.
- **The woman between them** — the sheriff's former wife, who cheated on him **with his twin
  brother**. Working name: **Naamah** *(the only named woman of Cain's line)*
  **[TO CONFIRM]**.

### Her sections (canon, and the strand's darkness)
Parts of this plotline are told **from her perspective, and they are dark**:

- She suffers **acute PTSD after giving birth to stillborn twins.**
- **Nobody tells her.** The town — both brothers included — lets her pretend: she keeps
  dolls, and everyone around her treats the dolls as her living babies. The horror is
  communal tenderness: an entire town maintaining a merciful lie.
- The sheriff is deeply wounded by her infidelity; she cries over it; **she knows she hurt a
  good man.** Guilt, grief, and the doll-babies braid into her POV sections.
- *(Note the resonance, never to be stated: twins born to her, twins in love with her.)*

### The plot engine
- The town **killed a man, and his friends are coming for revenge.**
- **The opening scene (canon — the strand begins here):** a stranger arrives in town,
  classic saloon-drama staging — and is **shot by three different residents, for three
  different reasons, the third shooter being the sheriff himself.** Three motives, one body.
  The dead man's friends now hunt the town.
- **[The author will elaborate the revenge plot later — stub held open: who the dead man
  was, the three reasons, the shape of the reckoning.]**
