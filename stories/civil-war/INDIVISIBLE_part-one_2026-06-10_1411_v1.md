## 1

Desmond set the axe to the log again and missed the split again, and the wood rolled off the block into the dirt.

Nia came out on the porch in the gray dress and watched him miss a while.

"You're chopping it like it owes you money," she said.

"Go on inside."

"Mama does it better than you."

"Inside, Nia."

She didn't go in. She sat on the step with her knees up under the dress and looked down the road, the way they all did now. The house stood at the end of a long dirt track that ran off a county road that ran off another, and the only reason they were still alive was that nobody had reason to come down it.

Della had the rifles apart on the kitchen table, wiping them down while she talked to herself about supper.

They kept their rules: nobody past the tree line alone, no lamp in the window after dark, somebody on the road from first light to last.

Nia saw the dust first. She didn't shout, just said "Daddy" and lifted her chin to where the track met the road, where something kicked up a haze with no wind to do it.

Desmond counted four riders.

He sent the girl up the stairs with a look and didn't watch her go. He heard a window slide above him, Della's gun coming to rest on the sill, then the second window, the girl at it. He leaned the axe against the block like a man who meant to get back to it, and stood in the yard with his hands where they showed.

The riders came up easy, in no hurry. Four men, dark as he was, dusty and well-mounted, rifles across their laps. The one in front walked his horse near to the porch before he stopped it. He looked Desmond over, the homespun and the broke-down shoes, and smiled.

"Which plantation you run off from?"

"Don't know what you mean."

"No?" He hung his hands on the horn. "We're after a man. Came through three, four days back. Tall, took a ball in the leg. Would've looked like he needed a place to lie up."

"Nobody comes out this far."

"Somebody did." The man looked the place over, the house, the well, the lean-to. "Bring your wife out. The girl too. We'll all talk about it."

"They got nothing to tell you I can't."

"Wasn't a request."

One of the others got down off his horse and started for the porch, taking his time. Desmond stepped into his way, not fast, just enough to stand between him and the door.

"There's no man here," Desmond said. "Ride on north, you'll cut his trail at the river. I wouldn't waste the daylight on us."

The man on the ground looked back at the leader. The leader sat his horse and chewed on it, looking at the house and the empty road north, and Desmond watched him weigh a houseful of women and a yard full of work against a stranger's leg and come up short.

"Mount up," the leader said.

The one on the ground spat, gave Desmond a last look, and climbed back on.

They were turning the horses when the young one at the back went still. He was looking past the lean-to at the bay tied in the shade, and the saddle on the rail beside her, silver let into the cantle, worth more than the house and everything in it.

"That's his horse," the young one said. "That's —"

Desmond shot him off it before he got the rest out.

After that it went fast. The windows came open above him and Della fired and then the girl, and the riders couldn't get the horses turned in the close of the yard. One went down and stayed down. Another took it high and held on somehow and got himself gone, and the last one went with him, the two of them low over their horses' necks, back down the track the way they'd come.

Desmond stood with the pistol up until there was nothing left to point it at. Then he went inside and up the stairs.

"Pack what you can carry," he said. "Ten minutes."

## 2

The realtor's name was Brad, and he had a way of announcing every room like it was the best one in the house.

"Now this," Brad said, "is your bonus room. Media room, home gym, man cave — whatever speaks to you."

What spoke to Desmond was the window. One window, street side, and past it the cul-de-sac curled back on itself with a single way in and out. He stood there working it the way he worked everything — where's the door, where's the second door, what happens when the first one fails.

"You're doing the thing," Della said, beside him, smiling out at the yard so Brad wouldn't catch her not smiling at her husband.

"I'm looking at the house."

"You're looking at the exits."

"One road in, Dell. One road in is one road out." He kept it low. "I keep telling you. We should be looking at land. Couple acres east of here, some space around us, something we can actually—"

"A compound." She almost laughed. "You want to raise our daughter in a compound."

"I want to raise our daughter."

"Desmond." She looked at him then, still smiling. "It's going to be okay. People have been calling the end of the country since before there was a country. We're going to buy a house, gripe about the HOA, complain about the heat after thirty years of St. Louis winters, and be fine."

"Schools out here are phenomenal, by the way," Brad said from the hall.

"See?" Della said. "Phenomenal schools."

Desmond looked back out the one window.

Outside, Nia found the only other kids on the street.

They were in the next yard over, a boy and a smaller girl, running something back and forth that took a lot of shrieking. The boy looked about her age. Nia went to the chain-link, hooked her fingers in it, and watched, waiting to be noticed. The smaller girl noticed first and stopped, and then the boy stopped.

Then the screen door banged and the mother came out fast, talking. The talk wasn't for Nia. It went over her head in a language she didn't have one word of. She didn't need the words. She had the face, and the way the woman caught the little girl by the wrist and turned her with it, and the way she put herself between her kids and the fence without once looking at what was on the other side of it.

Nia took her fingers out of the chain-link.

She knew it without the words. She'd known it without the words before.

She was almost back to her own yard when she heard the gate.

The boy had come through it. He stood a second like he wasn't sure why, then crossed the grass with his hands in his pockets and stopped a careful few feet off.

"She's not always like that," he said.

"It's okay."

"It's not, though." He looked back at his house, then at her. "You want to see something? There's a frog living in the storm drain by the hydrant. He's the size of a softball."

Nia looked at him.

"I'm serious," the boy said. "He's enormous."

"Okay," Nia said, and went with him.

## 3

The Nguyens were four hundred and ten pounds of people and sixty-one pounds of luggage, and Ray Cole had told them forty on the phone.

He stood at the scale he kept bolted to the hangar floor and watched the doctor take things out of a duffel and look at them and put them back. A framed thing. A folder of papers with a rubber band around it. Shoes.

"Forty," Ray said.

"The frame is my parents."

"The frame is six pounds."

The doctor's wife said something low in Vietnamese, and the doctor took the photograph out of the frame, rolled it, and put it in his coat. He set the frame on the concrete next to the scale, square to the wall, like he might come back for it. People did that. Lined their things up neat at the edge of the hangar the way they'd leave them at a curb. There was a toaster oven over there had been sitting square to the wall since March.

The little girl held the dog up so the dog could watch the scale, and the dog watched it.

"The dog is not on the list," the mother said. It came out half a question.

Ray looked at the dog. Some kind of terrier arithmetic, eleven, twelve pounds. The girl had it in a harness made out of a child's belt, and it had its ears up like the whole thing had been its idea.

"Dogs don't weigh what they cost," Ray said. "He flies."

He didn't say it kind and nobody thanked him, which was how he preferred a thing like that to go.

The gold was short. He knew it before the doctor did, watching him count it out on the wing in the worklight — coins laid in a row like the man was setting a table, and the row came up two shy of where it needed to stop. The doctor counted it again. The second count is for the counter, not the coins. Ray let him have it.

"I have a watch," the doctor said. "Omega. My father's."

"I got a watch."

"It's worth more than two coins."

"Then somebody'll give you more than two coins for it. I'm giving you two, on account of we're standing where we're standing at eleven-forty at night." He picked a coin up off the wing and turned it over and set it back down in the row. "Or you leave a seat empty and the price walks back to where the weight is. I don't care which. I do the math either way."

The doctor looked over at his family standing next to the airplane in their coats. It was sixty degrees out. People wore everything they couldn't pack; you could tell how far somebody was going by how much they had on.

"The watch," the doctor said, and took it off.

Ray put it in the coffee can on the workbench where the odd ends went and forgot it the way you forget a thing you'll find in a year and not remember the man.

The phone in the hangar had rung twice while the doctor counted. Ray took the second one, a man's voice, young, doing the thing where it stands up too straight.

"I heard you fly people."

"Where to?"

"Mexico."

"Where in Mexico."

A silence with swallowing in it. "Mexico," the man said.

"Call me when it's a place," Ray said, and hung up, and didn't think about it again, because a man with a destination is cargo and a man without one is weather, and he didn't fly weather.

Then he walked the airplane. He did it the same way every time, hand on the cowl, fuel sumps, control surfaces, a tug on tiedown chains he'd already taken off, which made no sense and he did it anyway. Under the right side of the glareshield, low where a passenger wouldn't see it unless they knew, there was a name stenciled in paint gone the color of old bone, and his thumb crossed it on the way by, the way a man does a thing with his hand at a door. Not looking. Not slowing.

"Get in," he said. "Belts on everything. Dog rides on the girl."

The strip had no lights and didn't need any. He knew where the dark got darker, where the trees quit. Half the county had stopped paying for the grid anyway and the night out here had gone back to what night was, so the only lights worth anything were the two fires up on the interstate overpass, five, six miles off, where the checkpoint was. They'd been burning every night that week. Nobody knew what they burned up there and everybody knew better than to drive up and ask.

The doctor leaned forward between the seats while Ray ran the engine up.

"They said on the radio they will register everyone. In the spring. Everyone files, everyone is counted, and then the moving begins. For safety, they said."

"They been saying it."

"Do you believe it?"

"I believe weather reports," Ray said, "after the weather."

He came off the strip flat and stayed low in the dark a while, the fires on the overpass sliding past his left window small and orderly, like pilot lights on a stove in a house with nobody home. The little girl had her face at the glass. The dog had its face at the glass. Whatever they were leaving, neither one was looking at it. Kids and dogs look where you're going.

The mother was looking back. The mother was always the one looking back; he could've drawn the load plan blind. The man up front doing arithmetic he was bad at, kids forward of the spar, somebody's parents riding rolled up in a coat pocket, and a woman in the back with her face turned around to forty years of a place going out from under the tail in the dark.

Two hours and ten minutes of nothing. Ray liked the nothing. The border used to be a thing you'd notice at night, towns paired up across the river like dance partners. Now the lights came up bright on the south side and stopped dead on the north like the country had been unplugged at the wall.

The strip outside Sabinas had a man named Beto on it who kept a generator and two sons and asked no questions that didn't have numbers in them. Ray put her down long and easy past the brush line and taxied to where Beto's truck sat with its parking lights on.

The Nguyens stood by their bags in the truck lights while the sons loaded. The doctor came back to the wing, where Ray was already pumping fuel out of Beto's drums for the empty leg home.

"In Monterrey there is a man who takes us to my brother in Toronto. Eleven days, they told us."

"Then you're most of the way there."

The doctor stood a second. He was working up to something with thank you in it, and Ray kept his eyes on the fuel going amber in the sight tube until the moment starved out. Over by the truck the girl walked the dog in the dirt, and the dog lifted its leg on Mexico like it was any other country, which it was, which was the whole thing nobody on that airplane would have said out loud.

"You carry many people out?" the doctor asked instead.

"I carry weight," Ray said. "Some of it talks."

He watched the truck lights go off down the ranch road until the brush took them. Then it was him and the airplane and Beto's generator knocking at the dark, and the leg home with nothing in it, which paid what it always paid.

He was airborne before one. Crossing back he picked up the overpass fires from thirty miles out. Still burning. Orderly. Two of them — then while he watched, a third came up small and new, further east, and he noted it the way he noted weather building. No opinion in it yet. Just where it was, and which way the wind would move it.

His thumb found the stencil in the dark.

Two coins short, a watch in a can, a dog flown free. He ran the numbers the whole way down the dark side of Texas and they came out the way they always came out. Even. Nothing owed anybody.

He landed at two-forty and chained the airplane down and stood a minute in the open hangar door. The toaster oven and the picture frame and a suitcase with a broken wheel sat square against the wall in the moonlight, all of it lined up neat.

Like the start of a town.

## 4

The Saturday the cash machines took the weekend off, Desmond was at the Brookshire's by seven with a list, and he was not the only one with a list.

He worked it top to bottom, no substitutions, while the aisles filled up behind him with people buying the way people buy ahead of a hurricane that isn't coming, which is wider and angrier than buying ahead of one that is. Water, yes. Batteries, yes. But also birthday candles, also charcoal, also four carts of bottled tea going by like a parade float, because nobody knew what to be afraid of yet and so they were afraid of everything in the store.

At the register the girl said the card machines were running slow and might quit, and the man ahead of Desmond paid for two hundred dollars of groceries with a roll of twenties rubber-banded like a fist, and nobody in the line found that strange, and Desmond noted that nobody found it strange.

"Bank holiday," the man said to the air, friendly. "That's what they're calling it. A holiday."

Desmond paid, loaded the truck, and sat in the lot a minute doing the quiet arithmetic. Two weeks of food was now three days of crowd away from being no food. The grid had hiccuped twice that month, June-style heat coming, and the generator he'd priced in May was four hundred dollars more in June if you could find one, and you could not find one.

He'd said all of this to Della in May. He did not say it again now. He drove home past the gas line forming at the QT, eleven cars at nine in the morning, and counted the line the way other men checked the score.

⸻

The thing about a porch is that it faces out.

They'd gotten in the habit without calling it one. Somewhere around the second week in the house, after Nia was down, Della would bring out two mugs of the decaf neither one of them liked all that much and they'd sit on the step while the sprinklers came on up the street, hers on the rail, his on the board beside him, fifteen minutes, sometimes not a word in it. The neighborhood ticking itself cool. Some nights her foot ended up against his knee. That was the whole ceremony.

"Janice at school says her husband's putting in a safe room," Della said. It was the Tuesday after the bank holiday. "Concrete. The whole thing."

"Where's he putting it?"

"Garage, I think."

"Then it's a safe room with a water heater in it and one door." He turned the mug a quarter turn on the board. "But fine. People are starting to think about doors. That's something."

"You've been thinking about doors since the open house."

"Since before that, probably."

She looked at him over her mug, and the sprinklers shut off up the street one yard at a time, and she didn't tell him he was wrong, which he noticed, the way you notice the first night of a season.

⸻

The bus came apart on a Wednesday, on the far side of the metroplex, and by Thursday everyone in the country had seen the inside of it.

Forty-one people, a church group, the wrong neighborhood by one exit ramp by one account and on purpose by another, and then the second thing, the thing that made it the only footage on every screen — the men walking the line of survivors at the shoulder of the road, checking faces against phones, letting some sit down in the grass and walking others toward the underpass, sorting them, on camera, with the brake lights still on.

Nobody reputable said which group the men belonged to. Then everybody said it. By Friday the no-name channels had names and the names didn't match each other, and by Saturday it didn't matter anymore whose names they were, because every street in Texas had picked one and was acting on it.

Desmond watched it once, the whole clip, standing in the kitchen with the sound off. Della came in and watched thirty seconds of it and went out back to where Nia was doing chin-up attempts on the swing set, and stayed out there with her until dinner, teaching her something loud with rules to it.

He watched it through a second time. Not the violence. The sorting. The man with the phone had a system — look, check, point — and the line did what lines do, it cooperated, it organized itself against itself, and the clip ran two minutes eleven and not one person in it ran.

A system has a load it's rated for, he thought, and a failure mode past it, and the failure mode of a line of people is that it stays a line.

Nia's laugh came in through the window screen, and under it the swing-set chain going like a slow hinge, and he turned the screen dark and stood there until he had his face right.

⸻

The lawn signs changed first.

The realtors' signs came down — nothing was for sale now, or everything was but not out loud — and the new ones came up in their place, yard by yard, like a crop coming in. WE LOOK OUT FOR EACH OTHER on a green sign with two clasped hands. Then the other kind, three houses down, black on white, NO ENTRY AFTER DARK — RESIDENTS ONLY, and nobody had voted on either one but there they were, multiplying.

The HOA meeting in June had been about the retention pond. The HOA meeting in August had a sign-in table, two men Desmond didn't know standing at the back with their arms crossed, and an agenda that used the word *perimeter* four times. Walt Pruitt, who in June had wanted everyone fined over trash-can timing, stood up at the front with a laminated map of the subdivision and started talking about *choke points*, and the room — schoolteachers, a dentist, the Nigerian engineer from Birchwood Court with his hand politely up the whole time and never called on — the room nodded along like this was the retention pond again, like this was normal upkeep, assessments and easements and who's on the roster for Thursday nights.

Desmond sat near the back and said nothing the whole hour. He was watching the map. Walt had the entrance circled twice in red, and the thing about a cul-de-sac, the thing he'd said in an empty bonus room a year ago to a wife who'd smiled at him for it, was on that map in red marker for a roomful of neighbors, only now it wasn't a worry. Now it was an asset.

One road in.

In the truck after, Della stared out the windshield at the clubhouse and didn't put her seatbelt on.

"Eight weeks ago that man was measuring people's grass," she said.

"Walt's not the problem. Walt's a vane. He turns whichever way it's blowing." Desmond started the truck. "I'm interested in the wind."

⸻

He kept the card in his shirt pocket, written out by hand: OIL · COOLANT · BELTS · BATTERY TERMINALS · TIRES INCL SPARE · FUEL FULL · CASH BOX · WATER BOX. Saturdays, the truck nose-out in the driveway, and Nia would come stand on the bumper in her school shoes and hold her hand out for the card like a surgeon, because the deal was she called it and he touched it, every item, out loud, in order.

"Coolant."

"Coolant's good."

"Belts."

"Belt's got a year in it. Maybe two."

"Battery." She leaned in over the fender next to him, dramatic about it. "Terminals look *corroded*."

"They look fine."

"I'm being thorough. You said thorough."

"I said accurate."

She held the card down at her side then and looked at him across the engine bay, and she had her mother's trick of asking the real question with her whole face level, like it weighed the same as the others.

"Are we getting ready for something?"

"We're keeping the truck ready," Desmond said. "Truck doesn't care what for."

She thought it over. Somewhere down the block the Kim boy was shooting baskets alone, the ball going up over the fence line slow and coming down, and the radio inside the truck was running the top of the hour, the new voice everyone had started recognizing without ever being introduced to it, a woman's voice, warm as a school nurse, saying *community restoration*, saying *everyone counted so everyone's cared for*, saying registration would open statewide the first of the month.

"Tires," Nia said.

"Tires," he said, and crouched down at the wheel with the gauge while the voice on the radio thanked everybody in advance for their cooperation.

## 5

The board lived in Avram's shop because the bakery had flour in the air, and a man who has cleaned one escapement of one watch ruined by flour does not need to learn that lesson twice.

Saturdays at one, when the lunch rush let go of him, Sami came through the door with the bell and a white box, and Avram turned the sign without being asked and got down the two glasses that were only for this. The game was eleven years old that summer. Not the same game — they finished one every few months, when neither could help it — but the same sitting, the same hour, the same argument about whose play had been whose the week before, which was why, four or five years in, they had each started photographing the board with their phones before leaving it. Not to settle the arguments. To keep having them on solid ground.

"You moved the knight back," Sami said, sitting. "Last week it was on the rim. A knight on the rim is grim, you taught me this, and now look at you."

"The knight is where I left it. What is in the box."

"Ma'amoul. The date ones. Rima says you don't eat enough to insult anybody." Sami opened the box and put it where the captured pieces went, which was an old fight neither bothered having anymore. "My father made these only for the Eid. I make them on a Tuesday because I can. This is a great country."

"It was a great country on the rim of a great country." Avram advanced a pawn he had been advancing in his head since Wednesday. "Play."

The bell went while Sami was thinking. A woman Avram knew by her watch — a Hamilton, her grandmother's, he had put a crystal in it in the spring — leaned in with the door half open and did not come all the way inside, and looked at the two of them at the board a moment too long before she asked, was he open, and could he look at a clasp Tuesday. Avram said Tuesday, yes. She thanked him twice and the door closed and Sami watched her cross the street in the glass.

"She comes to my counter eight years," Sami said. "Friday she asks me where I am from. Eight years she knows where I am from. Friday she needs to hear me say it."

"What did you say."

"I said from Homs. She said she meant before that." Sami moved his bishop without his usual theater. "There is no before that. Before that I was a boy."

Avram looked at the board awhile. Across the street the woman stood at the crossing with her bag held in front of her with both hands, and a city bus went by with its new advertisement, the green one, the two clasped hands and the sentence under them, and the light changed and she stayed where she was, looking at her phone, letting the crossing go.

"Take the bishop back," Avram said. "You moved it angry."

"I moved it fine."

"You moved it angry. I will beat you either way, but slower, with dignity, like neighbors."

⸻

The envelopes came Thursday, in both mailboxes, in the same post.

Avram's granddaughter would have said the envelope looked official-adjacent, which was a way she had of talking. Heavy paper. A seal that was not a state seal but stood next to where a state seal would stand, two clasped hands inside a circle of stars, and under the cellophane window his own name spelled out entire — AVRAM Y. MIZRAHI — the Y a thing no form had asked him for in forty years.

Dalia came by the shop after the pharmacy closed and read it at his workbench with her coat still on. She read fast, the way she read inserts and labels, for the line that mattered.

"Origin declaration," she said. "Ancestry, faith, language spoken at home, community of belonging. Belonging." She turned the page over. "Registration opens the first. Sixty days. It says assignments to follow."

"Assignments."

"That's the word on the paper, Abba."

"In my shop I assign. A movement to a case. A part to a drawer." He had the loupe still down over one eye, and he took it off and folded it and set it on the bench, and aligned it with the edge of the bench, and his daughter watched him do it. "A man fills out one form like this in his life, and then for the rest of his life he is filling out the same form. Your mother filled one. In '79. One."

"Eli's school sent a notice. They're asking the kids to bring them in filled out. Like permission slips." Dalia put the page down. "Tomorrow I'm booking the tickets. Tel Aviv direct, all four of us, before the first. I have it from Sarit there's nothing on the airline side yet, no flag, you walk on like a person. Sixty days from now, who knows."

"So go."

"All four of us, Abba."

"I have watches in the drawers that belong to people," Avram said. "A shop does not walk on like a person."

He said it mild, and he picked the loupe back up to say the discussion was a clasp and the clasp was closed. Dalia stood there with her coat on. Over on the little table by the window the board sat mid-game under the lamp, white slightly better, the date cookies gone, and she was forty-six years old and had grown up over a different shop in a different country he had also, at one time, declined to leave until her mother had made the case in the one way that could not be argued with, and she looked at the board and the two chairs and did not say any of that.

"Book yours," he said, not looking up. "Take Eli's mother her tea Sunday, she likes you. And bring me the boy Thursday, his watch runs fast, it embarrasses the family."

⸻

Saturday Sami studied the position longer than usual, the box of ma'amoul unopened, and finally moved the knight Avram had been quietly building a future for, and took the photograph after, one-handed, the flash on by mistake so the kings threw shadows.

"Rima's brother says we should be photographed less," Sami said, putting the phone away. "He says it like a man who knows. Everything with him now is what they will do with the lists. He has theories, his theories have theories." He turned the box around so it faced Avram. "I told him: I am on every list since I am sixteen. Aleppo list, Amman list, the UNHCR, the IRS. A man like me is a list with feet. One more list."

"One more list," Avram said.

He said it the way he priced a repair he didn't want to do, and moved his rook to the open file, and neither of them said anything else about it, because it was one o'clock on a Saturday, and the glasses were down, and there was a game on.

## 6

They held registration at the middle school, in the gym, and out front on the brown grass somebody had set up a bounce house.

It was the first thing Desmond saw from the parking lot and he stood by the truck a second looking at it. Red and yellow, a generator huffing beside it, three kids already inside going up and down. A banner over the door said FAMILY RESOURCE FAIR and a smaller one under it said REGISTRATION THIS WAY, and the arrow for both was the same arrow.

"There's a bounce house," Della said.

"I see it."

"Okay." She said it the way she said okay to a third-grader's answer that was wrong in a way she'd deal with later. "Nia, you stay where one of us can see you."

The line went out the gym doors and along the building in the shade, and the line was the neighborhood. Walt Pruitt up ahead in a tucked-in golf shirt. The Okafors near the front, all four of them dressed like church. Folding tables just inside the doors, A–K and L–Z, lanyards everywhere, and young people in green vests walking the line with a cooler, handing out water bottles like it was a turkey trot.

"Sir? Hydration?" The vest was maybe twenty, freckles, earnest as a golden retriever.

"I'm fine."

"It's no trouble. We want everybody comfortable."

"I know you do," Desmond said, and took the bottle so the kid would move on, and held it unopened the length of the line.

At the front of it, Emeka Okafor had his folder. Desmond had seen the folder at the HOA meeting, a portfolio thing with a zipper, and at the table Emeka unzipped it and laid his form down with the naturalization certificate stapled to the back like an exhibit, and said something Desmond couldn't hear but could read fine from the way the man stood — chin level, presenting it. The woman at the table smiled at the certificate the way you smile at a child's drawing of the house, and unstapled it, and handed it back.

The Kims went through two tables over in under four minutes. Howard had everything in a clear plastic sleeve, filled out in block letters, and he answered each question before the table finished asking it, with the section number. Grace stood behind him with her hands folded and her eyes on the middle distance and Min slouched at the end of the table, fifteen and made of elbows, and when the woman asked the last question and typed it in, Howard thanked her by her first name, off the lanyard.

Then it was Desmond, and Della beside him, and Nia between them with her library book.

The man at A–K was sixty-something, retired-teacher glasses, a volunteer doing his level best inside a system he hadn't read all of. He went down the form. Names, prior address, current address. Faith — Della said Baptist, lapsed, and the man typed Baptist. Language at home. Then he got to it.

"Community of belonging," the man read, and looked up, apologetic, like the form had used a fork it shouldn't. "They want — it's how they're organizing the assistance, is how it was explained to us."

"African-American," Desmond said. "Both of us. Her too."

Chidi turned nine on a Saturday, and by Okafor family law that meant the oath.

It came after the cake and before the presents, in that order, always, because — Emeka's words, every year — a person should know what they have before they see what they get. He sat down. Ada put a paper crown on his head. That was part of it now too; nobody remembered when that had started.

"Presents," Chidi said.

The second Saturday of June the Reyes family ate on the river, the way the Reyes family ate on the river.

The spot was theirs the way the date was theirs, a flat place under the ebanos upstream of the old pump station, and nobody owned it on paper, which in that family was the joke that wasn't one. "That's what I said."

## 11

The new rule came down the way the real rules did, not on the radio but at the fuel rack: Hobbs in Decatur wouldn't sell avgas without a tail number and a registry card now, and Hobbs apologized about it to every man he carded, which meant Hobbs was being watched too.

Ray bought four drums of car gas off a man in Bowie instead and blended it himself, did the math on the octane twice, and flew test laps over his own section line listening for the knock that didn't come. The Seal had a date now. October one. They'd said it on the noon broadcast in the school-nurse voice, *general aviation operations consolidated for the safety of all communities*, and at strips up and down the dry side of the metroplex, men who had not voted in twenty years stood in hangar doors doing arithmetic about what consolidated meant for a man with a deadline and an airplane.

What it meant for Ray was demand.

He was flying three nights a week now and turning work away, and the work had changed its clothes. In the spring it had been people like the Nguyens — savings, a plan on the far end, a brother in Toronto with a spare room. Now it was people with the plan half-made or not made, gold they'd gotten for a truck that had been worth twice the gold, asking him questions about Mexico like he was Mexico. He kept the price where the price was. A man who cuts his price in a falling market is advertising something, and what he's advertising is that he's about to stop showing up.

The motel went into the rotation in September, and it went in because of the diesel.

Beto's cousin ran fuel up from Saltillo, but the road had gotten expensive in the new way, and a man at a strip needed a place on the north side that could hold drums without it being anybody's business. The Wayfarer had a maintenance shed, a dead pool taking up insurance weight, and an owner who understood at a glance what was being asked, priced it, and wrote it down. LONG-STAY, RM 2, the ledger said. PAYS DIESEL.

The wife — Asha — had given up asking him to dinner by the third week and started leaving a covered plate on the air conditioner outside his door instead, no note, no transaction, and the first night he stood looking at the plate awhile like it might have a hook in it.

He watched the place while he pretended not to. The towel count. The marigolds. It was the kid from 9 that broke the rule.

The trucker's boy. Eight, nine, the family eating through the last of something while the father called about the impounded rig from the lobby phone every day at one like a man visiting a grave. The boy had found the dead pool, of course, and made it a fort, of course, and one Thursday Ray came out at dawn to run his engine and the boy was sitting on the diving board in the cold with a toy airplane, a die-cast thing with the paint worn off the wings, flying it in slow circles over the empty deep end with the engine sound boys make low in their throats when they think nobody's listening.

Ray stood by his truck with his thermos and watched longer than he had business to.

That night the father knocked on 2. Hat literally in his hands, which Ray had read about all his life and never once seen a man actually do.

"They say you fly," the father said. "Out."

"Who says."

"Nobody. The walls." He turned the hat. "I got maybe sixty cents on the dollar coming for the rig if the auction's honest, which. And Karen's sister is in Costa Rica, legal, fifteen years, she'll sponsor us. I'm asking what it runs. That's all I'm asking tonight, what it runs."

The number was eleven thousand and Ray had said it that week to three sets of strangers without his pulse moving. He looked at this one — the laundry-soap smell of him, the boy's silhouette over in 9's window behind the curtain with the airplane still in his hand, doing slow rolls against the lamp — and the number wouldn't load.

"I don't fly people I eat breakfast with," Ray said.

The man blinked. "We never ate—"

"Wednesday. The wife had the waffle iron out in the lobby." Ray had taken his to the room, but he'd taken it, and the man's boy had carried it to him across the lot two-handed, level as a surgeon, proud of every step. "That's breakfast."

The father stood there working it, polite, lost, a man getting turned down for a thing he couldn't afford by a reason he couldn't see. Ray let the silence sit until it taught instead of insulted.

"Auction's Tuesday," Ray said finally. "Take the sixty cents. Then go talk to the owner here about the schoolteacher's husband, who has a van and paper that's still good, and a route worked out through Laredo he thinks nobody knows he's working out. Ground's slower. Ground takes families of four." He looked past the man at the lit curtain, the little plane banking through the lamplight. "Costa Rica will still be there. Place is famous for it."

He shut the door easy. Then he stood in room 2 with his back against it, in the bluebonnet light off the parking lot, doing the one piece of arithmetic that had never once come out even in his life, and the next morning the plate on the air conditioner had pie on it next to the eggs, which meant the walls talked in both directions, and he ate it standing up, looking at the dead pool, and didn't run his engine that day at all.

## 12

Every fall since her second year teaching, Della had her third-graders draw their street.

It was the first art project of the year and secretly the first assessment — you learned who held a pencil like a friend and who held it like a court summons, who lived with grandparents, who drew the dog bigger than the house because the dog was the house. The maps went up over the cubbies and stayed up all year, a paper neighborhood above the real one, and in June she'd send them home and the mothers would keep them forever.

This October she pinned up nineteen.

Last October it had been twenty-six. The difference wasn't down some hallway in a number on a roster page — it was specific, it had faces, it had hooks. Marisol's backpack hook, third from the door, still had its laminated name tag because Marisol had left on a Saturday with the buses and tags came down at year-end, that was the system, and Della was not going to invent a new system in October for taking down a child's name early. So the tag stayed. Kids hung things on the other hooks. Nobody hung anything on Marisol's, all year, without one word ever being said about it, because children have a liturgy nobody teaches them.

The maps this year had new vocabulary.

DeShawn drew his street the way he always drew it, but with a coach bus on it, copied careful, the little toilet window and all, because his cousins' street had gotten buses and DeShawn was eight and buses were interesting. Katie B. drew her cul-de-sac with Walt Pruitt's floodlights as six yellow suns. And quiet Ethan, who held the pencil like a friend, drew his house and his grass and at the foot of his driveway, small and neat and white, a number — 9-A — because it was there, so you drew it, the way you drew the mailbox.

Della hung them all. You hang them all; that's the rule of the wall.

⸻

The substitute pool had thinned with everything else — Ms. Okonkwo gone east with her street in September, Mr. Reyes-no-relation gone south, the science aide just gone — so when the office needed Della's class covered for twenty minutes on a Thursday, the office sent the assistant principal, and the assistant principal brought a folder instead of a lesson.

Form 12-E. One sheet per child. STUDENT COMMUNITY CONSISTENCY VERIFICATION across the top, the clasped hands in the corner, and a memo clipped on saying instructional staff, having daily contact, were *well positioned to confirm that each student's documented community of belonging remains consistent with observed indicators*, and there was a column for the indicator, and a column for CONSISTENT Y/N, and a deadline.

Della read it twice at her desk while nineteen kids did silent reading that was eleven percent silent.

She thought about Mr. Castle's slipper. She thought about Howard Kim on her porch clicking his pen. She thought, longer than anything, about what an *observed indicator* was on a person eight years old — what they wore, what they brought in their lunch, what language their mother used at pickup, which holidays they drew pumpkins for — and she took out her own pen, and in the community column, on every sheet, in her best board-printing, the printing parents could read across a gym, she wrote the same thing nineteen times.

THIRD GRADE.

Consistent: Y. Nineteen times. She signed every page, dated every page, clipped the memo back on top, and returned the folder to the office before lunch, and if anyone ever asked she was prepared to say, in her teacher voice, the one that had outlasted two lockdowns and a thousand fire drills, that it was the only community she had personally observed.

Nobody ever asked. The Office's systems had a box for refusal and a box for compliance, but nothing yet for a woman complying in a way that broke the column, and by the time anyone built one, the file was where files go.

⸻

At 2:15, like every day, she read aloud.

It was the oldest thing in her room — the chair, the rug, the book with the spine gone soft — and she had defended that twenty minutes against three principals and one pandemic and she defended it now against the hour itself, because at 2:15 the announcements liked to come on with their bright orderly voice, and Della had learned to start reading at 2:14.

Nineteen came to the rug. They still came like it was water in a drought, even the big tough ones, especially the big tough ones. Ethan sat where Marisol used to sit, not because anyone assigned it, the way water finds a level. Out the window the live oak had let go of nothing yet; the buses — the yellow ones, the old kind, the kind that just took you home — idled in their line like nothing anywhere had changed.

Della opened the book to the dog-eared page and read, and did the voices, all of them, the way she had for nineteen years, and for twenty minutes the count in the room held steady at twenty, and nobody was pending anything.

## 13

The Saturday before Halloween, Nia ran the checklist with her breath showing.

"Oil."

"Oil's good."

"Coolant."

"Good."

"Belts." She had the card two-handed against the cold. The card had grown over the fall — the original column in Desmond's print, and then additions down the margin in different inks, like sediment you could read the season in. CASH BOX. WATER BOX. The new ones at the bottom: SPARE FUEL ×3. MAPS — PAPER. RADIO + BATT. And one in pencil, lighter than the others, that he hadn't written and hadn't erased: SNACKS THAT KEEP.

"Belt's got a year in it," Desmond said. "Maybe two."

"You said that in June."

"It was true in June."

She looked at him over the fender, weighing whether that was an answer or a dodge, and filed it wherever she filed things now. The notebook had come outside with her lately — a marbled composition book gone fat with use, rubber-banded, tabs of torn paper sticking out the side in three colors. She set it on the air cleaner while she worked the card, and Desmond, reaching past it for the dipstick, read the cover because it was facing him. THE BOOK OF HOW THINGS GO, it said, in her serious print. He looked at it the time it takes a man to decide something, and did not open it, then or ever, and was never sure afterward whether that had been respect or fear.

"Tires," Nia said.

"Tires," he said, and got the gauge.

⸻

They held the conference that night because the letter that morning made it three.

Three letters now, same heavy paper, same circle: determination in process, do not relocate, thank you for remaining where services can reach you. Same sentences in the same order, like the page wasn't written but poured. Della set the third one on the kitchen table next to the first two after Nia went up, and the two of them sat down with the overhead light on, which between them had always meant business; the porch was for the other thing.

Desmond took the legal pad and drew his lines.

It had been years since Della had watched him do this with anything that mattered — the columns, the small caps, the way his hand steadied once the page had structure, a man laying conduit through a swamp. STAY, the first column said. The case for it filled four lines: the house. The job, what currently existed of the job. The letter's instruction, for what a letter's instruction was worth. Schools — he wrote it and looked at it, and across the table Della said, "I had twenty-six in August. I have nineteen. Two more leave Friday," in the voice she used for attendance, which was how she could say it at all, and he drew a line through SCHOOLS, one line, level, like closing a valve.

"The letter's the real problem," he said. "Not what it says. What it doesn't bound. 'In process' has no end date. A loop with no exit condition isn't a wait, Dell. It's a halt that hasn't been admitted yet."

"In English, baby."

"Nobody's coming to tell us when. Because there is no when. The system that's supposed to produce the when —" he turned the pen over, set it down "— I don't think it terminates. I think PENDING is the determination."

The second column said VISA. That one was short. The exit-visa office had a name now and a building and a line around the building, and what came out of it, the walls said — and the walls had gotten reliable as the channels got bright — was appointments. Appointments for interviews for applications for permits, the loop again wearing a different lanyard, and PENDING files, the walls said, didn't even get the appointment. He wrote DENIED BY DESIGN in the column and boxed it, not because it needed a box but because his hand wanted one.

The third column he headed GROUND, and under it: LAREDO ROUTE — SLOW. CHECKPOINTS ×9 LAST KNOWN. FAMILIES OF 4 — and then, after it, the thing that had actually moved, the reason the conference was tonight and not some night: WAITS UNKNOWN. It had come through the teachers — the group chat that had outlived the district that built it and was now the most reliable newspaper in three counties — a teacher who ran a homework table at a motel out west of town had a husband, and the husband had a van, and that was a sentence you could no longer finish out loud anywhere with a wall, but the end of it was: full through January. The ground was filling up. Slow things fill from the back.

Which left the fourth column, and the fourth column had no heading yet, and one item.

In September, Marcus Whitfield — fourteen years two cubicles over, the only other man in the building who'd cared whether the failovers actually failed over — had called him from a number with no state on it, talked about nothing for four minutes in a voice doing its best, and then said, the way you'd hand somebody a tool you were done with: *If it ever gets to where you need it to be somewhere else fast — there's a man flies out of a strip past Bowie. Cole. Tell him the Nguyens sent you. He'll act like that means nothing. It means he'll listen.*

Desmond had written nothing down that night. He'd been writing it down in the back of his head for six weeks.

Now he took a notecard from the drawer where the notecards had always lived, through every house, every state, every version of their lives — Della's drawer, really, recipe stock, unlined — and he printed on it, in the small caps the legal pad got:

R. COLE — STRIP PAST BOWIE — "THE NGUYENS"

and under that, after a moment, the only number that mattered, the one with the date on it: BEFORE OCT 1.

Della read it upside down, the way her daughter read everything.

"That's eleven days," she said.

"That's eleven days to decide. Not eleven days to go." He squared the card to the edge of the table. "There's a difference. I want the option live, that's all this is. You build the failover before the outage. You build it and you test it and you hope it rusts."

"And if it doesn't rust?"

He didn't answer that one, and she didn't make him, because twenty years will teach you which silences are load-bearing. She got up instead and opened the cabinet door, the one by the stove, where the household kept its true documents — the recipe card in careful English block letters, FOR THE SPINACH, IF YOUR FAMILY LIKES, taped where it had been taped since summer like a flag of a country with two citizens — and she took the magnet off the tin and put the notecard up under it, COLE facing out, the Kims' spinach facing out, side by side, the whole foreign policy of the house on one cabinet door.

Then she put the kettle on, because it had gotten too cold for the porch to be an accident anymore; the porch was now a decision, and they made it anyway, every night that week.

⸻

Outside, the cold had brought the stars down close. Della had the blanket over both their laps and both mugs were full and steaming and hers was on the rail and his was on the board beside him, and for a while the only sound was the neighborhood doing its small remaining things — a garage door, a dog two streets over, the Kims' kitchen window going dark at ten exactly, the way it went dark at ten exactly every night now, like a lighthouse running in reverse.

"Fifteen minutes," Della said, not a question, her foot finding his knee under the blanket.

"Fifteen minutes."

Up the street Walt's floodlights burned their cold lane across the entrance, and past them, eight hundred miles south by a route with nine known checkpoints, the river ran through the legs of sleeping birds; and out west a man they had never met slept in a room with bluebonnets over the bed and a plate outside the door; and on a cabinet door in the kitchen behind them a notecard and a recipe hung side by side under one magnet, the option and the reason, and neither Desmond nor Della said one word more until the fifteen minutes were over, and then they said five more.
