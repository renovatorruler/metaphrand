## The flattened chapter

The system wanted a number Mrs. Adeyemi did not have, and Thanh Nguyen had been staring at the field for most of a minute.

"It's the update," he said. "They put a new field in. It won't take blank."

"What number is it asking for?"

"It doesn't say." He turned the screen so she could see the empty field. It had a name made of initials, and a red asterisk. He had been filling her blood-pressure pills for twenty-one years. He keyed back out, took a stock bottle off the shelf behind him, counted fourteen days of tablets into a vial by fives, the tray clicking, and pushed the vial across with the label blank.

"That's a sample," he said. "Samples don't go in the computer. By the time you're through them, the field will have a help line."

He rang up her cough drops so she'd have a receipt for the walk back, and watched her out the door. Then he stood a while looking at the pickup shelf, at the rows of stapled white bags, each one with a family's name on it in his own block letters.

The radio on the back counter was running the noon update, turned low. Registration would open statewide the first of the month, the warm voice said. Everyone counted, so everyone's cared for.

Hoa called at two with the question she asked once a week.

"Binh says the hospital will sponsor. He says his attending did it for a family from Laos, four people, eight weeks start to finish." Somewhere behind her a tap ran and shut off. "Eight weeks, Thanh. If we filed now."

"Who fills Mrs. Adeyemi on the fifteenth?"

"Walgreens fills her."

"Walgreens' computer is the same computer." He said it gently, and not for the first time. Through the window, the parking lot sat empty in the heat. "We'll talk at dinner."

"We'll talk at dinner," she said, and hung up first.

Dr. Mehta came in at ten to six. Tuesdays he was at the hospital, and he never came himself anyway; his prescriptions arrived by fax. He stood at the counter in a golf shirt, and out front, past the glass, a small dog sat up in the basket of a girl's bicycle with its ears up.

"Thanh," he said. "I find myself with a free afternoon."

"On a Tuesday."

"On all the Tuesdays." Dr. Mehta straightened the counter mat with one finger. "There was an audit this morning. A category audit. My privileges are suspended pending —" he turned his hand over "— pending."

Neither of them said anything for a moment.

"Priya's brother drove to the airport Friday," Dr. Mehta said. "He had visas, tickets, everything stamped. They boarded him back off at the gate, and the paper gives no reason. It says see addendum, and there is no addendum." He kept his voice level, and it mostly held. "You said once — last winter, you said a thing to me. About a man with an airplane."

Thanh came around the counter, went to the door, and looked at the lot — the girl and the bicycle and the dog, the two parked cars, the road. He flipped the sign to CLOSED and came back.

"You were never in today," he said.

"I was never in."

"There's a strip past Bowie." He said a phone number once. "Say it back."

Dr. Mehta said it back. Thanh nodded and did not say it again, and wrote nothing down, and the doctor didn't write it down either. They stood a moment in the smell of vitamins.

"He prices in gold," Thanh said. "If you bargain, he subtracts. And he'll want a place, not a country — have the place before you call." He went down the second aisle and came back with a small box and put it in the doctor's hand. "For the girl. Half a tablet, twenty minutes before. It's a small airplane."

Dr. Mehta looked at the box of motion-sickness tablets, his thumb moving once across it.

"I would want to pay something," he said. "For the introduction."

"You'd pay him. There's nothing to pay me for. I wasn't in today, and I didn't do anything, and you'll remember that I didn't." Thanh said it kindly. "Toronto?"

"My sister."

"Eat at her place a year before you open anything." He unlocked the door. "Doctor. The girl takes the dog, or the girl learns the word for what happens to it. There's no third thing in between, whatever Priya is hoping."

The bell went. The bicycle wobbled off the curb with the dog in the basket, and Thanh turned the sign back and stood at his counter with the lights buzzing.

At dinner Hoa put Binh on speaker while she served. The boy's voice came out of the phone bright and tired. Eight weeks, he said, maybe ten now; the forms kept changing but the attending knew the new forms.

Thanh ate and said the food was good. Across the table his wife watched him and didn't ask. The pickup shelf stood in his mind, the stapled bags with every name in his own square hand. Forty years ago, in a room in Songkhla, somebody had said a number out loud once, and it was the reason his family existed.

"Ten weeks is fast," he said to the phone. "That's good news, Binh. Tell me again after the fifteenth."

Hoa set the serving spoon down with no sound at all.
