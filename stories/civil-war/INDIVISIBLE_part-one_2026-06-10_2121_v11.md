## 1

Desmond set the axe to the log again and missed the split again, and the wood rolled off the block into the dirt.

"Nia," he said, not looking up. "Water."

"I watered." Her voice came from the lean-to, where she had the bay's big head down in both hands and was feeding it oats out of her apron pocket a few at a time, making it work for them.

"You watered the one you like. Troughs mean all three."

She gave the bay the last of the oats and went for the well bucket with the put-upon walk she'd carried since she was small, and on her way past the rail she touched the saddle that sat there, the dark tooled leather with silver let into the cantle, the way you'd touch a thing in a store you'd been told twice not to handle. She had it drawn in her notebook already and had gotten the fork of it wrong, and had written under the drawing the question nobody downstairs would answer: WHOSE.

In the side yard Della had the wash pot going over a low fire, boiling her good linen on a Tuesday, stirring with the dolly stick, the lye smell flat on the heat.

"You're chopping it like it owes you money," Nia said from the well.

"Go on and pour."

"Mama does it better than you."

"Pour, Nia."

They kept their rules: nobody past the tree line alone, no lamp in the window after dark, somebody on the road from first light to last.

Nia saw the dust first. She didn't shout, just said "Daddy" and lifted her chin to where the track met the county road, where something kicked up a haze with no wind to do it, and above Desmond a window slid in its frame before he'd turned all the way around. Della's rifle came to rest on the sill like it was being set in a rack built for it. He sent the girl up the stairs with a look and didn't watch her go, and heard the second window go up, the girl at it.

He leaned the axe against the block like a man who meant to get back to it, and stood in the yard with his hands where they showed.

The riders came up the track easy, in no hurry. They were four men as dark as he was, dusty and well-mounted, and they carried their rifles across their laps, oilcloth slickers rolled and tied behind the cantles, riding government leather on horses too good for field work. The one in front wore broadcloth gone green at the seams and a low crowned hat, and he walked his horse near to the porch before he stopped it and looked Desmond over, the homespun gone soft as paper, the brogans split at the welt, and smiled like the inventory pleased him. The oldest of them stopped well back by the gate post and didn't look at the house at all. He looked at the tree line, and at the wind in it, and then at the upstairs windows, in that order, and after that he sat still.

"Which plantation you run off from?" the front one said.

"Don't know what you mean."

"No?" He hung his hands on the horn, comfortable. "We're after a nigger come through here three, four days back. Tall, took a ball in the leg above the knee. Would've looked like he needed a place to lie up." He drew a paper from inside his coat and held it up unfolded, a printed thing, slick as a playing card and shining where the light got it. "Recovery paper, all lawful. Man that gives him up holds forty dollars gold. Word of him alone is worth ten."

"Nobody comes out this far."

"Somebody did." He looked the place over while he said it, the house, the well, the lean-to. "Bring your wife on out. The girl too. We'll all of us talk about it."

"They got nothing to tell you I can't."

"Wasn't a request."

One of the others got down off his horse and started for the porch, taking his time about it, a big man eating sunflower seeds out of his palm. Desmond stepped into his way, not fast, just enough to stand between him and the door, and the big man stopped and looked back — past the talker, all the way back to the old one at the gate post, who hadn't said a word yet. Desmond marked where the look went.

"There's no man here," Desmond said, to the gate post. "Ride north, you'll cut his trail at the river. I wouldn't waste your daylight on us."

The old one sat his horse and chewed on it, looking at the house and the empty road north, and Desmond watched him weigh a houseful of women and a yard full of work against a lamed stranger and come up short.

"Mount up," the old one said.

The big man spat a hull at Desmond's feet and turned for his horse.

They were bringing the horses around when the young one at the back went still. He was looking past the lean-to at the bay standing easy in the shade, and at the saddle on the rail beside her, the silver in the cantle throwing light, worth more than the house and everything in it.

"That's his saddle," the young one said, rising in his stirrups. "That's the—"

Della fired once from the upstairs window and took him off the horse with the sentence unfinished.

After that it went fast. The big man was already swinging his rifle up — he had started the swing before her shot, some cold corner of Desmond saw that and kept it — and Desmond pulled the pistol from the small of his back and put him down on the second shot. The second window opened up over him, the girl firing slow and level the way she'd been taught, and the yard filled with wheeling horses that couldn't turn in the close of it. The talker took one high in the shoulder, held on somehow, and got himself out low on his horse's neck. The old one was a length ahead of him going down the track, flat to the mane, and Desmond couldn't have said when the man started — only that nobody had been shooting at him yet when he did.

Desmond stood with the pistol up until there was nothing left to point it at. The young one lay where the yard's one piece of shade came up short, seventeen years old, maybe, and right, was all, half a second too loud about it.

Desmond went inside and up the stairs. Della was thumbing rounds back into the rifle, the window still open, and she looked at his face once and went back to the work.

"I had them leaving," he said.

"You had them turning." She seated the last round. "The big one started his swing before I fired. The old one was gone before anybody touched a trigger. Look again."

He played it back: the rein hand drifting while the talker talked, and the old man who'd looked at the wind and the windows and never once at him, pricing the place the whole time the front man kept him busy being brave.

"Two got out," Della said. "The old one's blood kin to that boy in the yard, or I've never watched a man not look at something. We have an hour. Maybe two."

"Pack what you can carry," Desmond said. "Ten minutes."

Della was already past him with the rifle slung, calling the girl down off the landing. Desmond stood one moment more at the window, looking at the lean-to, where the bay stood easy in the shade wearing a dead boy's worth of silver.

Ten minutes was not going to be enough.

## 2

*Eighteen months earlier.*

The realtor's name was Brad, and he had a way of announcing every room like it was the best one in the house.

"Now this," Brad said, "is your bonus room. Media room, home gym, man cave — whatever speaks to you."

What spoke to Desmond was the window. One window, street side, and past it the cul-de-sac curled back on itself with a single way in and out. He stood there working it the way he worked everything — where's the door, where's the second door, what happens when the first one fails.

"You're doing the thing," Della said, beside him, smiling out at the yard so Brad wouldn't catch her not smiling at her husband.

"I'm looking at the house."

"You're looking at the exits."

"One road in, Dell. One road in is one road out." He kept it low. "I keep telling you. We should be looking at land. Couple acres east of here, some space around us, something we can actually—"

"A compound." She almost laughed. "You want to raise our daughter in a compound."

"I want to raise our daughter."

"Desmond." She looked at him then, still smiling. "It's going to be okay. People have been calling the end of the country since before there was a country. We're going to buy a house, gripe about the HOA, complain about the heat after thirty years of St. Louis winters, and be fine."

"Schools out here are phenomenal, by the way," Brad said from the hall.

"See?" Della said. "Phenomenal schools."

Desmond looked back out the one window.

Outside, Nia found the only other kids on the street.

They were in the next yard over, a boy and a smaller girl, running something back and forth that took a lot of shrieking. The boy looked about her age. Nia went to the chain-link, hooked her fingers in it, and watched, waiting to be noticed. The smaller girl noticed first and stopped, and then the boy stopped.

Then the screen door banged and the mother came out fast, talking, and behind her, inside the house, a thin old voice was calling one name, the same name, twice. The talk wasn't for Nia. It went over her head in a language she didn't have one word of. She didn't need the words. She had the face, and the way the woman caught the little girl by the wrist and turned her with it, and the way she put herself between her kids and the fence without once looking at what was on the other side of it.

Nia took her fingers out of the chain-link.

She knew it without the words. She'd known it without the words before.

She was almost back to her own yard when she heard the gate.

The boy had come through it. He stood a second like he wasn't sure why, then crossed the grass with his hands in his pockets and stopped a careful few feet off.

"She's not always like that," he said. "She's like that since my halmoni came."

"It's okay."

"It's not, though." He looked back at his house, then at her. "You want to see something? There's a frog living in the storm drain by the hydrant. He's the size of a softball."

Nia looked at him.

"I'm serious," the boy said. "He's enormous."

"Okay," Nia said, and went with him.

## 3

The system wanted a number Mrs. Adeyemi did not have, and Thanh Nguyen had been staring at the field for most of a minute.

"It's the update," he said. "They put a new field in. It won't take blank."

"What number is it asking for?"

"It doesn't say." He turned the screen so she could see it not saying. The field had a name made of initials, and a red asterisk, and twenty-one years of filling her blood-pressure pills stood on one side of it and the refill stood on the other. Thanh keyed back out, took a stock bottle off the shelf behind him, counted fourteen days of tablets into a vial by fives, the tray clicking, and pushed the vial across with the label blank.

"That's a sample," he said. "Samples don't go in the computer. By the time you're through them, the field will have a help line."

He rang up her cough drops so she'd have a receipt for the walk back, and watched her out the door, and then he stood a while looking at the shelf of will-call bags, stapled shut and patient, each one with a family's name on it in his own block letters.

The radio on the back counter, low, was running the noon update. Registration would open statewide the first of the month, the warm voice said. Everyone counted, so everyone's cared for.

Hoa called at two, which was her time, with the question she asked once a week the way other people went to church.

"Binh says the hospital will sponsor. He says his attending did it for a family from Laos, four people, eight weeks start to finish." A pause that had a kitchen in it, a tap running and shutting off. "Eight weeks, Thanh. If we filed now."

"Who fills Mrs. Okafor on the fifteenth?"

"Walgreens fills her."

"Walgreens' computer is the same computer." He said it gently, and not for the first time, and through the window he watched the parking lot do nothing in the heat. "We'll talk at dinner."

"We'll talk at dinner," she said, in the voice of a woman filing the case for next week, and was kind enough to hang up first.

Dr. Mehta came in at ten to six, which was wrong twice — wrong because Tuesdays were his hospital days, and wrong because the man never came himself; his scripts arrived by fax with a signature like a cardiogram. He stood at the counter in a golf shirt that had never been worn to golf, and out front, past the glass, a small dog sat up in the basket of a girl's bicycle with its ears up like a captain.

"Thanh," he said. "I find myself with a free afternoon."

"On a Tuesday."

"On all the Tuesdays." Dr. Mehta straightened the counter mat with one finger. "There was an audit this morning. A category audit. My privileges are suspended pending — " he turned his hand over " — pending."

The word sat on the counter between them like a thing that had crawled up there.

"Priya's brother drove to the airport Friday with tickets," Dr. Mehta said. "Visas, tickets, everything stamped. They boarded him back off at the gate. No reason given on the paper. The paper says see addendum, and there is no addendum." He was working to keep it in the contractual voice, the voice that had read labs to frightened men for thirty years, and it mostly held. "You said once — last winter, you said a thing to me. About a man with an airplane."

Thanh came around the counter, went to the door, and stood looking at the lot a moment — at the girl and the bicycle and the dog, at the two parked cars, at the road. Then he flipped the sign to CLOSED and came back.

"You were never in today," he said.

"I was never in."

"There's a strip past Bowie." He said a number then, a phone number, once, at the speed a man says his own. "Say it back."

Dr. Mehta said it back. Thanh nodded and did not say it again, and wrote nothing down, and watched the doctor not write it down either, the two of them standing in the smell of vitamins committing a phone number the old way, like men their age memorizing a girl's address in 1985.

"He prices in gold," Thanh said. "Don't bargain. He doesn't bargain, he subtracts. And he'll want a place, not a country — have the place before you call." He went down the second aisle and came back with a small box and put it in the doctor's hand. "For the girl. Half a tablet, twenty minutes before. It's a small airplane."

Dr. Mehta looked at the box of motion-sickness tablets a long moment, his thumb moving once across it.

"I would want to pay something," he said. "For the introduction."

"You'd pay him. There's nothing to pay me for. I wasn't in today, and I didn't do anything, and you'll remember that I didn't." Thanh said it kindly, the way he said take with food. "Toronto?"

"My sister."

"Eat at her place a year before you open anything." He unlocked the door. "Doctor. The girl takes the dog, or the girl learns the word for what happens to it. There's no third thing in between, whatever Priya is hoping."

The bell went, and the doctor was a customer leaving, and the bicycle wobbled off the curb with its captain riding high, and Thanh turned the sign back and stood at his counter with the lights buzzing.

At dinner Hoa put Binh on speaker while she served, the boy's voice coming out of the phone bright and far, resident-tired, full of a city where the word pending was something you did to a file. Eight weeks, he said. Maybe ten now. The forms change but the attending knows the new forms.

Thanh ate, and said the food was good, and it was. Across the table his wife watched him the way she'd watched him for thirty years, adding up what he wasn't saying, and the will-call shelf stood in his mind with its stapled bags, patient, each name in his own square hand, and forty years ago somebody had said a number out loud once in a room in Songkhla and his whole family had happened because of it.

"Ten weeks is fast," he said to the phone. "That's good news, Binh. Tell me again after the fifteenth."

Hoa set the serving spoon down with no sound at all, which was the loudest thing in the house.

## 4

The card reader at the Brookshire's considered Desmond's card longer than machines consider anything, and then the register girl's lane light was blinking and a manager was coming up the line with a roll of tape and a hand-lettered sign that said CASH ONLY — CARD SYSTEM DOWN, and the Saturday got quiet in a way a full store should not be able to get.

Desmond left his cart with the girl, walked to the truck, and came back with the envelope from under the seat rail, the one Della called his weather money, counted and banded and three months old. He paid out of it flat-faced while the line watched him do it, and the dent it left in the envelope was a number he'd be carrying the rest of the day. Half the list was still housewarming — felt pads for the chair legs, a hose splitter — and the other half had written itself that morning when the radio said the word holiday.

The man behind him paid for two hundred dollars of groceries with a roll of twenties rubber-banded like a fist, and nobody in the line found that strange, and Desmond noted that nobody found it strange.

"Bank holiday," the man said to the air, friendly. "That's what they're calling it. A holiday."

On his way out the aisles were filling behind him, people buying the way people buy ahead of a hurricane that isn't coming, which is wider and angrier than buying ahead of one that is — water and batteries, sure, but also birthday candles and charcoal, and four carts of bottled tea going by like a parade float, because nobody knew what to be afraid of yet and so they were afraid of everything in the store.

Desmond loaded the truck and sat in the lot a minute doing the quiet arithmetic. Two weeks of food was now three days of crowd away from being no food. The grid had hiccuped twice that month, June-style heat coming, and the generator he'd priced in May was four hundred dollars more in June if you could find one, and you could not find one.

Except there was one. A pawn shop out toward Lewisville had a tri-fuel unit on consignment, the listing four days old, which in this market meant the photo was probably of a thing already sold. Desmond called from the parking lot. The man said it was there, nineteen hundred, firm, cash, and no he would not hold it — then listened to Desmond not haggle, and allowed that he'd hold it to Friday for a hundred down, non-refundable, brought today.

Desmond drove to Lewisville with the hundred before he drove home. He looked at the generator, ran it, put his hand on the housing like a man buying a horse, and gave the man the bill. Friday at five, the man said. After that she floats.

He drove home past the gas line forming at the QT, eleven cars at nine in the morning, and counted the line the way other men checked the score, and for once the count had something under it that wasn't dread. He had until Friday. He had a plan with a receipt.

The thing about a porch is that it faces out.

They'd gotten in the habit without calling it one. Somewhere around the second week in the house, after Nia was down, Della would bring out two mugs of the decaf neither one of them liked all that much and they'd sit on the step while the sprinklers came on up the street, hers on the rail, his on the board beside him, fifteen minutes, sometimes not a word in it. The neighborhood ticking itself cool. Some nights her foot ended up against his knee. That was the whole ceremony.

"Janice at school says her husband's putting in a safe room," Della said. It was the Tuesday after the bank holiday. "Concrete. The whole thing."

"Where's he putting it?"

"Garage, I think."

"Then it's a safe room with a water heater in it and one door." He turned the mug a quarter turn on the board. "But fine. People are starting to think about doors. That's something."

"You've been thinking about doors since the open house."

"Since before that, probably."

She looked at him over her mug, and the sprinklers shut off up the street one yard at a time, and she didn't tell him he was wrong, which he noticed, the way you notice the first night of a season.

The bus came apart on a Wednesday, on the far side of the metroplex, and by Thursday everyone in the country had seen the inside of it.

A church group of about forty people had taken a wrong exit and ended up in the wrong neighborhood, and the thing that happened to them there ended up on every screen. Men walked the line of survivors at the shoulder of the road and checked faces against phones. Some they let sit down in the grass, and some they walked toward the underpass, and they were sorting them, on camera, with the brake lights still on.

Nobody reputable said which group the men belonged to. Then everybody said it. By Friday the no-name channels had names and the names didn't match each other, and by Saturday it didn't matter anymore whose names they were, because every street in Texas had picked one and was acting on it.

Desmond watched it once, the whole clip, standing in the kitchen with the sound off. Della came in and watched thirty seconds of it and went out back to where Nia was doing chin-up attempts on the swing set, and stayed out there with her until dinner, teaching her something loud with rules to it.

The second time through, he watched the sorting instead of the violence. The man with the phone had a system — look, check, point — and the line did what lines do, it cooperated, it organized itself against itself, and the clip ran two minutes eleven and not one person in it ran.

A system has a load it's rated for, and a failure mode past it, and the failure mode of a line of people is that it stays a line.

Nia's laugh came in through the window screen, and under it the swing-set chain going like a slow hinge, and he turned the screen dark and stood there until he had his face right.

The lawn signs changed first.

The realtors' signs came down — nothing was for sale now, or everything was but not out loud — and the new ones came up in their place, yard by yard, like a crop coming in. WE LOOK OUT FOR EACH OTHER on a green sign with two clasped hands. Then the other kind, three houses down, black on white, NO ENTRY AFTER DARK — RESIDENTS ONLY, and nobody had voted on either one but there they were, multiplying.

The HOA meeting in June had been about the retention pond. The HOA meeting in August had a sign-in table, two men Desmond didn't know standing at the back with their arms crossed, and an agenda that used the word *perimeter* four times. Walt Pruitt, who in June had wanted everyone fined over trash-can timing, stood up at the front with a laminated map of the subdivision and started talking about *choke points*, and the room — schoolteachers, a dentist, the Nigerian engineer from Birchwood Court with his hand politely up the whole time and never called on — the room nodded along like this was the retention pond again, like this was normal upkeep, assessments and who's on the roster for Thursday nights.

Desmond sat near the back and said nothing the whole hour. He was watching the map. Walt had the entrance circled twice in red, and the thing about a cul-de-sac, the thing he'd said in an empty bonus room a year ago to a wife who'd smiled at him for it, was on that map in red marker for a roomful of neighbors, the worry promoted to an asset.

One road in.

Then Walt got to resources, and two of the men from the back walked a hand truck up the center aisle, and on it, under a ratchet strap, were six generators, boxed and unboxed both — community resilience assets, acquired ahead of the shortage — and the third one back was tri-fuel, scuffed on the left of the housing where it had sat against a pawn-shop wall in Lewisville, the consignment tag still on the handle with one corner torn where a hold slip used to be stapled.

"Sourced the last of them in the county Wednesday," Walt said. "Some folks tried to go it alone on these. That's exactly the thinking we're moving past."

Desmond looked at his generator on Walt's hand truck and did the arithmetic on the hundred dollars, which was gone, and on Friday at five, which had been a fiction since Wednesday, and on the word acquired, and said nothing, in the manner of a man filing a failure report on himself.

In the truck after, Della stared out the windshield at the clubhouse and didn't put her seatbelt on.

"Eight weeks ago that man was measuring people's grass," she said.

"Walt's a vane. He turns whichever way it's blowing." Desmond started the truck. "I'm interested in the wind."

He kept the card in his shirt pocket, written out by hand: OIL · COOLANT · BELTS · BATTERY TERMINALS · TIRES INCL SPARE · FUEL FULL · CASH BOX · WATER BOX. Saturdays, the truck nose-out in the driveway, and Nia would come stand on the bumper in her school shoes and hold her hand out for the card like a surgeon, because the deal was she called it and he touched it, every item, out loud, in order.

"Coolant."

"Coolant's good."

"Belts."

"Belt's got a year in it. Maybe two."

"Battery." She leaned in over the fender next to him, dramatic about it. "Terminals look *corroded*."

"They look fine."

"I'm being thorough. You said thorough."

"I said accurate."

She held the card down at her side then and looked at him across the engine bay, and she had her mother's trick of asking the real question with her whole face level, like it weighed the same as the others.

"Are we getting ready for something?"

"We're keeping the truck ready," Desmond said. "Truck doesn't care what for."

She thought it over. Somewhere down the block the Kim boy was shooting baskets alone, the ball going up over the fence line slow and coming down, and the radio inside the truck was running the top of the hour, the new voice everyone had started recognizing without ever being introduced to it, a woman's voice, warm as a school nurse, saying *community restoration*, saying *everyone counted so everyone's cared for*, and registration opening statewide the first of the month.

"Tires," Nia said.

"Tires," he said, and crouched down at the wheel with the gauge while the voice on the radio thanked everybody in advance for their cooperation.

He was letting air hiss back into the stem when Walt Pruitt came up the driveway with the clipboard, sun visor on at six in the evening, friendly as a coupon.

"Desmond. Putting together the roster for Thursdays. Eight to midnight, two-man shifts — you'd be with Gary." He clicked his pen. "Course, you know the entrance better than anybody. You're the one called it a choke point before I did."

Desmond stood up slow, and Nia stopped pretending to read the card.

"I'll let you know," Desmond said.

"Sign-ups close Sunday." Walt smiled at Nia, tapped the clipboard twice on his thigh, and went back down the drive, and Desmond stood there with the gauge in his hand, listening to the hiss of his own air going back where it came from.

## 5

The woman with the Hamilton came into the bakery on a Friday at her regular time and ordered her regular things, and while Sami boxed them she stood looking at the photograph on the wall behind the register — a man in a doorway, an old shopfront, somewhere with different light.

"Sami," she said. "Where are you from?"

His hands kept working. Eight years she had come to this counter. He had catered her daughter's graduation. He knew her order before the door finished chiming.

"From Homs," Sami said.

"No, I know, but — before that. Originally."

He folded the box flaps in, one over the other, and set it on the counter with the string handle toward her, and when he answered, his English, which ran loose and warm all day long, came out level and exact, each word checked before it shipped.

"There is no before that. Before that I was a boy."

She paid. She thanked him twice, the second one for nothing, and at the door she half turned, like a woman who wanted to leave the question at the counter for store credit, and didn't, and went.

Sami stood a moment with his hands flat on the glass case, over the rows of things his father had made only for weddings and he made on Tuesdays because he could. Then the oven timer went, because it didn't know anything, and he went and pulled the bread.

⸻

The board lived in Avram's shop because the bakery had flour in the air, and a man who has cleaned one escapement of one watch ruined by flour does not need to learn that lesson twice.

Saturdays at one, when the lunch rush let go of him, Sami came through the door with the bell and a white box, and Avram turned the sign without being asked and got down the two glasses that were only for this. The game was eleven years old that summer. Not the same game — they finished one every few months, when neither could help it — but the same sitting, the same hour, the same argument about whose play had been whose the week before, which was why, four or five years in, they had each started photographing the board with their phones before leaving it. It kept the arguments on solid ground.

"You moved the knight back," Sami said, sitting. "Last week it was on the rim. A knight on the rim is grim, you taught me this, and now look at you."

"The knight is where I left it. What is in the box."

"Ma'amoul. The date ones. Rima says you don't eat enough to insult anybody." Sami opened the box and put it where the captured pieces went, which was an old fight neither bothered having anymore. "My father made these only for the Eid. I make them on a Tuesday because I can. This is a great country."

"It was a great country on the rim of a great country." Avram advanced a pawn he had been advancing in his head since Wednesday. "Play."

The bell went while Sami was thinking. A woman Avram knew by her watch — a Hamilton, her grandmother's, he had put a crystal in it in the spring — leaned in with the door half open and did not come all the way inside, and looked at the two of them at the board a moment too long before she asked, was he open, and could he look at a clasp Tuesday. Avram said Tuesday, yes. She thanked him twice and the door closed and Sami watched her cross the street in the glass.

"She comes to my counter eight years," Sami said. "Friday she asks me where I am from. Eight years she knows where I am from. Friday she needs to hear me say it."

"What did you say."

"I said from Homs. She said she meant before that." Sami moved his bishop without his usual theater. "There is no before that. Before that I was a boy."

Avram looked at the board awhile. Across the street the woman stood at the crossing with her bag held in front of her with both hands, and a city bus went by with its new advertisement, the green one, the two clasped hands and the sentence under them, and the light changed and she stayed where she was, looking at her phone, letting the crossing go.

"Take the bishop back," Avram said. "You moved it angry."

"I moved it fine."

"You moved it angry. I will beat you either way, but slower, with dignity, like neighbors."

The envelopes came Thursday, in both mailboxes, in the same post.

Avram's granddaughter would have said the envelope looked official-adjacent, which was a way she had of talking. It was heavy paper, with a seal that stood next to where a state seal would stand — two clasped hands inside a circle of stars — and under the cellophane window his own name spelled out entire — AVRAM Y. MIZRAHI — the Y a thing no form had asked him for in forty years.

Dalia came by the shop after the pharmacy closed and read it at his workbench with her coat still on. She read fast, the way she read inserts and labels, for the line that mattered.

"Origin declaration," she said. "Ancestry, faith, language spoken at home, community of belonging. Belonging." She turned the page over. "Registration opens the first. Sixty days. It says assignments to follow."

"Assignments."

"You carry many people out?" the doctor asked instead.

The dog weighed twelve pounds, which was two gallons, near enough. He ran the numbers the whole quiet length of the rollout and for once they didn't come out even, and he knew exactly which line item had done it, and he'd fly it again the same way tomorrow, which was the part he sat with.

## 11

"Okay so this is the river," Danny said, to no camera, because the phones stayed in the truck now. "It looks slow but that's how it gets you. My tía Bega swam it right here. Like right here. "Other way," Marco said. "She swam it the other way, mijo. South to north."

"You pointed wrong."

"Next year we put the pit closer to the trees," he said, which was a thing he said annually, and which meant the day had been correct.

## 12

The new rule came down the way the real rules did, not on the radio but at the fuel rack: Hobbs in Decatur wouldn't sell avgas without a tail number and a registry card now, and Hobbs apologized about it to every man he carded, which meant Hobbs was being watched too.

Ray bought four drums of car gas off a man in Bowie instead and blended it himself, did the math on the octane twice, and flew test laps over his own section line listening for the knock that didn't come. The Seal had a date now. October one. They'd said it on the noon broadcast in the school-nurse voice, *general aviation operations consolidated for the safety of all communities*, and at strips up and down the dry side of the metroplex, men who had not voted in twenty years stood in hangar doors doing arithmetic about what consolidated meant for a man with a deadline and an airplane.

What it meant for Ray was demand.

He was flying three nights a week now and turning work away, and the work had changed its clothes. In the spring it had been people like the Mehtas — savings, a plan on the far end, a sister in Toronto with a spare room. Now it was people with the plan half-made or not made, gold they'd gotten for a truck that had been worth twice the gold, asking him questions about Mexico like he was Mexico. He kept the price where the price was. A man who cuts his price in a falling market is advertising something, and what he's advertising is that he's about to stop showing up.

The motel went into the rotation in September, and it went in because of the diesel.

Beto's cousin ran fuel up from Saltillo, but the road had gotten expensive in the new way, and a man at a strip needed a place on the north side that could hold drums without it being anybody's business. The Wayfarer had a maintenance shed, a dead pool taking up insurance weight, and an owner who understood at a glance what was being asked, priced it, and wrote it down. LONG-STAY, RM 2, the ledger said. PAYS DIESEL.

Ray slept there Tuesdays and Thursdays now, in 2, under a framed print of bluebonnets, and the dog slept where the dog had decided it slept, which was the foot of the bed on a folded moving blanket Ray had carried in one night with no comment and never once acknowledged after. He'd run the want-ads on the Mehtas for a month — a man in Monterrey who knew a man, the sister's people in Toronto, a number that rang and rang. Eleven days had come and gone twice over. The dog rode shotgun now with its chin on the window ledge, watching Texas the way Ray watched weather, and at the strip it had a spot in the shade of the wing it considered contractual, and neither of them had named the arrangement anything, because naming it would have put it on a manifest, and some weight you carry off the books. The wife — Asha — had given up asking him to dinner by the third week and started leaving a covered plate on the air conditioner outside his door instead, no note, no transaction, and the first night he stood looking at the plate awhile like it might have a hook in it.

He ate it. He washed the plate at the rust-stained sink and set it back out, and Tuesday there was another one, and that was the arrangement, and neither of them ever said one word about it, which was the only set of terms he'd have accepted.

He watched the place while he pretended not to: the towel count, the marigolds, the long-stays paying in chores when the cash thinned — the schoolteacher in 14 who ran a homework table in the laundry room at four o'clock, eleven kids at it now, three languages going and all of it sounding like homework anyway. The owner walked the property at ten every night with a flashlight he never turned on, and wrote the walk down too, Ray would've bet anything.

It was the kid from 9 that broke the rule.

The trucker's boy was eight, maybe nine, and his family was eating through the last of something while the father called about the impounded rig from the lobby phone every day at one, like a man visiting a grave. The boy had found the dead pool, of course, and made it a fort, of course, and one Thursday Ray came out at dawn to run his engine and the boy was sitting on the diving board in the cold with a toy airplane, a die-cast thing with the paint worn off the wings, flying it in slow circles over the empty deep end with the engine sound boys make low in their throats when they think nobody's listening — and the dog was sitting at the shallow end watching the toy plane bank, head tilting with every turn, because the dog went wherever the boy was now, whenever Ray's truck was parked, an arrangement the two of them had come to without consulting anybody, the way the dog came to all its arrangements.

Ray stood by his truck with his thermos and watched longer than he had business to.

That night the father knocked on 2 with his hat literally in his hands, a thing Ray had read about all his life and never once seen a man actually do.

"They say you fly," the father said. "Out."

"Who says."

"Nobody. The walls." He turned the hat. "I got maybe sixty cents on the dollar coming for the rig if the auction's honest, which. And Karen's sister is in Costa Rica, legal, fifteen years, she'll sponsor us. I'm asking what it runs. That's all I'm asking tonight, what it runs."

The number was eleven thousand and Ray had said it that week to three sets of strangers without his pulse moving. He looked at this one — the laundry-soap smell of him, the boy's silhouette over in 9's window behind the curtain with the airplane still in his hand, doing slow rolls against the lamp — and the number wouldn't load.

"I don't fly people I eat breakfast with," Ray said.

The man blinked. "We never ate—"

"Wednesday. The wife had the waffle iron out in the lobby." Ray had taken his to the room, but he'd taken it, and the man's boy had carried it to him across the lot two-handed, watching it the whole way, proud of every step. "That's breakfast."

The father stood there working it, polite, lost, a man getting turned down for a thing he couldn't afford by a reason he couldn't see. Ray let the silence sit until it taught instead of insulted.

"Auction's Tuesday," Ray said finally. "Take the sixty cents. Then go talk to the owner here about the schoolteacher's husband, who has a van and paper that's still good, and a route worked out through Laredo he thinks nobody knows he's working out. Ground's slower. Ground takes families of four." He looked past the man at the lit curtain, the little plane banking through the lamplight. "Costa Rica will still be there. Place is famous for it."

He shut the door easy. Then he stood in room 2 with his back against it, in the bluebonnet light off the parking lot, doing the one piece of arithmetic that had never once come out even in his life, and the next morning the plate on the air conditioner had pie on it next to the eggs, which meant the walls talked in both directions, and he ate it standing up, looking at the dead pool, and didn't run his engine that day at all.

"And?"

"Then try patience."

Every fall since her second year teaching, Della had her third-graders draw their street.

It was the first art project of the year and secretly the first assessment — you learned who held a pencil like a friend and who held it like a court summons, who lived with grandparents, who drew the dog bigger than the house because the dog was the house. The maps went up over the cubbies and stayed up all year, a paper neighborhood above the real one, and in June she'd send them home and the mothers would keep them forever.

This October she pinned up nineteen.

Last October it had been twenty-six. The difference was specific. It had faces, and it had hooks. Marisol's backpack hook, third from the door, still had its laminated name tag because Marisol had left on a Saturday with the buses and tags came down at year-end, that was the system, and Della was not going to invent a new system in October for taking down a child's name early. The maps this year had new vocabulary.

DeShawn drew his street the way he always drew it, but with a coach bus on it, copied careful, the little toilet window and all, because his cousins' street had gotten buses and DeShawn was eight and buses were interesting. Katie B. drew her cul-de-sac with Walt Pruitt's floodlights as six yellow suns. THIRD GRADE.

Consistent: Y. Nineteen times. Della set the third one on the kitchen table next to the first two after Nia went up, and the two of them sat down with the overhead light on, which between them had always meant business; the porch was for the other thing.

Desmond took the legal pad and drew his lines.

It had been years since Della had watched him do this with anything that mattered — the columns in small caps, his hand steadying once the page had structure, a man laying conduit through a swamp. STAY, the first column said. The case for it filled four lines: the house. The job, what currently existed of the job. The letter's instruction, for what a letter's instruction was worth. Schools — he wrote it and looked at it, and across the table Della said, "I had twenty-six in August. I have nineteen. Two more leave Friday," in the voice she used for attendance, which was how she could say it at all, and he drew a line through SCHOOLS, one line, level, like closing a valve.

"The real problem with the letter is what it doesn't bound," he said. " 'In process' has no end date. A loop with no exit condition is a halt that hasn't been admitted yet, Dell."

"In English, baby."

"Nobody's coming to tell us when. Because there is no when. The system that's supposed to produce the when —" he turned the pen over, set it down "— I don't think it terminates. I think PENDING is the determination."

The second column said VISA. That one was short. The exit-visa office had a name now and a building and a line around the building, and what came out of it, the walls said — and the walls had gotten reliable as the channels got bright — was appointments. Appointments for interviews for applications for permits, the loop again wearing a different lanyard, and PENDING files, the walls said, didn't even get the appointment. He wrote DENIED BY DESIGN in the column and put a box around it. His hand wanted the box.

The third column he headed GROUND, and under it: LAREDO ROUTE — SLOW. CHECKPOINTS ×9 LAST KNOWN. FAMILIES OF 4 — and then, after it, the thing that had actually moved, the reason the conference was tonight and not some night: WAITS UNKNOWN. It had come through the teachers — the group chat that had outlived the district that built it and was now the most reliable newspaper in three counties — a teacher who ran a homework table at a motel out west of town had a husband, and the husband had a van, and that was a sentence you could no longer finish out loud anywhere with a wall, but the end of it was: full through January. The ground was filling up. Slow things fill from the back.

Which left the fourth column, and the fourth column had no heading yet, and one item.

In September, Marcus Whitfield — fourteen years two cubicles over, the only other man in the building who'd cared whether the failovers actually failed over — had called him from a number with no state on it, talked about nothing for four minutes in a voice doing its best, and then said, the way you'd hand somebody a tool you were done with: *If it ever gets to where you need it to be somewhere else fast — there's a man flies out of a strip past Bowie. Cole. Tell him the Nguyens sent you. He'll act like that means nothing. It means he'll listen.*

Desmond had written nothing down that night. He'd been writing it down in the back of his head for six weeks.

Now he took a notecard from the drawer where the notecards had always lived, through every house, every state, every version of their lives — Della's drawer, really, recipe stock, unlined — and he printed on it, in the small caps the legal pad got:

R. COLE — STRIP PAST BOWIE — "THE NGUYENS"

and under that, after a moment, the only number that mattered, the one with the date on it: BEFORE OCT 1.

Della read it upside down, the way her daughter read everything.

"That's eleven days," she said.

"Eleven days to decide. Going is a separate decision." He squared the card to the edge of the table. "There's a difference. I want the option live, that's all this is. You build the failover before the outage. You build it and you test it and you hope it rusts."

"And if it doesn't rust?"

He didn't answer that one, and she didn't make him, because twenty years will teach you which silences are load-bearing. She got up instead and opened the cabinet door, the one by the stove, where the household kept its true documents — the recipe card in careful English block letters, FOR THE SPINACH, IF YOUR FAMILY LIKES, taped where it had been taped since summer like a flag of a country with two citizens — and she took the magnet off the tin and put the notecard up under it, COLE facing out, the Kims' spinach facing out, side by side, the whole foreign policy of the house on one cabinet door.

Then she put the kettle on, because it had gotten too cold for the porch to be anything but a decision, and they made it anyway, every night that week.

Outside, the cold had brought the stars down close. Della had the blanket over both their laps and both mugs were full and steaming and hers was on the rail and his was on the board beside him, and for a while the only sound was the neighborhood doing its small remaining things — a garage door, a dog two streets over, the Kims' kitchen window going dark at ten exactly, the way it went dark at ten exactly every night now, like a lighthouse running in reverse.

"Fifteen minutes," Della said, not a question, her foot finding his knee under the blanket.

"Fifteen minutes."

Up the street Walt's floodlights burned their cold lane across the entrance, and past them, eight hundred miles south by a route with nine known checkpoints, the river ran through the legs of sleeping birds; and out west a man they had never met slept in a room with bluebonnets over the bed and a plate outside the door; and on a cabinet door in the kitchen behind them a notecard and a recipe hung side by side under one magnet, the option and the reason, and neither Desmond nor Della said one word more until the fifteen minutes were over, and then they said five more.
