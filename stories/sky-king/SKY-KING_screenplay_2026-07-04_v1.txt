> **ACT ONE — THE EVENT** <


EXT. SEA-TAC PERIMETER - CCTV - GOLDEN HOUR

A fixed camera on the perimeter road holds a stretch of chain-link fence, and the timestamp in the corner reads 18:42 with the low sun flaring gold across the lens.

The man comes out of the scrub on the far side of the ditch, his hood up and a canvas bag across his back, and the long shadows run out ahead of him toward the fence.

He crosses the ditch and goes up the chain-link and over the wire in one motion, and the camera watches him drop to the road with the timestamp still running and nobody coming.

A camera on the fuel farm picks him up crossing the service road, his face turned away from the pole, while a fuel truck rolls past behind him and its driver looks the other way.

He walks the edge of the equipment rows and the camera above the baggage carts takes him from behind as the sun sits lower in this frame than the last.

Out on the apron a tug backs an aircraft off its gate, and the pushback crew work the nosewheel while the man passes along the far side of the carts and none of them turn their heads.

A camera on a light pole catches him in the open between two service lanes, and the floodlights on the ramp flick on early against the falling light while gulls lift off the grass around him.

He keeps to the blind side of each pole, and camera to camera his face never once comes toward a lens.

The last camera on the outer ramp takes him small and far, crossing the concrete toward the parked rows with his shadow stretched long across the paint.

He walks to the edge of the frame and out of it, and the camera does not follow.

The frame holds on empty concrete and the long gold shadows lying across it.

The timestamp in the corner ticks over, and far off in the corner of the image the tail of a parked airliner stands against the low sun.


EXT. SEA-TAC - NORTH RAMP - NIGHT

The belt keeps running in the last low gold light, long shadows thrown across the apron, bags riding up it and dropping off the end into a cart nobody is loading.

GUS, older, a Texan in a fuel company jacket, stands on the platform of his truck with the nozzle in the wing and one boot up on the fender.

Forty feet down, a jet sits half-turned off the gate, the tug still hooked to the nose, the pushback driver leaning out of his seat looking at the tower.

DEZ, young, a handheld radio in his fist, stands off past the last row of tails with his back half to the crew, looking out at the far end of the field, the sun nearly down behind it.

The PA crackles up on the terminal wall.

PA (RADIO)
Ground stop. All ramp personnel, ground stop in effect. Hold all aircraft, hold all vehicles. Nothing moves.

Gus's nozzle clicks off in his hand. He looks at the numbers on the pump, then up at the terminal.

GUS
That's not weather.

The pushback driver kills his engine. The tug sits there hooked to the nose, going nowhere. The belt keeps running, bags climbing and dropping, climbing and dropping, into the cart nobody touches.

He walks out a few feet past the wing and stands in the low gold light looking at the row of tails.

Far across the field, blue and red lights slide along the far taxiway, two sets, then three, running low and fast with no siren the ramp can hear.

The handhelds on every hip go clipped and strange, the tower voice coming through in pieces, half a word and then gone.

TOWER (RADIO)
— hold your position, do not — say again, do not —

The office door bangs open and WARD comes out, the ramp boss, a windbreaker thrown over his uniform shirt, a phone pressed hard to his ear. He walks out onto the apron a few steps and stops, looking south, past the field, at the sky going from gold to blue, the sun down, the west still faintly lit.

WARD
Yeah.

He listens. His free hand comes up and rubs the back of his neck and stays there.

WARD
Wait. Say that again.

He goes still. Whatever is coming through the phone, he takes it standing, staring south, and gives the ramp nothing.

Gus watches Ward a second, then turns back to the row of tails. He lifts his chin and starts down the line, counting under his breath, slow.

GUS
One. Two.

He points with two fingers, tail to tail, walking the row.

GUS
Three. Four.

He stops. He looks at a black gap in the line where the low gold light falls on empty concrete, chocks lying loose on the ground, no airplane over them.

He counts the rest of the row quick, then walks back to the gap and stands at it.

GUS
We're one short.

The pushback driver climbs down and comes over and looks at the empty spot with him. Nobody says anything. The belt runs. A bag drops off the end and hits the concrete and lies there.

Gus looks back down the line at Dez, still out past the tails with the radio in his hand.

GUS
Dez. You were out that end.

Dez doesn't turn all the way around. He looks at Gus over his shoulder.

GUS
You see anything go?

Dez looks down at the radio in his hand, then back out at the fading light in the south.

DEZ
I don't know. I wasn't— I don't know what I saw.

Gus lets it sit. He looks at the kid a long beat, then looks away, back at the empty concrete and the loose chocks.

GUS
Okay.

Headlights swing onto the ramp from the service road. A port-police SUV rolls up the line, slow, past the frozen tug, past the running belt, coming toward the crew and the gap in the tails.

Ward lowers the phone from his ear and watches it come.

Dez watches it too. The SUV rolls closer, its lights washing over him.

He turns his head and looks south, out at the last of the light where the airplane went, and the radio hangs in his hand at his side.


INT. ALERT FACILITY, AIR NATIONAL GUARD FIGHTER BASE - DAY

The room holds two vinyl recliners, a folding table, a wall clock, and a coffee maker with the pot half-empty. DEACON sits with a binder open on his knee, reading. BANJO is sunk low in the second recliner, one boot up on the folding table, working a plastic spoon around an empty yogurt cup.

BANJO
They put the strawberry back in the machine. Row C. I checked twice.

DEACON turns a page.

BANJO
I'm not saying it's a big thing. It's just the strawberry's the one nobody buys, so it just sits there, and the peanut ones are gone by like nine every morning, and nobody ever moves them around, that's all.

DEACON
Put it on the sheet.

BANJO
On the sheet?

DEACON
The DFAC comment sheet. By the door.

BANJO
Yeah. I'll write up the yogurt.

BANJO drops the cup on the table. It rolls a little and stops. The clock ticks. DEACON keeps reading.

BANJO
This chair's got a thing in it now. Right here, in the back, like a bolt or something come loose, so when you lean it just kind of. It doesn't go all the way anymore.

DEACON
They swapped it in March.

BANJO
This is the swapped one.

DEACON
Maintenance can look at it.

BANJO
I don't want maintenance to look at it, I just want to lean back in the chair.

DEACON doesn't answer. He reaches for his coffee, finds it cold, sets it back down without drinking.

BANJO
You ever count how many times we been out here this month.

DEACON
We're on the schedule through Thursday.

BANJO
No, I know we're on the schedule. I mean sitting here. Like this. The two of us in the chairs.

DEACON
It's the rotation.

BANJO
I know it's the rotation.

BANJO takes his boot off the table and sits up a little.

BANJO
You talk to Karen this week or.

DEACON
About what.

BANJO
I don't know. Anything. She good.

DEACON
She's fine.

BANJO
That's good. That's good, that she's fine.

DEACON turns another page. BANJO watches him for a second, then looks back at the clock.

BANJO
You could just say the thing. Whatever it is. It's just me out here.

DEACON
Nothing to say.

BANJO
Okay.

DEACON
Read your checklist stuff. You're due for the quals.

BANJO
I did the quals.

DEACON
The recurring.

BANJO leans his head back against the broken recliner and stares up.

BANJO
Right. The recurring.

A loud tone cuts through the room from the wall speaker. Both men are up out of the chairs before the second beat, moving. DEACON's binder hits the floor. They cross to the gear.

RADIO
Alert, alert, unknown low and slow, scramble the alert.

DEACON has his G-suit half on, one leg zipped, reaching for the harness. BANJO is right beside him, helmet already in his hand, breathing hard.

DEACON
You got the freq.

BANJO
I got it.

The wall speaker crackles again.

RADIO
Stand down, stand down. Contact's turned. He's a strayed Cessna, he's coming about, we got him. Stand down the alert.

Neither man moves for a moment. DEACON stands there with one leg of the suit zipped, the harness in his hand. BANJO lowers the helmet.

BANJO
Turned around.

DEACON
Copy the stand-down.

DEACON unzips the suit leg. He picks his binder up off the floor and sets it back on the table. BANJO stands by the gear a second longer, then hangs the helmet back on the hook.

BANJO
A Cessna.

DEACON
They wander up out of the county strip. Happens.

BANJO walks back to the recliners and drops into the broken one. It doesn't lean.

BANJO
We're gonna do this till they hand us the paperwork, you know that.

DEACON sits down, opens the binder to where he was.

BANJO
Just you and me out here. And that strawberry nobody even eats.

DEACON
Write it up.

BANJO
I'll write it up.

DEACON reads. BANJO looks at the ceiling. The clock ticks. Neither of them says anything for a while.


INT. SEATTLE APPROACH - RADAR ROOM - NIGHT

Bishop sits at the radar scope in a cardigan under his headset, his reading glasses pushed up on his head, a controller in his fifties working the quiet end of a long night shift.

A blip crawls onto the bottom of his scope with no tag beside it, and he watches it for a second before he thumbs his mic toward the man at the next position.

BISHOP
Price. You working anything down off the field, south end?

Price, thirties, his sleeves shoved up past his elbows and his own sector's traffic still going in one ear, leans back in his chair without looking over.

PRICE
Down where.

BISHOP
Off Sea-Tac. Came off the ground a minute ago, tracking south. No tag on him. No ID, no altitude, just the blip.

PRICE
Not mine. Nobody handed me anything off the field.

Behind the chairs Kemp stands at the supervisor's desk in a government sport coat, a visitor badge clipped crooked on his pocket, signing the bottom of a thin folder of forms, the DHS liaison at the end of a quarterly night visit where nothing has happened.

BISHOP
Well, somebody's gotta be working him. He didn't file a plan, there's nothing on him.

A line on Price's console lights up and buzzes, and he picks up the handset and wedges it against his shoulder.

PRICE
Approach.

Price listens, and his chair comes forward off its slouch.

PRICE
Say that again. They watched it go?

He covers the mouthpiece and says it across to Bishop, flat.

PRICE
Tower's got eyes on it. Says it's one of the airline's turboprops, the big twin-prop. Taxied out on its own, nobody cleared it, and it took off.

PRICE
Yeah. Yeah, we got him down here, no tag on him. Okay.

Price hangs up the line and turns half back to his own scope.

PRICE
They don't have a name on who's in it. Just watched it roll.

Bishop pulls his chair in and thumbs the transmit key, his eyes on the blip crawling with nothing next to it.

BISHOP
Aircraft southbound off Sea-Tac, this is Seattle Approach. Identify yourself and say your intentions.

The frequency keys open with a hiss. An engine drones somewhere behind it. Someone breathes. The mic clicks off. No words come.

BISHOP
Aircraft off Sea-Tac, Seattle Approach. How do you hear.

The same hiss opens and holds. The engine drones behind it. A breath goes by. The mic clicks off again.

Bishop leans back and lifts his voice without turning around.

BISHOP
Hey. I need the sup over here.

The Supervisor comes off his desk, sixties, gray, his reading glasses hanging on a lanyard, a man twenty years of night shifts have made impossible to hurry, Kemp's half-signed folder still in one hand.

SUPERVISOR
What've you got.

BISHOP
Airplane off the field, no plan, no ID. He's keying up and not saying anything.

Kemp follows his folder over, his jacket over one arm, and stops behind Bishop's chair looking at the untagged blip.

KEMP
When he keys up and there's nothing. That happen a lot, a guy keys the mic and doesn't talk?

BISHOP
Stuck mic, sometimes. Guy bumps it in the seat. This isn't that.

KEMP
I'm just, I gotta know what I'm writing down here.

The Supervisor sets the folder on the console and leans over Bishop's shoulder toward the speaker.

SUPERVISOR
Try him again.

Bishop keys the transmit.

BISHOP
Aircraft off Sea-Tac, Seattle Approach. You're up on your own frequency, go ahead.

The frequency opens and the engine drones behind it. This time a voice comes through, soft and slow.

BIRDY (RADIO)
Yeah, uh. Hi. Sorry to bug you guys.

The room goes still around the speaker.

BIRDY (RADIO)
I, uh. I kind of did a bad thing. This airplane's not really mine to have. Real sorry.

Bishop's hand stays on the key and his voice flattens down into the job.

BISHOP
Okay. I hear you. Anybody else on the airplane with you?

BIRDY (RADIO)
No. No, it's empty. Nobody back there.

BISHOP
And it's you flying it right now. Just you.

BIRDY (RADIO)
Yeah. Just me up here.

Price has turned all the way around in his chair now, his own traffic still going in the ear nobody's listening to.

PRICE (WHISPER)
That's a hijacking.

The Supervisor doesn't look up from the speaker.

SUPERVISOR
Hijacked off who. He's the only one on it. Keep that word off the phone.

KEMP
The floor's gonna ask me what it is, though. What do I call it.

The Supervisor straightens up and thinks about it for a second.

SUPERVISOR
A guy took an airplane that wasn't his. It's a stolen airplane. That's what it is.

Kemp uncaps a pen against the folder and writes it down.

KEMP
Stolen aircraft.

Kemp reads back over what he wrote, his lips moving, then stops.

KEMP
They're gonna want who, though. I say a guy took it, first thing out of their mouth is which guy. And I got nothing for that.

BISHOP
We don't have a name. Tower doesn't either.

KEMP
Yeah. That's the part that sounds made up.

Kemp works the sentence half under his breath, then lifts the phone off the wall and dials.

KEMP
Somebody took one of the airline's turboprops off Sea-Tac a few minutes ago. It's empty, one man on board flying it, up talking to us on the radio right now. And we don't know who he is.

Nobody in the room moves. Kemp stands there with the phone still at his ear.

On Bishop's scope the untagged blip crawls off the last of the ground and out over the black of the water.


INT. SEATTLE APPROACH - RADAR ROOM - NIGHT (MOMENTS LATER)

Kemp stands at the edge of the console with the phone still at his ear, one hand cupped over the mouthpiece, listening to the watch floor.

He lowers the phone a few inches and turns to the room.

KEMP
Okay. They want him kept talking. Whoever's got the frequency, keep him on it.

The Supervisor is behind Bishop's chair with his reading glasses pushed up on his head, and he pulls them down to look at the scope.

SUPERVISOR
That's you, Bishop. Just keep him talking.

Kemp brings the phone back up, listens, then covers it again.

KEMP
And they're bringing some airplanes up out of the base. To take a look at him.

Nobody in the room says anything to that. Kemp goes back to the phone.

On Bishop's scope the one slow blip drifts out over the black water, no tag, no altitude on it. Bishop keys up.

BISHOP
Hey. Same guy down here you had before. I'm gonna stay with you a while, okay?

BIRDY (RADIO)
Yeah. Yeah, I'm here.

BISHOP
That's a big airplane you got there. Lot of airplane to be flying around by hand.

BIRDY (RADIO)
She's, uh. She's flying okay, I think. I don't really know if I'm doing it right, to be honest with you.

Bishop leans back a little in the chair and keeps his eyes on the blip.

BISHOP
You're doing fine. Doing just fine.

BIRDY (RADIO)
I don't want to hurt anybody, that's the main thing. I hope I'm not scaring people down there.

BISHOP
Nobody's scared. It's a quiet night, there's not a lot of traffic out there right now.

Price, half turned back to his own traffic at the next position, watches his own sector while he half-listens.

BIRDY (RADIO)
It's real pretty up here. You can see the lights and everything.

BISHOP
Yeah? I bet it is. Hey, listen, I'm gonna be talking to you a while here. What do I call you?

BIRDY (RADIO)
Aw. It's, uh.

The line hisses for a second. Bishop waits, doesn't fill it.

BIRDY (RADIO)
Everybody just calls me Birdy. You can call me that.

BISHOP
Okay. Birdy.

Kemp has his log flat on the console and he writes across it, saying it down at the paper.

KEMP (WHISPER)
Goes by Birdy, quote-unquote. No name.

Price's eyes go to the bottom of his scope where two new tags come up off the base, moving fast, and he calls it flat across the room without turning his head.

PRICE
Bishop, you got two fast movers coming up off the south, climbing hard.

Bishop looks down at the corner of his own scope and finds them. He keys up, easy.

BISHOP
Hey Birdy, couple airplanes are gonna come up and take a look at you. They're not gonna do anything, all right?

BIRDY (RADIO)
Oh. Okay. That's okay.

On the scope the two fast tags climb toward the one slow blip, closing the water between them.


INT. F-15 COCKPIT / NIGHT SKY - NIGHT

The klaxon rips through the ready room and both men are up and moving before the second cycle.

DEACON
That's the real tone.

BANJO
Yeah, it always is. Then it's some crop duster nobody shut off.

They cross the ramp in the last dusk light, the ramp lights just coming on, helmets under their arms, boots loud on the concrete.

DEACON
Brief said airliner. Q400 out of Cascade, went dark, quit answering, came around off its clearance.

BANJO
Off its clearance how far?

DEACON
Hijack profile. That's what they handed us.

Canopies drop. Engines wind up and the two jets roll and go, afterburners lighting the taxiway behind them.

DEACON (RADIO)
Sword flight airborne, passing angels eight, looking for the picture.

BISHOP (RADIO)
Sword lead, Bishop. Your target's your eleven o'clock, forty miles, angels one-two, slow. Very slow.

BANJO (RADIO)
Copy slow. Here we go again. Another one out of the county strip.

DEACON (RADIO)
Negative. This one's moving.

BANJO (RADIO)
Say again?

DEACON (RADIO)
It's a real one, Banjo. It's up and it's turning.

The HUD steps down the range. A single contact sits low and alone over the darkening water.

BANJO (RADIO)
Okay. Okay, so it's not a Cessna.

DEACON (RADIO)
No.

They climb toward it, the two jets closing on the one slow return in the failing light.

DEACON (RADIO)
Bishop, confirm the intent. You getting anything off him. Demands, a number, anything.

BISHOP (RADIO)
Nothing like that. I've got a man on the frequency. He's talking to me. That's all he's doing.

DEACON (RADIO)
There's a guy actually talking to you?

BISHOP (RADIO)
Yeah. Just him, far as I can tell.

BANJO (RADIO)
Tally the strobe. My twelve, low.

DEACON (RADIO)
No joy. Where.

BANJO (RADIO)
Below the horizon line. He's under us and he's just kind of, drifting.

Deacon banks and drops. Ahead the running lights blink on the wings of the airliner. It is not flying straight. It wanders, noses up, sinks, comes back.

DEACON (RADIO)
Visual. I've got him.

BANJO (RADIO)
The hell is he doing. He's all over the place.

DEACON (RADIO)
He's chasing the altitude. He keeps losing it and getting it back.

BISHOP (RADIO)
He told me he's not sure he's doing this right. Those were his words.

The two fighters slide in above and behind the Q400, matching its slow crawl, throttles back near the edge of the envelope.

BANJO (RADIO)
There's no gun on this thing, Deak, there's nothing on it at all. It's just a guy up there.

DEACON (RADIO)
I see him.

The airliner's cockpit is a faint green glow against the blue dusk. A single figure sits in the left seat, hands on the yoke.

BANJO (RADIO)
So what do we do here.

DEACON (RADIO)
I don't know.

BANJO (RADIO)
You're lead, Deak.

DEACON (RADIO)
I know I am. Just give me a second.

The Q400 sinks again, a hundred feet, then more, the wings rocking.

DEACON (RADIO)
He's coming down. Bishop, he's descending, he's got a wing down.

BISHOP (RADIO)
I know. Talk him up if he asks. He's not trying to hurt anybody. He keeps saying that.

The airliner's nose lifts. The wings level. Slowly, evenly, it rolls into a long clean turn against the last light in the west, holding it all the way through.

BANJO (RADIO)
Whoa.

BANJO (RADIO)
Deak. Deak, you see that.

DEACON (RADIO)
Yeah, I got it.

BANJO (RADIO)
He actually flew that. That was a real turn.

DEACON (RADIO)
Yeah.

BANJO (RADIO)
Where's he even learn to do that.

Deacon says nothing. His jet holds formation off the airliner's wing, matching the slow bank, following it around.

DEACON (RADIO)
Bishop. What's his name.

BISHOP (RADIO)
He goes by Birdy. That's what he asked me to call him.

A faint voice bleeds across the frequency under Bishop's, warm and unhurried, the words lost.

DEACON (RADIO)
Say your last, I stepped on it.

BISHOP (RADIO)
That was him, not me. He was just saying it's real pretty up here.

The two jets stay locked on the Q400's wingtip as it comes level again, all three aircraft crawling through the last of the light together.

DEACON (RADIO)
What do you want us to do with him.

BISHOP (RADIO)
Just stay with him. Right now, just stay with him.

The airliner drifts on, running lights blinking, and neither fighter moves off it.


INT. AIRPORT OPERATIONS CENTER - COMMAND POST - NIGHT

A folding table scrapes across the linoleum as two guys in windbreakers carry it past and set it down against the wall.

MERCER, fifties, jacket off and sleeves rolled, a phone pressed to his ear, walks the length of the room stepping over a cable somebody is taping to the floor.

MERCER
Yeah, I've got you. Put the second one on the bridge line and keep it open, I don't want to be dialing anybody twice tonight.

A wall screen wakes up blue, then black, then a slow green track appears looping out over the water, and a tech leans in and thumbs two small tags onto it that ride along behind the loop.

MERCER lowers the phone from his ear and points at a woman seating agencies at the tables.

MERCER
Where are we on the human side. I've got a room and I've got a picture and I don't have anybody to work the guy.

The woman shakes her head and keeps plugging in a monitor.

REYES comes through the door, forties, dressed and quick, a bag over one shoulder, still pulling her badge around on its lanyard, looking for whoever's in charge.

REYES
They pulled me out of my kitchen. Somebody tell me what I've got.

MERCER crosses to her and puts the phone facedown against his chest for a second.

MERCER
You're the negotiator. Good. Okay. There's an airplane up.

MERCER turns and tips his head at the screen with the looping green track.

MERCER
One of the airline's turboprops off Sea-Tac. Empty, no passengers, and there's one guy flying it. That's what we've got from the tower and from Kemp's call.

REYES
One guy. And he's, what — crew? A pilot?

MERCER
He's not on anybody's roster. He told the tower it isn't his, the airplane. That's the whole thing we know about him.

A phone starts ringing on one of the new tables and nobody gets to it and it keeps going.

REYES
Okay. Anybody else up there with him, in the cabin, anybody hurt that we know of.

MERCER
No. Just him.

MERCER lifts the phone back to his ear, says "hold on," and lowers it again.

MERCER
And there's two fighters on him. Those are the two tags you see sitting on the track. That's already up, that came up with the military side, that's not us.

REYES looks at the two tags riding the green loop and sets her bag down against a table leg.

REYES
Right. So before I get near him I need to know what I'm authorized to offer him. What can I actually put on the table.

MERCER glances at a printer that's grinding out a page and doesn't answer for a second.

MERCER
Whatever gets him down.

REYES stands there a beat.

REYES
I hear you, but I mean the real answer, Mercer. If I say a thing to this man, is there paper behind it. Is there somebody I can name who's good for it.

MERCER's phone lights again in his hand and he silences it against his leg.

MERCER
There's you. You get him on the ground.

MERCER holds her look, then turns toward the tables filling with badges.

REYES
So there's nothing to point to. It's me saying it, and then it's the same job over again.

MERCER
That's what I've got tonight.

Neither of them says anything, and around them a fourth screen wakes up and two more agents come in with coffee and set their go-bags on the floor.

MERCER steps back in and lowers his voice under the room.

MERCER (WHISPER)
I can't define it for you. You know I can't. The second I define it it's mine, and then it's slow, and we don't have slow.

REYES nods, once, and doesn't push it.

REYES
Okay. Then give me a place to start, because right now I've got nobody. No name, no file, nothing, I'm talking to a hole.

MERCER
Two things, in order. Find out who he is first. Then you get him down.

MERCER points across the room at a door where a duty officer is walking two men in high-visibility vests through security.

MERCER
Airline's bringing their people in over there. There's a ramp boss and somebody who saw him. Start with them, get me a name.

A RADIO on the tech's desk crackles and a flat voice comes through it.

CONTROLLER (RADIO)
Command, we're still holding him over the water, he's just making the turn again, no change.

MERCER is already walking, the phone coming back up to his ear.

MERCER
Copy, keep him talking to somebody.

REYES doesn't move. She stands with her bag at her feet and looks up at the screen, at the slow green track making its loop and the two fast tags riding it, while behind her the phones ring and the tables keep filling.


INT. AIRPORT OPERATIONS CENTER - COMMAND POST - NIGHT

Fluorescent light on a wide low-ceilinged room jammed with people, folding tables shoved together, laptops open, cables taped down the carpet in silver.

A blue windbreaker with FBI across the back moves past a man in a Homeland polo; a port police officer leans over a monitor; a TSA supervisor talks into a headset.

On the big wall screen a green track crawls west over a coastline, a data block ticking beside it.

REYES stands at the end of a table with a legal pad, phone pinned between shoulder and ear, waving two men into folding chairs across from her.

— copy, Center's got him level at one-two thousand, no squawk change, still off any filed —

Yeah, I'm here, I'm still here. Give me two minutes and I'll have a name for you.

Okay. I'm Reyes, I'm with the Bureau, I'm the one who's gonna be asking you guys stuff for a while, so. Sorry. It's loud in here.

We already talked to the airline people. Two of 'em.

I know. I know you did. This is a different piece of it. The guy that took the aircraft up, he works your ramp, is that right, he's yours.

He's on the ground crew, yeah.

Name.

P-E-T-R-E-K.
