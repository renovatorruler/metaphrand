INT. BIRDY AND MAYA'S HOUSE — KITCHEN/LIVING ROOM — NIGHT

A small rented house. The TV is on in the next room, low — a wildlife
show, blue light moving on the walls. In the kitchen MAYA (late 20s)
plates two pork chops, mashed potatoes, the same supper as always. She's
still in her grocery-store polo.

BIRDY (early 30s) comes in from the hall in his socks, baggage-tag
lanyard still around his neck. He sets his keys in the bowl. Slips an
envelope out of his back pocket as he does it — turns it face-down under
the mail flyers in one motion, smooth, practiced. Maya's back is to him.
<!-- fix: "smooth, like a man used to doing it" -> "smooth, practiced" -->

MAYA
Wash up. It's ready.

He washes his hands at the kitchen sink, a long time, watching the water.

They sit. The TV keeps talking from the other room. They eat. Forks on
plates. Maya looks at him. Looks at her food.

MAYA (CONT'D)
Donna asked about you today. At the register.

BIRDY
Yeah?

MAYA
I told her you were gonna fix her gate. You said that.

BIRDY
I'll get to it.

She smiles a little, reaching for the old version of him.

MAYA
Remember when we drove up to Index? You climbed that thing in your
church shoes.

For a second he's with her — almost. Then his eyes go to the middle
distance, somewhere past her shoulder, and he's gone. She watches it
happen. She waits. He doesn't come back.

MAYA (CONT'D)
(quieter)
Anyway.

She turns back to her plate.

A while. The wildlife show swells, ebbs.

MAYA (CONT'D)
Hey.

He looks up.

MAYA (CONT'D)
How are you. Really. Just — one. The real one.

A pause. Something moves across his face and gets put away. He gives her
a small warm smile, the one he gives everybody.

BIRDY
I'm fine.

She holds his eyes. She knows. He holds the smile, kind, like a door
closed gently.

MAYA
Okay.

She hates that she said it. She picks up both plates though he's not
finished, takes them to the sink, runs the water hard.

Birdy stands. He drifts into the living room.

INT. LIVING ROOM — CONTINUOUS

He lowers himself into one particular armchair — old, the wrong era for
this house, the armrests worn pale where hands have gone for years. He
doesn't reach for the remote. He just sits, the blue light on his face,
very still. A young man sitting like a much older one. On the side table
beside him: a man's wristwatch, not running, that he doesn't wear.

From the kitchen the water runs.

His gaze drifts off the TV — across the room, down the dark hall, and
stops on a closed door. The spare room. He looks at it the way you look
at water on a hot day.

The kitchen tap shuts off. Maya appears in the doorway, drying her hands.
She watches the back of his head, the stillness of him, the blue light.
She wants to cross the room. She stands where she is.

MAYA
I'm going up.

BIRDY
(not turning)
I'll be up.

She goes up the stairs. He hears them creak. He doesn't move.

When the house is quiet he leans forward, pulls the face-down envelope
from under the flyers on the side table — he's carried it in here. He
thumbs it open in his lap, low, below the armrest where no one walking in
could see. Inside: a folded notice, red lettering across the top. He
reads the number on it. He folds it again, smaller, and slides it into
the gap between the cushion and the arm of the chair.

He sits back. The animals run across the screen. He watches the
spare-room door.

FADE OUT.
