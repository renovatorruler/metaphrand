EXT. AIRPORT RAMP — GATE C7 — DUSK

Rain. Not heavy — the steady kind that soaks through everything and
never makes a sound you'd notice over the engines.

A regional jet sits at the gate, parked, its beacon turning.
Floodlights. Wet concrete throwing the light back up. Bag carts in a
row, tarps shining.

BIRDY (Eli), 30, orange vest gone dark at the shoulders with rain, works
the belt loader at the forward hold. He's good at it — fast, quiet, no
wasted motion. He hoists each bag clean, swings it onto the belt, never
looks at his hands. Ear defenders pushed up off one ear.

A few feet off, DEZ, big, leans on a cart and watches Birdy work instead
of working.

DEZ
You know what Marquez got. The new guy. Six months.

BIRDY
(loading)
I don't, no.

DEZ
Guess.

BIRDY
Dez.

A bag with a broken strap. Birdy tucks the strap under so it won't
catch, sets it on the belt. Small thing. Nobody's watching but Dez.

DEZ
More than you. Six months in and more than you.

Birdy gives a short laugh, keeps loading.

BIRDY
Good for him.

DEZ
That's not — no. That's not good for him. That's them robbing you and
you saying thank you.

Birdy reaches the last bag. Slings it. Slaps the side of the loader
twice — done. He drops down onto the wet concrete, pulls the belt back,
latches the hold.

BIRDY
He asked. I bet he asked.

DEZ
So go ask.

BIRDY
(small smile)
Now?

DEZ
Klein's still up there. Light's on. Right now, before he leaves. You
walk in, you say the number, you stand there till he says yes. I did the
math last night, Birdy. What they pay you an hour — for this —

He waves a hand at the rain, the jet, all of it.

DEZ (CONT'D)
It's a joke. It's an actual joke.

Birdy coils a tie-down strap, neat loops, hangs it on the cart hook.

BIRDY
It's enough.

DEZ
It's not the point if it's enough.

BIRDY
I'll talk to him. Next week. When it's not — when he's not running out
the door.

DEZ
That's what you told me last month.            <!-- fix: was the echo "Next week." -->

BIRDY
He's a busy guy.

Dez looks at him. Wants to drag him up the stairs by the vest. Doesn't.

DEZ
You'd take less. Just to not — to not make him look at you.

Birdy pulls his ear defenders back down. He bends, frees the nose wheel
chocks, drags them clear, stacks them on the cart. The little headset
crackles.

BIRDY
(into headset, easy)
C7, chocks away. Good to push.

He picks the lighted wands off the cart. Orange, glowing. He clicks them
on.

DEZ
(louder, over the engines)
Birdy.

BIRDY
I'll take what he offers.

And he walks out in front of the jet.

EXT. RAMP — IN FRONT OF GATE C7 — CONTINUOUS

Birdy plants his boots on the wet line, faces the aircraft, lifts both
wands. The engines come up. The rain blows sideways through the
wand-light.

He brings the jet forward — arms wide, then in, then wide — reading the
nose wheel, walking backward a step, steady. He turns it onto the taxi
line. Crosses the wands over his head: STOP. Then one arm out, flat,
pointing it away down the taxiway.

The jet rolls past him, huge, close enough to feel the heat off it, and
goes. He stands in the spray of its passing.

He watches it taxi out. Watches it sit at the hold line. Watches it run
up and go — wheels off the wet runway, gear folding up, the lights
climbing into the low cloud and the gold of the last of the day above it.

He's still got the wands up.

A beat longer than he needs to.

Then he lowers them. Clicks them off.

DEZ (O.S.)
(from back at the gate)
You're soaked, man. Come on.

Birdy turns and goes back to the carts, into the floodlight, into the rain.
