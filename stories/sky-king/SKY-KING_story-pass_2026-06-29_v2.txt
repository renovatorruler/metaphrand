SKY KING — STORY PASS: the real conflict
(right-brain re-break of the back half)
29 June 2026 — v2


================================================================
WHAT WAS WRONG  (you're right)
================================================================

The old back half made the central question "can he land the plane,"
and handed him a deal the second he asked for one. No jeopardy. And a
prosecutor who lies to him one minute and gives him mercy the next isn't
just undramatic — it isn't how any of it works. It let the audience off
the hook. We never put him up the tree and threw stones. So the story
went soft exactly where it should grip hardest.


================================================================
THE FIX — the antagonist is PEOPLE, and the question is "will they LET him live"
================================================================

What the audience should feel:
   I love this guy. He's in deep trouble. PLEASE don't let them kill him.

Two life-or-death questions, running at once, INTERCUT THROUGH THE SAME
HOUR (so the film gets no longer — the dreamy hour aloft is now also a
desperate fight on the ground):

   1) Will the system let him live?      <- the institutional fight
   2) Even if they do, can he survive putting it down?   <- no runway, no fuel

And underneath both: he came up here to die. He may not help them save him.


================================================================
THE PEOPLE
================================================================

BIRDY — up the tree.
BISHOP — the controller. The steady human voice; never stops reaching for
   the man instead of the threat.
MAYA — the wife. Brought to the tower. Her love is a lifeline and an agony
   (he can't stand to hurt her).
REYES — the prosecutor. HIS ARC IS THE AUDIENCE'S: he starts as the
   system's instrument — sent in to lie Birdy down — meets the man, comes
   to like him, and ends up fighting his own bosses for his life. Our way in.
HOLT — the boss / incident command. Cold institutional indifference: a
   stolen plane, jets on it, away from people; "a bad guy"; if he goes down,
   "it solves itself." Not cartoon-evil — he just doesn't care. THIS is the
   wall we fight.


================================================================
THE NEW SHAPE
================================================================

Light arc unchanged: golden hour -> sunset (the roll) -> dusk -> lit night.

ACT 2 — THE MAN + THE DREAM (mostly stands; this is where we fall for him)
  • First contact (golden hour) — Bishop reaches the man, not the threat.
  • Rainier (late gold) — the dreamy confession. [WRITTEN]
  • Bringing Maya in (sunset) — NEW. A patrol car at the house; they bring
    her in, frightened, from her own kitchen (porch light already on) to
    the tower. At the same time the machine mobilizes: we meet Reyes and
    Holt and the room. (Your "bring the wife in" beat + it plants the
    ground antagonist.)
  • The porch light (sunset) — Bishop keys Maya in. "I left it on."
  • The roll + the watchers (last light -> dusk) — he decides he wants to
    live. KEY CHANGE: this no longer resolves anything — it LOADS the gun.
    The moment we (and he) want him to live is the moment the system
    decides he isn't worth saving.

ACT 3 — THE FIGHT FOR HIS LIFE (night, lit)
  • THE LIE. Holt tells Reyes: say whatever it takes, promise no charges,
    just get it down. Reyes runs the lie. Birdy catches it cold — "I'm not
    stupid. Tell me the real one." Trust broken. Reyes has nothing true
    left to say. [this is the old DA scene, repurposed — now it FAILS]
  • REYES vs HOLT. Shaken by the man he just talked to, Reyes goes back and
    fights: you want it down clean, you give him a REAL deal — no prison.
    Holt refuses. Plane's away from people; the jets can end it; he's a
    criminal; if he augers in, no loss. A man arguing for a stranger's life
    against a room that shrugs. THE CORE CONFLICT.
  • THE SQUEEZE. Everything tightens at once: fuel near gone; Birdy
    drifting, tired, trust gone; the fighters going hot; Maya holding him
    on the radio; Bishop holding the line; Reyes losing upstairs. The
    darkest point — they are ready to let him die.
  • THE DEAL, EARNED. Reyes breaks through — the country is watching this
    live (you do not shoot a clearly broken man down on camera), and/or he
    stakes his own career and makes it real himself. He gets on the radio
    and finally tells Birdy the WHOLE truth: the charges, the real
    exposure, and that he will personally fight for no prison. Birdy, who
    catches lies, hears truth this time, and believes it. Late. Costly.
    [the honest-deal beat you liked — now EARNED, not handed over]
  • NO RUNWAY. Out of fuel, no airport in reach. He has to put it down
    where it was never meant to go — a dark field, a back road, a sandbar.
    He's "safe" now, on paper — and might still die in the next ninety
    seconds. Talked down by Bishop and Maya. THE LAST WORRY: after all of
    it, does he physically make it?
  • HE LIVES. A brutal, barely-survived landing.
  • CODA (short). Alive; the deal holds; Maya; the living-folk-hero note.
    Kept tight.


================================================================
WHY IT'S STILL TIGHT
================================================================
It's the same one hour. The ground-fight is INTERCUT with the dreamy
cockpit, and the contrast makes both hit harder — his strange peace up
there against the room deciding his fate down here. The aftermath is one
short coda. Nothing here lengthens the film; it fills the hour with the
jeopardy it was missing.


================================================================
WHAT STANDS
================================================================
Act 1 (the cage) and the front of Act 2 (the man, the confession, the
dream) are right — that's where we fall in love with him. This pass is
about what he's UP AGAINST once we do. And it folds the work already
written — the Rainier scene stands; the DA "honest deal" scene gets moved
to the END and re-earned.
