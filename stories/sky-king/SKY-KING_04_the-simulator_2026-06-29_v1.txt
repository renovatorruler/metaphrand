SKY KING — Scene 4: THE SIMULATOR  (Act 1 — the secret aliveness)

[ The one room where the drowning man comes alive. The transformation is all
  body — hands, shoulders, breath, a real smile. Maya steals the glimpse; the
  shutter drops; she's left watching from the dark. The traded self / the
  depression stay submerged. This is the "want him to live" fuel. ]

----------------------------------------------------------------

INT. SPARE ROOM — NIGHT

A small room that never got finished. Cardboard boxes still taped along
one wall. A folding table. On it: three monitors lined edge to edge, a
yoke bolted to a scrap of plywood, rudder pedals on the carpet. A coffee
mug gone cold. A space heater ticking in the corner.

The monitors are dark.

BIRDY sits in a worn office chair, slumped, the way we've watched him sit
all night. Hollow. Looking at nothing.

His hand finds the power switch.

The monitors wake. A cockpit. Beyond the glass, a runway, and past it a
flat gray dawn.

He pulls a headset over his ears. Settles his feet on the pedals. Wraps
both hands around the yoke.

Something happens to his hands first. They stop being heavy. They sit on
the controls without searching, fingers spread, easy, certain — the only
certain thing about him all night.

He eases the throttle. The engine note climbs in the headset, tinny,
small, and his shoulders drop a full inch.

The plane rolls. Gathers. Lifts.

The gray on the screens warms. The horizon tips, and gold comes up under
the wing as he banks into it.

His breathing changes. Slower. Deeper.

His face opens. The slackness goes out of it. And a smile — not the one
he hands the cashier, not the one he gave Maya at the door. A real one,
private, helpless, a kid's.

He nudges the stick and the world rolls with him, sky to ground to sky,
and he laughs once, soft, to himself.

INT. HALLWAY — CONTINUOUS

Dark. The door stands open a few inches. A bar of monitor-light lies
across the floor.

MAYA, in a robe, barefoot, stops there.

Through the gap she sees him. Sees the smile. Sees him laugh.

She goes very still. The kind of still you go when a wild thing has come
close and you don't want to be the one who scares it off.

Her eyes shine. She doesn't wipe them. She watches the man she married
fly, and she stays in the dark where he can't see her watching, because
watching is all she's allowed.

INT. SPARE ROOM — CONTINUOUS

The horizon levels. The gold thins back toward gray.

Birdy's hand drifts toward the throttle — and stops. Something tightens
at the back of his neck. He half-turns toward the door.

INT. HALLWAY — CONTINUOUS

Maya steps back out of the light. Too late, maybe. Or not.

INT. SPARE ROOM — CONTINUOUS

He doesn't get up to check. He just turns back to the screens.

And it leaves him. The shoulders come up. The face closes, fold by fold,
until it's the face from the kitchen again. His hands come off the yoke
and go quiet in his lap.

He reaches out and taps a key. The plane freezes mid-air, hanging there
in the cold light, going nowhere.

She's in the doorway now. He knows it without turning.

BIRDY
Lost track of time.

He pulls the headset down to his neck. Doesn't look at her.

MAYA
I saw you.

A beat. He could let it be about the time, the late hour, the door he
forgot to shut.

BIRDY
I'll be up in a minute.

He reaches over and powers the monitors off, one, then the next, then the
last. The room goes dark except for the heater's small orange coil.

Maya stands in it a moment longer. She doesn't push. She holds the
doorframe with one hand.                  <!-- fix: cut "the way you hold a rail" simile -->

MAYA
Don't be long.

She goes. Her bare feet on the stairs.

Birdy sits in the dark with the dead screens in front of him. He puts the
headset back on over both ears, though there's nothing playing now, and
folds his hands in his lap, and stays.
