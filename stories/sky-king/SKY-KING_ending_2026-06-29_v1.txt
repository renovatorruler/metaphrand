SKY KING — THE ENDING
(the suspense engine + the inspirational line)
29 June 2026 — v1


================================================================
THE ENGINE: doubt, to the last second
================================================================

Even an audience that knows the real man died must NOT be sure ours
lives — until the very end. We use their foreknowledge against them:
every signal points at death, so they brace for it — and then he lives.
That braced dread, and the release, is the whole payoff.

How we keep the doubt alive:

  • He signals it himself. The real "I don't know. I don't know." He
    never once promises to live. Right up to the approach we cannot tell
    whether he even wants to.

  • The FUSED AMBIGUITY: when the descent goes wrong, no one — not the
    tower, not us — can tell if he is putting it in on purpose or just
    fighting a plane he can't fly. It sounds exactly the same either way.
    That not-knowing is the agony.


================================================================
THE LANDING — HEARD, NOT SEEN
================================================================

It's off-airport, far out, dark — out of sight. The tower (and the
audience) get it only over the radio. We do NOT see the touchdown.

  • A fighter pilot / the tower: "His descent's too strong."
        "Pull up. Straighten it out. Pull UP —"
  • Then: static. Nothing.
  • We hold on the faces in the room — MAYA most of all — listening to
    her husband maybe dying, unable to see, unable to do one thing. We
    are put exactly where she is.
  • The wait goes on too long.
  • Then the word comes: "...Safe touchdown. She's banged up. We got him
    out." He's alive.

(This is why it must be off-airport and on radio: it is the only way to
hold "is he dead?" all the way to the last second.)


================================================================
THE INSPIRATIONAL LINE — the whole point of the film
================================================================

After he's confirmed alive:

    BISHOP:  Why'd you put it down, Birdy? ... Why'd you land it after all?
    BIRDY:   [the line]

This is the man on the bridge who takes his hands off the rail at the
last second. It must be PLAIN — his dry voice, not a speech, not a
slogan — and it should pay off something small we planted earlier: a
tiny claim on tomorrow.

PLANT (early, Act 1 / first contact): a small thing he does out of habit
"for tomorrow" even while he means to die — e.g., he rinses a coffee cup
and sets it on the rack to dry.

PAYOFF (sketch — register only, NOT locked):

    BISHOP:  Why'd you land it, Birdy?
    BIRDY:   ...Set a cup out to dry this morning. Habit.
             (beat)
             Figured I oughta come use it.

Plain. Tied to the ordinary. It says "I found I expected a tomorrow"
without ever saying it. When we lock this scene I'll write several takes
of this one line — it's the most important line in the picture, and it
should not sound written.
