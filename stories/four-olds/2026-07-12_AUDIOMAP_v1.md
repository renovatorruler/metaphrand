# THE FOUR OLDS — audio play adaptation map (v1, against locked v14.1)

Form (locked): pure drama, no narrator; one feature ~2hr; three act breaks
with chapter marks; one-way adaptation, engine-generated per scene from
audio seeds + studio/AUDIO_DRAMA.md. This map is the controlling design —
nothing generates until it's approved.

- BROADCAST WORLD: anchors, pressers, the streamer — each with its own transmission timbre (TV, AM radio, phone speaker, shortwave).

**Sound motifs:** the two-two knock code (taught in Act 1 as sound, paid all the way to the finale); the percolator; the padlock's one pull; the screen door; morse; the three-second light-lag in the coda.

**Presence law per scene:** speaking population capped at 4 foreground voices; extra bodies anchored by name-address, tool sounds, or breath; crowd scenes = 2–3 voiced + walla bed.

**Act breaks / chapter marks:** Part One ends sc13 (the over-spec beat left sitting on the table); Part Two ends sc20 (bootlaces — "Somebody time me getting in."); Part Three ends sc34 (TLI + "He flew it. The rest of his life."); Part Four to the end. ~28–33 min per part.

## The five hard conversions, designed

1. **THE REVEAL (sc37) — the described image.** Brandt's climb arrives as breath and boot-crunch on comms; the loop goes silent one beat too long — and then Earth sees it FOR us, in character voices, in this order: a mission-control controller's half-sentence dying; Roy's ("Where's Earl and them? They're missing the damnedest thing that ever happened." / Buck: "There they are."); the panelist identifying the suits; BRANDT himself, on the open loop, reading the four crate stencils aloud — "Lot A. Old Ideas. Lot B…" — which is the title landing, by voice; Warsaw clapping around one phone. Nobody describes prettily; everybody describes in their own register.
2. **THE PRIMER BURN (sc31).** The gala toast dies mid-sentence under Marwani's live mic; the diner comes off its stools as one sound; an anchor's clinical voice cracks into plain speech ("the paint is — the paint's coming off, and underneath it's—"). We never see it; the whole planet tells us.
3. **THE FINALE WAVE (sc42 + sc44).** Hale's post is read aloud off the register phone at Roy's ("Two words. 'Look up.'"). Then the wave arrives as RADIO, not light: Danny keys two clicks; one far station answers; then ten; then the band blooms — hams relaying city names like a roll call, static thick with two-twos on every frequency — until the noise floor of the planet IS the knock code. In the coda, Cricket on the Moon keys twice into it, three seconds pass, and the world answers again. The film's final image becomes the show's final sound.
4. **THE WORDLESS BEATS.** The dockworker (sc30): breath, torque clicks, three counted seconds of held air, and a man muttering block letters as he writes — "All… clear." The Shen palm (sc32): the glove seal peeling, then NOTHING for eight seconds except Cricket's held breath — silence spent where it detonates; Brandt's call breaks it. The zinnia (sc28): pocketknife through old stitches, paper-dry rustle, one breath — FLAGGED DECISION: either silence-and-cut (purest) or one whispered word ("Peg.") as the register-crack of the whole show. Your call at the scene.
5. **STENCILS, SCREENS, PAPER.** Every visual insert already has a natural reader: the bank clerk reads the $0.00 line (exists); Pell reads manifests for a living (weigh-in); Tito sounds out the stencil letters as he paints; the school worksheet becomes the BOY asking his dad how to spell "cookout" — an upgrade on the film version; Danny half-mutters the pay stub at the kitchen table; Brandt reads the crates at the reveal.

## Ruled (2026-07-12)

1. **THE SELF-TALK LAW (his, general):** audio characters naturally voice
   what they do and find — spoken discovery ("What's this? … Peg. You
   finally sewed it in.") calibrated to each character's register. A beat
   that cannot be voiced naturally (a man silently tearing up at a photo)
   is NOT forced — it is REPLACED with a beat suited to the medium, or
   cut. Applies to every wordless-visual beat in the ledger. The zinnia
   plays as spoken discovery in Cricket's plain register (he already
   mutters while packing — canon).
2. **THE IMPACT LAW (his):** vacuum events are FELT, not heard — the
   fireworks render as structure-borne vibration: sub-bass concussions
   through ground, boots, and suit, in the chest rather than the ear,
   under the loop's crackle and Earth's gasps. Same law governs the
   launch from inside the crate, the touchdown slam, and the ascent at
   the departure — vacuum never carries air-borne sound; bodies and
   structures carry force. **His refinement: the characters REACT to the
   physics on the loop** — Earth-side, somebody flags the silence
   (thinking the feed dropped: 'There's no sound—'), and one of the olds
   answers plainly ('No air up here. You feel 'em.'); on the surface, the
   felt-in-the-chest reaction gets voiced ('You feel that?' / 'In the
   boots.'). The physics taught by reaction, never lecture — and the
   listener's is-my-audio-broken doubt dies inside two lines. Goes into
   sc43's audio seed as a required exchange (engine phrases it; the
   sketch is directional).
3. **Part boundaries confirmed:** breaks after sc13, sc20, sc34.
